RELEASES · ALBUMS

The shelf

Every album on the MAP, shelved in release order — cover, title, artist, exact date. Eighteen records today; built to hold five hundred.

DESIGN RATIONALE

The ALBUMS landing is the one category page where a text list actively destroys information. Album covers are the strongest recall key the collection owns — you remember the Pet Sounds goats and the white void of the White Album long before you remember filenames — and a list flattens every record into equal, invisible weight. The shelf inverts that: artwork first, square and large, in strict release order, so the page reads like the wall of a good record shop. The chronology becomes an argument you can see — the 1963–66 Beatles/Beach Boys arms race plays out left to right across two rows, and Tapestry and The Smile Sessions sit visibly apart as the codas they are.

The form stays editorial rather than skeuomorphic. No wood grain — one thin rail under each row, drawn half a gutter past the covers so it reads as a continuous ledge the records stand on, on the same cream the whole MAP lives on. The caption stack (title, artist, exact date) deliberately mirrors the header block of every album note — Artist:, Released: — so the shelf is the index of the notes, not a separate design system. On hover the record lifts off the ledge; the ledge stays put. Click-through goes to the live note.

The infrastructure is the half of the brief you can't see at 18 albums, so it's wired to be visible: the page never renders the whole collection. Decade chips narrow to year chips — a bounded UI no matter how large the catalog grows, since a decade can never produce more than ten year chips — and the grid appends in batches of twelve via an IntersectionObserver on the "More" button (which doubles as a manual fallback). Cover images load lazily. At 500 albums the first paint costs exactly what it costs today: twelve covers. The data is a single flat array of slug/artist/date — precisely what the Quartz build already knows from frontmatter, so going live means emitting one JSON file at build time, same pattern as the Library cards.
