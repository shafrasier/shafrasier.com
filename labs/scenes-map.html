SCENES

A scene is a coordinate — a place crossed with a moment where a sound coalesced. A movement is the same energy once it slips that coordinate and travels. Read down the spine in order of ignition.

Design rationale

Every other category on the MAP organizes along one axis — genres branch, eras succeed, releases date. A scene note is the only kind that is two-axis by definition: it names a place crossed with a moment ("Liverpool, 1958–1966"), and the note is about what happened when that geography and that chronology intersected. A few of these are movements rather than scenes — the same sound after it slips its origin and travels, so the geographic axis widens from a single pin to a spread (the beat boom is Merseybeat gone national) — and each card is tagged for which it is. An alphabetical card list throws both axes away. This landing page's one job is to make you feel where and when before you've read a word.

So each card stamps the coordinate twice. The where is a hand-drawn ink glyph — recognition over accuracy, like a fake-book head: the Mersey's bottleneck estuary, the Thames squiggle with Soho pinned just north of it, the off-grid wedge of the Village cut at 14th Street, the whole archipelago lighting up city by city for the beat boom. The when is carried twice on purpose: the vertical spine orders scenes by ignition year and annotates the clusters (three scenes catch fire around 1958 — exactly the kind of fact a sorted list hides), while the strip at each card's foot draws the scene's lifespan on a shared 1956–1968 scale, so durations compare at a glance — Mod outlives them all; the beat boom burns half as long as the scenes that fed it.

It scales cheaply. A new scene costs one glyph (a few SVG paths, drawn once), one sentence, and two dates; the spine simply grows downward, and year markers appear only where new scenes ignite, so the page stays an annotated chronicle rather than a grid that needs re-sorting. When the MAP reaches Haight-Ashbury, Laurel Canyon, and Detroit, the geography goes transatlantic and the form gets better, not worse — the glyphs do the establishing shot a heading never could. Sibling categories get sibling forms, not the same form: GENRES has its sheet-music timelines; SCENES gets cartography, because scenes are the only notes that live on a map.
