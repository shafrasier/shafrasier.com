What the crate captures

Two classes of data, two rules. Locked facts are verified once against the record, then closed; corrections go through review. Votable reads are judgment calls, and the crowd that spins the records owns them. In rekordbox, Serato, and Traktor the shared lockable core is library basics — title, artist, album, genre, label, remixer, BPM, key, comment, rating, color, play count. Nothing structural, nothing about how the record mixes.

🔒 locked Facts of the record

  • Mix type + lineagealbum cut · 12″ mix · dub · edit — and its parent version. The edit wearing the same title as the mix with the intro is the classic 1 a.m. trap.
  • Resolved authorshipthe human behind the production-company alias. Where the alias won’t resolve, the crate says so — see Hard Times Productions, below.
  • Intro runwaybars of beat-only room before the first melodic or vocal element, and what fills it (drums / bass / pad / vocal / cold start). Remix services printed this on DJ pressings in the 1980s.
  • Intro vocal flaga vocal inside the first 16 bars vetoes a vocal-over-vocal blend.
  • Outro type + fades-outfade · percussion run-out · cold end · reprise. You cannot mix out of a fade.
  • Fake-extended flagthe “12-inch” that is the album take with a longer tail — no mixable runway despite the label.
  • Breakdown map + mix-out pointbar positions of breakdowns, rebuilds, and the last clean phrase boundary — where working DJs actually leave the record.
  • Tempo, never flattenedBPM per section where it shifts, the count convention (75 vs 150), first-downbeat offset, odd-bar warnings, and a drift flag for live kits and tape wobble.
  • Key, never flattenedkey per section with the Camelot code — a mid-song modulation silently invalidates a single stored key.
  • Drum fingerprintTR-808 · LinnDrum · DMX · live kit · sampled break. Machine-to-machine layering locks; a live kit flamming against an 808 never will.
  • Acappella / dub existsthe overlay and the talkover bed for this composition, and which pressing carries them — the vinyl-era answer to stems.
  • Availabilitystreaming · physical-only · promo-only · acetate — what you can pull up tonight vs what you hunt.

◍ votable Reads of the room

  • Energy-curve shapebuilder · plateau · peak-and-release · rollercoaster. A single 1–10 number can’t say the track dips for 90 seconds mid-peak.
  • Set rolewarm-up · peak · after-hours · closer · reset. Selection is about the job the record does.
  • Vocal prominencenone · hook-only · full song — how much attention the record demands and what you can layer over it.
  • Recognizabilitya known hook resets a flagging room; an obscure banger keeps heads down. Both are tools; know which you’re holding.
  • Mix-hostility notes“rubato piano first 8 bars” · “fade starts under the last chorus” — hazards accumulated the way record-pool feedback sheets once were.
  • Burn levelscene-local and time-decaying: this bomb already went off in your city this season.
  • Mood / texturedark · euphoric · chugging · dreamy · percussive — the fast cross-genre filter DJs currently fake with private My Tags that never leave their laptop.
  • Descriptors from the literatureseeded from cited writing — Kevorkian’s Go Bang “dubbed out” per Tim Lawrence — then opened to votes.

An old practice, given a shared home

DJs have annotated records this way since the form began. Tom Lewis’s Disco Beats books listed hits by BPM from 1977; James Hamilton stopwatch-counted a BPM into every Record Mirror dance review from 1979; the remix services of the eighties printed BPMs and intro bar counts on their DJ-only pressings; Pet Shop Boys put a BPM on each label of Disco (122 · 128 · 122 · 121 · 111 · 114); and the record pools that started at the Prince Street Loft in 1975 ran on written feedback from the DJs who played the promos. What never existed is a shared, structured, correctable home for that knowledge — organized under the composition, one entry per unique mix. That is the Crate.

Sources: Wikipedia (History of DJing; James Hamilton; Music pool; Remix service), Discogs release 584545 (the printed Disco BPMs), Red Bull Music Academy on Razormaid.

How it lands on the MAP

The Crate rides the SINGLE note — a versions: table in frontmatter, rendered by a transformer, the same architecture as the album tracklists. Mixes stay data: no note per mix, no graph flooding. The people are the nodes — Arthur Russell, François Kevorkian, Walter Gibbons earn ARTIST and PRODUCER notes because they are load-bearing for the dance lineage the MAP already gestures at; each crate row links to them. A mix that earns real prose can be promoted to a TRACKS note later, and its row points there. Every locked fact carries a verification state, which is the seam where community submission bolts on without changing the model.
