The Woodshed

One engine, and an instrument for every concept. Each player below is mounted from a declarative spec; in production each lives inside its THEORY note, so a new note means writing a spec rather than code. One player runs at a time; starting one stops the others.

01

Backbeat

Rhythm read the note ↗

March music claps on one and three; American pop claps on two and four. Flip between the presets and feel your body change its mind — then edit the cells and build your own pattern. "The band" plays a vamp underneath so the accents read as feel.

What to hear
  1. Play March and count one-two-three-four out loud — your hands land with the count.
  2. Switch to Backbeat mid-play. Nothing else changed, but the claps move without asking you.
  3. The Motown stack: the same two-and-four, tripled — snare, claps, tambourine — so the message survives a transistor radio.
In the wild: Earl Palmer's snare on "The Fat Man" (1949) · the stacked two-and-four on "I Can't Help Myself" (1965)
02

Call and response

Form read the note ↗

A leader calls; the group answers. Let the engine answer — or take the response bars yourself: in You answer mode the gap is yours, and the keyboard is locked to notes that can't miss. The claps you'll hear are unit 01, working inside unit 02.

What to hear
  1. Echo vs Answer: the same gap, two kinds of reply — repetition and resolution.
  2. You answer: play anything at all in the pulsing bars. The gap makes it sound intentional — that's the form doing the work.
  3. Turn the claps off and the exchange loses its floor; the response no longer knows where to land.
In the wild: the "yeah yeah yeah" answers on "She Loves You" (1963) · Ray Charles and the Raelettes on "What'd I Say" (1959) · "Shout" (1959)
03

Twelve-bar blues

Form read the note ↗

The form is a circle you can hear coming around. The three rows are the three four-bar phrases of the AAB lyric — statement, repeat, answer. Tap any bar to jump there; flip to the quick-change; straighten the shuffle and hear the ride stiffen.

What to hear
  1. Count the bars out loud once through — feel bar 9 lift onto the V and bar 12 lean you back home.
  2. Quick-change: bar 2 leaves home early. Most rock & roll runs this way.
  3. Turn the shuffle off. Same notes, same chords — different century.
In the wild: "Johnny B. Goode" (1958) is this grid in B♭ — count along · the compressed eight-bar cousin: "Key to the Highway"
04

Pentatonic & blues scales

Melody read the note ↗

Five notes with the tension removed. Turn on the drone, switch on the can't-miss keys, close your eyes and wander — you cannot land wrong. Then add the sixth note back and learn why the blue one has to bend.

What to hear
  1. Minor pentatonic + drone + can't-miss keys: noodle freely. This is why the scale is where guitarists live.
  2. Switch to the full major scale and find the 4th — feel it itch against the drone.
  3. Blues scale: play the ♭5 straight, then bend through it. Wrong note; blue note.
In the wild: the opening guitar riff of "My Girl" (1964) climbs the major pentatonic note by note — five notes, zero tension. Then hear the blue edge in "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" (1965), whose fuzz line leans on the ♭7
05

I–vi–IV–V

Harmony read the note ↗

Home, shadow, warmth, tension — the four-bar cycle beneath every doo-wop ballad, rolled here in the triplet feel a slow-dance band would give it. Watch the vi bar: same key, darker room. Then take it away and hear what goes missing.

What to hear
  1. Follow the keyboard through one cycle: the vi keeps two of home's three notes — it's a clouding, not a departure.
  2. Skip the vi: three destinations left, and the walk home goes straight — no yearning.
  3. Slow it to 60 and it's a gymnasium slow dance; push past 110 and it's the same heart beating faster.
In the wild: "Surfer Girl" (1963) · "Earth Angel" (1954) · "Stand By Me" (1961) · "This Boy" (1963)
06

Modal interchange

Harmony read the note ↗

Two shelves of chords: the seven that live in C major, and the seven next door in its parallel minor. Tap anything to hear it. Then run the famous borrowings — and flip each one back to diatonic to hear exactly what the borrowed chord was buying.

What to hear
  1. Tap B♭ on the bottom shelf, then B° on the top — the chord pop borrows vs the one the key actually offers.
  2. The "Hey Jude" loop: the ♭VII is the harmony exhaling. Flip it diatonic and the exhale is gone.
  3. The iv shadow: F, then Fm — warmth, then dusk falls on the same chord.
In the wild: the coda of "Hey Jude" (1968) — F, E♭, B♭, and home again, for four minutes, and it never needs to go anywhere else
