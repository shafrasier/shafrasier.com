DESIGN RATIONALE

An era's defining property is duration — when it started, when it dissolved, and what else was running at the same time. A list of links (the default Quartz folder page) erases exactly that: it tells you the British Invasion exists, but not that Beatlemania detonated inside the folk revival, or that the album era was already underway before the Invasion finished dissolving into it. The Gantt form makes collision the first thing you see. The question an ERAS landing page should answer isn't "what eras are there?" — it's "what was happening at once?"

One lane per era, sorted by onset, so reading top-to-bottom is reading chronologically and overlap reads as vertical adjacency: glance down any year gridline and every bar it crosses was alive that year. Hovering a bar dims the rest, so a single era's footprint pops against its neighbors. The faint lane rules deliberately echo the staves of the genres timeline — these are sibling pages in the same sheet-music register — but pills instead of notation keeps ERAS visually distinct: genres flow and branch; eras start and end. Names sit inside their bars and step outside when a bar is too short, so nothing ever truncates, and the whole thing prints clean.

It scales by addition, not redesign. The vault has one real era today, so the dashed ghosts do double duty: they demonstrate how the page behaves at realistic density, and they're an honest to-do list — each one is an era the MAP plausibly writes next. When a ghost note lands, its bar flips solid, gains its real dates and argument, and links to the live page; twenty eras is twenty lanes and a scroll, with the axis fixed so the eye keeps its calibration. On phones the chart scrolls sideways inside its card rather than crushing forty years into 380px.
