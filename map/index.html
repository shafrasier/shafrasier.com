About the MAP

The MAP charts popular music as networks of influence, context, and argument. It covers popular music — primarily American and British, with eventual expansion into global traditions. It is not a comprehensive database but a curated canon: every album, artist, and idea that appears here earned its place.

I built this because I believe context is the most important thing when engaging with art. Situating a record historically — knowing what it inherited, what it rejected, what it made possible — can be the difference between experiencing surface and experiencing meaning. The existing resources don’t do this work well enough. Wikipedia is encyclopedic but sometimes lacks a sense of proportion — it can tell you that something existed without conveying why it was important. Rate Your Music has extraordinary data, but databases don’t have a point of view. It can tell you an album is tagged “Brill Building” and rated 3.81, but it won’t tell you why that category matters or how one thing led to another.

The MAP sits between those poles: more focused than an encyclopedia, more narrative than a database. It is grounded in a personal canon — nearly ten thousand albums I’ve heard, considered, and tracked — which means it doesn’t claim to be a definitive history. It is, however, a serious attempt at historicization: the works that appear here are situated in their context, connected to what came before and after, and written with the rigor of criticism and close reading. The canon is mine, but the history it traces is real, and the arguments it makes are intended to hold up to scrutiny.

What’s inside

The MAP maps popular music through releases, taxonomy, artists, industry, techniques, ideas, and sources. It currently contains 18 albums, 10 singles, 1 track, 30 artists (including 6 songwriters, 2 producers, and 2 session musician ensembles), 17 genres, 3 scenes, 1 era, 1 moment, 4 labels, 2 studios, 1 executive, 1 production technique, 5 music theory concepts, 6 conceptual essays, and 14 scholarly sources — and growing steadily.

FolderWhat you’ll find
RELEASESThe music itself. ALBUMS holds notes on foundational records. SINGLES holds historically important tracks released as singles — especially vital for the pre-album era, when the single was the primary artistic unit. TRACKS holds notes on influential album tracks that were never released as singles but whose innovation or influence merits standalone treatment.
TAXONOMYClassification and periodization. ERAS are the broadest orientation — broad historical periods that contextualize everything else. GENRES follow RYM’s taxonomy, explaining not just what a genre sounds like but why it emerged and what it displaced. SCENES are geographic and temporal clusters where concentration produced something distinctive.
MOMENTSDiscrete historical events — hours or days, not years — that changed the trajectory of music. Narrative inflection points, often marking where one era ends and another begins.
ARTISTSCritical profiles positioning each musician within their scene, genre, and era — not biographies, but maps of influence in both directions. Includes subfolders for SONGWRITERS, PRODUCERS, and SESSION MUSICIANS.
INDUSTRYThe business and physical infrastructure of recorded music. LABELS profiles record labels whose aesthetic identity or institutional structure shaped the music released on them. STUDIOS profiles recording studios whose acoustic properties, equipment, or house engineers shaped the sound of the records made there. EXECUTIVES profiles non-performing industry figures whose creative-commercial decisions shaped what music got made and released.
TECHNIQUESThe craft layer, split in two. PRODUCTION covers sonic innovations and historically significant instruments that changed what music could sound like. THEORY covers musical-theory concepts that recur as connective tissue between genres, artists, and records.
IDEASThe analytical core. Conceptual notes that cut across the vault, making arguments about influence, commerce, race, craft, and authenticity by engaging with specific scholars and critics.
SOURCESThe scholarly backbone. Each note maps a book, essay, or critical text’s core argument, key contributions, and connections to the MAP — not bibliographic entries, but argument maps that IDEAS notes engage with directly.

How to navigate

Every note links to other notes. Follow the links. The MAP is designed to be read laterally — click from an album to its producer, from a producer to the technique they pioneered, from that technique to the records it influenced. The Connections view shows the full web at a glance.

If you’re arriving fresh, pick an entry point that matches your interest: an era (like The British Invasion), a genre (like Brill Building), or an album you know (like (1966) Pet Sounds) — and follow the links outward.

The influence principle

Influence is the primary connective tissue of the MAP. Every note answers at least one of these questions: What did this inherit? What did it reject? What did it make possible? The MAP reads as history — not a timeline, but a web of cause and effect, action and reaction, inheritance and reinvention.

What this is not

It is not a database of ratings or a completist catalog — I maintain spreadsheets for that. It is not an encyclopedia trying to cover everything — only works that are foundational to a genre, scene, or era earn entries. And it is not neutral: the MAP has a point of view, makes claims, and stakes positions. The arguments are intended to be earned by evidence and close reading, but they are arguments nonetheless.
