(1975) Jeanne Dielman, 23, quai du Commerce, 1080 Bruxelles

(1975) Jeanne Dielman, 23, quai du Commerce, 1080 Bruxelles

Mar 04, 20269 min read

Director: Chantal Akerman Movement: Modernist cinema

Over three days, Jeanne Dielman — a widowed mother in Brussels — performs her domestic routine. She cooks, cleans, makes the bed, sets the table, polishes shoes, bathes, shops for groceries, and receives a male client in the afternoon for sex. Everything is filmed in real time, from fixed camera positions, in the actual duration the tasks require. On the first day, the routine is flawless. On the second day, small disruptions appear: she overcooks the potatoes, drops a spoon, leaves a light on, sits in a chair doing nothing. On the third day, after the client leaves, she kills him with a pair of scissors. Then she sits at the dining table, hands bloodied, in the dark, for nearly seven minutes.

Jeanne Dielman is one of cinema’s most radical propositions: a three-and-a-half-hour film in which the primary action is housework, and in which the primary formal strategy is refusing to edit it down. Akerman shows you what domestic labor actually takes — not the montage version, not the establishing shot that tells you “she’s cleaning” before cutting to something interesting, but the full duration of peeling potatoes, running a bath, setting out the coffee things. The film converts what cinema has always treated as transitional material — the stuff between events — into the subject itself. When violence finally arrives, it arrives not as climax but as the collapse of a system you’ve spent three hours watching her maintain.

Key formal strategies

  • Fixed camera positions: the camera doesn’t follow Jeanne; it waits for her in each room
  • Real-time duration: domestic tasks shown in their actual length, uncompressed
  • Frontal, symmetrical framing: doorways, hallways, kitchen counters filmed like architectural studies
  • No non-diegetic music: only the sounds of labor — water, cutlery, footsteps, gas burners
  • Three-day structure: repetition establishing a baseline so that deviations register as seismic events
  • Delphine Seyrig’s performance: precise, contained, procedural — the body as instrument of routine

The routine

The first day establishes the system. Jeanne wakes her son, makes breakfast, walks him to school, shops, prepares dinner, receives a client, bathes, sets the table, eats with her son, cleans up, prepares for bed. Akerman films each task from a fixed position — usually from the doorway or across the room — and holds until the task is complete. Nothing is abbreviated. The potatoes are peeled in their entirety. The table is set place by place. The bathtub fills at the rate water fills a bathtub.

The effect is cumulative and double. First: boredom, restlessness, the awareness that you’re watching someone do things you normally fast-forward through. Second, emerging through the boredom: recognition. This is work. It takes this long. It requires this much attention. Someone does it every day, and cinema — along with everything else — has agreed to pretend it doesn’t take any time at all. Akerman doesn’t argue that domestic labor is undervalued. She makes you sit inside the time it consumes, and the argument makes itself.

The camera

Akerman’s camera positions are architectural. The camera occupies a fixed point in each room — usually the doorway, at Jeanne’s height — and waits. Jeanne enters the frame, performs her task, exits. The camera stays. Sometimes the room is empty for several seconds before she returns. The effect is of a space that persists independently of the person maintaining it — the apartment as a machine that requires constant operation.

The camera never follows Jeanne. It doesn’t track, pan, or zoom. It doesn’t offer close-ups of her face during emotional moments. It doesn’t provide the shot/reverse-shot structure that would create psychological access. Jeanne is always seen in full or medium shot, within the architecture of her apartment, performing labor. The refusal of the close-up is a refusal to aestheticize, psychologize, or sentimentalize. We don’t see into Jeanne. We see what she does.

This connects to The female face in cinema by negation. Where Godard, Hitchcock, Bergman, and Lynch make the female face their primary cinematic surface, Akerman makes the female body at work hers. The face is visible but never privileged. Delphine Seyrig’s face — one of cinema’s most beautiful — is treated as part of the body performing labor, not as a screen for projection or desire. The radical move isn’t showing Jeanne’s face differently. It’s refusing to make the face the point.

The disruptions

On the second day, the system begins to fail. Small things: the potatoes overcook. Jeanne drops a brush. She sits in a chair in the living room — doing nothing — for what feels like a long time. She forgets to put the lid on the soup tureen. She misses a button on her housecoat.

These are tiny events. In any other film they wouldn’t register. But Akerman has spent two hours establishing the routine’s precision, and now every deviation is legible. The overcooked potatoes land like a crisis because you know — because you’ve watched — exactly how carefully she usually makes them. The film has trained your perception. You notice what she notices. The system is failing, and the failures are visible in the same register as the system itself: domestic, material, specific.

The client

Each afternoon, a man arrives. Jeanne takes him to the bedroom. The door closes. He leaves. She bathes. The sex is part of the routine — scheduled, functional, compensated. Akerman doesn’t film the encounters on the first two days. The bedroom door closes; time passes; it opens. The sex is another task, another maintenance operation, folded into the domestic schedule between shopping and dinner preparation.

On the third day, the camera enters the bedroom. We see the encounter. Jeanne appears to have an orgasm — an involuntary disruption of her bodily control, the system breaking at the level of the flesh. Afterward, she kills the man with a pair of scissors. The act is sudden, matter-of-fact, and almost silent. Akerman doesn’t sensationalize it. The murder is filmed in the same register as the potato peeling: an action, performed by a body, in a room.

The ending

After the killing, Jeanne sits at the dining table. Her hands are bloodied. The room is dark except for the light from outside. She sits for nearly seven minutes. The camera holds from across the room — the same position it’s occupied for every dinner scene. Nothing happens. She doesn’t cry, confess, flee, or explain. She sits.

The duration is the point. After three and a half hours of watching Jeanne fill time with labor, we now watch her inhabit time without it. The routine has been destroyed. There is nothing to do. The empty time — the time the routine was designed to fill, to structure, to make bearable — is all that’s left. The seven minutes at the table are the film’s most devastating passage, not because of the murder but because of the silence that follows: the sound of a life with no system left to maintain.

Relation to Akerman

Jeanne Dielman is Akerman’s defining work — the film that established her as one of cinema’s most important formalists and the one that made the argument for domestic duration as cinematic subject more completely than any film before or since. She was twenty-five when she made it. Delphine Seyrig, already a star from Last Year at Marienbad and The Discreet Charm of the Bourgeoisie, agreed to the role and brought to it a precision that matches Akerman’s formal rigor: every gesture exact, every task performed with the concentration of ritual. The collaboration between the young director’s structural vision and the veteran actress’s physical intelligence is what makes the film work — it’s neither pure conceptual cinema nor pure performance, but something between, where the concept lives inside the body.

Connects to

  • Architecture as psychology in cinema — the apartment as the vault’s most complete domestic architecture: every room a function, every surface maintained, the space as system rather than setting. When objects shift — a lid, a light, a button — the architecture registers the collapse before the character does
  • Feminist frameworks in cinema — feminist cinema’s most radical formal proposition: the political argument is in the duration, not the content. The spectator must inhabit the time domestic labor actually takes
  • The female face in cinema — the face refused as privileged surface: Seyrig’s face visible but never aestheticized, never made the screen for projection; the female body at work replacing the female face in close-up
  • Transcendental style and slow cinema — Schrader’s “surveillance camera” axis: cinema approaching the condition of objective recording, though Akerman’s camera is more precise than neutral
  • Modernist alienation — alienation as the invisible structure of routine: Jeanne isn’t alienated from her work the way Antonioni’s characters are alienated from their environments; she is her work, and the alienation is what happens when the work stops
  • Active spectatorship in modernist cinema — the film trains perception through repetition, then activates it through deviation: the spectator notices the overcooked potatoes because Akerman has taught them to notice
  • Ellipsis and absence in modernist cinema — the film inverts modernist ellipsis: instead of withholding the significant, it withholds nothing, and the refusal to edit becomes its own kind of radical statement about what cinema has always considered insignificant
  • (1961) La Notte — both films about women moving through confined space and discovering that the structure (marriage, routine) is empty; both produce dread through the observation of systems that can no longer sustain themselves
  • (1985) Vagabond — inverted companion: Jeanne is trapped inside domestic structure; Mona has refused all structure. Both women are destroyed. Varda’s investigation-from-outside and Akerman’s observation-from-fixed-positions are complementary feminist methods
