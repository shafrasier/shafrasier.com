(1975) Mirror

(1975) Mirror

Mar 04, 20265 min read

Original title: Zerkalo Director: Andrei Tarkovsky Movement: Soviet Poetic Cinema

A dying man remembers. His childhood in the countryside, his mother young and waiting, a barn burning, wind moving through grass. His marriage failing, his son growing distant, the same faces appearing across generations. Newsreel footage of war, of Soviet history, of a world in upheaval. Mirror doesn’t tell a story; it moves through memory the way memory actually moves—by association, recurrence, emotional logic rather than chronology. Tarkovsky made his most personal film and his most formally radical: an autobiography in fragments, where the self is dispersed across time and the past is never past.

Key formal strategies

  • Three temporal layers (1930s childhood, 1970s present, historical newsreel) interwoven without clear markers
  • Margarita Terekhova playing both mother and wife, collapsing generations
  • Long tracking shots through domestic spaces saturated with water, light, and texture
  • Poetry (Tarkovsky’s father Arseny’s actual poems) on the soundtrack
  • Color and sepia shifting to mark temporal register—but not consistently
  • The narrator (Alexei) heard but almost never seen

The structure

Mirror has no plot. It has recurrence. Childhood scenes in the country house. Present-day scenes where the narrator’s marriage is ending. Newsreel footage of the Soviet century: the Spanish Civil War, World War II, the Cultural Revolution in China, Soviet soldiers crossing Lake Sivash. Dreams that might be memories. Memories that feel like dreams.

Tarkovsky intercuts these layers without explanation, trusting emotional resonance to create coherence. The film makes sense the way a life makes sense when you’re dying and it all comes back at once—not as sequence but as constellation.

The mother

Margarita Terekhova plays Maria, the narrator’s mother—young, beautiful, waiting for a husband who may not return, raising children in wartime rural Russia. She also plays Natalya, the narrator’s wife in the present day, struggling with a marriage that mirrors her mother-in-law’s disappointments.

The doubling is the film’s central formal gesture. Mother and wife blur; the narrator marries a woman who looks like his mother; the son watches his father the way the father once watched his mother. Generations fold into each other. The self is not individual but inherited, repeated, caught in patterns that precede and outlast it.

The father’s voice

Arseny Tarkovsky—Andrei’s actual father—reads his own poetry on the soundtrack. The poems are about memory, loss, time, the persistence of the past. The son’s film becomes a vessel for the father’s words. The absent father (who left when Tarkovsky was young) is present as voice, as language, as the frame through which memory is organized.

This is autobiography that refuses the autobiographical: the self is always already penetrated by others, by inheritance, by words that aren’t your own.

The barn burning

In the film’s most famous sequence, Maria sits on a fence at dusk. Across the field, a barn catches fire. She doesn’t move. The camera watches the fire grow, the smoke rise, the structure collapse. Nothing is explained. The image is overwhelming—beautiful, terrible, somehow primal.

Tarkovsky loads the image with elemental force: fire, wind, earth, the woman’s stillness against destruction. It’s not symbolic of anything specific; it’s an image that exceeds interpretation while demanding attention. This is memory as image rather than narrative.

The newsreel

Tarkovsky intercuts his personal memories with footage of Soviet history: soldiers, refugees, war, political upheaval. The private and the historical interpenetrate. The narrator’s childhood happened inside history; history happened inside his childhood. The self cannot be separated from the century that shaped it.

The newsreel passages are the film’s most controversial element—some viewers find them distracting, others essential. Tarkovsky insists that personal memory is never only personal: we remember inside a world, and the world’s catastrophes are part of what we carry.

Water

Rain falls constantly. Puddles form in rooms. A ceiling drips. A woman washes her hair. Water runs through every temporal layer, connecting them not narratively but elementally. For Tarkovsky, water is memory’s medium—fluid, reflective, capable of holding images, always moving.

The water also decays: walls are damp, plaster crumbles, rooms flood. Mirror is saturated with the sense of time passing through matter, wearing things down even as memory preserves them.

Relation to Tarkovsky

Mirror is Tarkovsky’s most personal and most difficult film. He claimed it was autobiography; he also claimed it was about everyone’s childhood. The Soviet authorities hated it—too obscure, too personal, too uncommitted to socialist purpose. Audiences were divided; the film was barely released. Now it’s recognized as one of cinema’s supreme achievements: proof that film can render memory not as flashback but as lived duration, that the self can be shown as dispersed across time rather than unified in the present.

Connects to

  • Memory as structure in modernist cinema — the purest example: memory as form rather than content
  • (1959) Hiroshima mon amour — another film where personal and historical memory interpenetrate
  • (1962) La Jetée — another film built from fragments of memory and images of the past
  • (1979) Stalker — shares Tarkovsky’s elemental imagery but applies it to allegory rather than autobiography
