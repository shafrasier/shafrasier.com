Using this vault

Using this vault

Mar 04, 202613 min read

This vault maps popular music — primarily American and British for now, but eventually extending into global traditions — as a network of influence, genre, scene, and craft. It is a reference tool for understanding how music connects across time and place, written through the lens of music criticism and journalism. It is not a comprehensive database (that’s what my spreadsheets are for!) but a curated canon: every album, artist, genre, and scene that appears here earned its place.

I’ve created this vault because I’ve come to believe that context is the most important thing in music. Situating a record historically — knowing what it inherited, what it rejected, what it made possible — can be the difference between hearing sound and hearing meaning. The existing resources don’t do this work well enough. Take the Wikipedia page for Brill Building as a genre — it’s three paragraphs long. Three paragraphs for a songwriting tradition that shaped the sound of American pop for nearly a decade, that produced some of the most enduring songs of the twentieth century, that posed fundamental questions about the relationship between craft and commerce that the music industry is still grappling with. If you read that page and you’re new to the genre, you leave with almost no sense of why it mattered. Wikipedia is encyclopedic but it sometimes lacks a sense of proportion — it can tell you that something existed without conveying why it was important.

Rate Your Music has the opposite problem. The data is extraordinary — genre taxonomies, user ratings and reviews, release metadata — but it’s a database, and databases don’t have a point of view. RYM can tell you that an album is tagged “Brill Building” and rated 3.81, but it might not tell you that the Brill Building tradition collapsed because the Beatles made professional songwriters look obsolete, or that Carole King’s Tapestry was a Brill Building songwriter stepping out from behind the curtain a decade later and proving the craft had been the real thing all along. There’s no real narrative that connects one entry to the next.

This vault sits between those two poles: more focused than an encyclopedia, more narrative than a database. It is grounded in a personal canon — nearly ten thousand albums that I’ve heard, considered, and tracked in decade spreadsheets that I’ve maintained since 2019 — which means it doesn’t claim to be a definitive history of all recorded music. It is, however, a serious attempt at historicization: the records that appear here are situated in their context, connected to what came before and after, and written about with the analytical rigor of music criticism and musicology. The canon is mine, but the history it traces is real, and the arguments it makes are intended to hold up to scrutiny.

Folder structure

FolderPurpose
ERASHighest-level orientation. Broad historical periods (Tin Pan Alley to Brill Building, the British Invasion, Punk and Its Afterlives, the Hip-Hop Era) that contextualize everything else. Start here if you’re new to the vault.
GENRESThe backbone of musical taxonomy, following RYM’s genre/scene distinction. These entries reference primary sources, album reviews, and historical context to explain not just what a genre sounds like but why it emerged and what it displaced. Includes genres that RYM classifies as genres even when they have strong geographic associations (e.g., Brill Building).
SCENESGeographic and temporal clusters where music happened: Merseybeat, Mod, Laurel Canyon, CBGB, the Düsseldorf school, Memphis in the sixties, Kingston in the seventies. These mostly follow RYM’s scene classification — movements defined primarily by a specific place and time rather than a sonic template.
ARTISTSOrientation documents positioning each musician within their scene, genre, and era. Not biographies — more like critical profiles that map influence in both directions (who shaped them, who they shaped). Contains two subfolders: ARTISTS/SONGWRITERS for behind-the-scenes writers (Gerry Goffin, Hal David, Holland-Dozier-Holland) and ARTISTS/PRODUCERS for producers whose sonic signatures shaped genres (Phil Spector, Lee “Scratch” Perry, Brian Eno). Everyone who makes music lives under ARTISTS; the subfolders distinguish their role.
ALBUMSNotes on foundational records, organized chronologically: (Year) Title.md. Only albums that are somewhat foundational to a genre or scene earn an entry. These situate each record in its commercial, critical, and musical context.
SINGLESNotes on historically important individual tracks, organized chronologically: (Year) "Title".md. Singles earn entries when they changed the direction of a genre, introduced a production innovation, or became cultural events independent of any album. Especially important for the pre-album era (pre-1967), when the single was the primary artistic unit.
LABELSProfiles of record labels whose aesthetic identity, A&R vision, or institutional structure shaped the music released on them. Motown, Stax, Atlantic, Factory, 4AD, Sub Pop, Def Jam — labels are institutions that shaped taste, and some labels were scenes.
TECHNIQUESProduction techniques, sonic innovations, and historically significant instruments that changed what music could sound like: the Wall of Sound, gated reverb, the Minimoog, the TR-808, sampling, Auto-Tune. These are the material history of recorded music.
IDEASConceptual notes that cut across the vault. These make arguments — about influence, about the relationship between commerce and art, about what “authenticity” means in popular music, about the songwriter-performer divide.
SOURCESReference cards for music criticism, journalism, and scholarship. Important album reviews, canonical texts (Mystery Train, Psychotic Reactions and Carburetor Dung), interviews, and primary documents that shaped how we talk about music.
METAThis note and vault documentation.

Division of labor

  • Spreadsheets → factual tracking (ratings, metadata, genre taxonomy, completeness)
  • Obsidian → context, influence, arguments, critical history
  • SHA-STACK / Firebird Magazine → public-facing criticism and journalism

The vault doesn’t duplicate the spreadsheets. If you want to know every album I’ve heard from 1977 in chronological order, check the spreadsheet. If you want to understand why 1977 mattered — why punk and disco and Bowie’s Berlin trilogy all happened simultaneously — come here.

What gets its own note

  • Genres that need historical explanation beyond a one-line definition
  • Scenes where geographic concentration produced something distinctive
  • Artists whose influence extends meaningfully beyond their own discography
  • Albums that are foundational to a genre, scene, or era
  • Producers whose work constitutes a recognizable sonic signature
  • Techniques that changed the sound of recorded music
  • Ideas that apply across multiple genres, scenes, or eras
  • Sources that shaped music criticism or captured a scene in real time

What stays embedded

  • One-off observations (keep them in album or artist notes)
  • Discography details beyond the foundational records (that’s what the spreadsheets are for)
  • Chart positions and sales figures (mention in context, don’t catalog)
  • Personal ratings or rankings (the spreadsheets handle this)

The influence principle

Influence is the primary connective tissue of this vault. Every note should answer at least one of these questions: What did this inherit? What did it reject? What did it make possible? An album note that doesn’t connect backward and forward isn’t finished. A genre note that doesn’t explain what came before and what came after isn’t useful. A scene note that doesn’t map the cross-pollination between artists working in proximity isn’t doing its job.

This means the vault reads as history — not a timeline, but a web of cause and effect, action and reaction, inheritance and reinvention.

Note templates

ERA notes are entry points for entire periods:

  • Scope (dates, geography, defining tension)
  • What the era inherits and what it rejects
  • Key genres and scenes that define the period
  • Commercial and technological context (how music was made, sold, heard)
  • The critical landscape (how music was written about)
  • Key figures
  • Legacy and dissolution
  • Links

GENRE notes explain genres through history and context:

  • One-paragraph definition with sonic and historical orientation
  • Origins (what genres or traditions it emerged from)
  • Key characteristics (sonic, lyrical, structural)
  • Geography and timeline
  • Foundational records (linked to ALBUMS)
  • Key artists
  • Subgenres and adjacent genres (with links)
  • Critical and commercial reception
  • Legacy and influence
  • Links

SCENE notes map geographic and temporal clusters:

  • Location, period, and defining characteristics
  • Historical and economic context (why here, why now)
  • Key venues, labels, and institutions
  • Key artists (with links)
  • Cross-pollination (what genres mixed here)
  • Foundational records
  • Primary sources and documents (with links to SOURCES)
  • Dissolution or evolution
  • Links

ARTIST notes are critical profiles, not biographies:

  • Genre, scene, and era metadata
  • Opening paragraph with critical positioning
  • Influences and inheritance (who shaped them)
  • Core musical identity (what they sound like and why)
  • Key records (linked to ALBUMS)
  • Production relationships (linked to PRODUCERS where relevant)
  • Legacy and influence (who they shaped)
  • Links

ALBUM notes situate records historically:

  • Artist, released (exact date when available, month or year when not), label, genre (primary/secondary per RYM, bulleted with bold labels), scene metadata
  • Opening paragraph with thesis (why this album matters)
  • Musical and production context (who produced it, where, with what)
  • What it inherits and what it introduces
  • Reception (contemporary and retrospective, with links to SOURCES)
  • Commercial history
  • Influence and legacy
  • Connects to

SINGLE notes capture historically significant tracks:

  • Artist, year, label, genre metadata
  • Opening paragraph with thesis (why this single matters as a historical event)
  • Musical and production context
  • What it inherits and what it introduces
  • Chart performance and cultural impact
  • Influence and legacy
  • Connects to

SONGWRITER notes (in ARTISTS/SONGWRITERS) profile behind-the-scenes writers:

  • Era, genres, key collaborators metadata
  • Opening paragraph with critical positioning
  • Songwriting style and signature characteristics
  • Key songs (linked to SINGLES, ALBUMS, and ARTISTS who recorded them)
  • Partnerships (co-writers, producers, publishers)
  • Legacy and influence
  • Links

PRODUCER notes (in ARTISTS/PRODUCERS) profile sonic authors:

  • Era, genres, key artists metadata
  • Opening paragraph with critical positioning
  • Sonic signature (what they sound like and how they achieve it)
  • Key techniques (linked to TECHNIQUES)
  • Key records (linked to ALBUMS)
  • Influence and legacy
  • Links

LABEL notes profile institutional tastemakers:

  • Founded, location, key years, founder(s)
  • Opening paragraph with the label’s significance
  • Aesthetic identity (what the label sounds like, if applicable)
  • Key artists and releases
  • A&R philosophy and business model
  • Historical context (what market or cultural gap the label filled)
  • Legacy
  • Links

TECHNIQUE notes document sonic innovations and historically significant instruments:

  • Origin (who, when, where, how)
  • What it sounds like
  • Technical explanation (accessible, not engineering-manual)
  • Key records that defined or popularized it
  • Producers and engineers associated with it
  • Influence on subsequent production
  • Links

IDEAS notes make arguments:

  • Opening paragraph with thesis (no heading — start with prose)
  • All headings use #### **Bold** format
  • Examples section labeled #### **Heard in**
  • Final links section labeled #### **Related ideas**
  • Content sections vary by topic

SOURCES notes are reference cards:

  • Author, title, year, type (book, essay, review, interview, documentary)
  • Core argument or significance (1–3 paragraphs)
  • #### **Key contributions** — what this text introduced to music criticism or discourse
  • #### **Connections** — how it relates to other vault entries
  • For album reviews: note the publication, the critic, and why this particular review mattered

Formatting conventions

  • No blank line between a heading and the paragraph text that follows it. Headings connect directly to their content.
  • All section headings in IDEAS notes: #### **Bold text**
  • Album notes end with: #### **Connects to**
  • Artist notes end with: #### **Links**
  • Film note title format: (Year) Title.md for chronological sorting
  • Scene notes are named plainly: Merseybeat.md, Laurel Canyon.md
  • Genre notes are named by genre: Girl group.md, Heartland rock.md
  • No trailing colons on headings
  • When listing foundational records, key records, or any sequence of albums/singles, list them in order of release date

Cross-linking with the FILM vault

Music and cinema are deeply entangled — soundtracks, scoring, musical movements that parallel film movements, artists who cross between them. This vault links freely to the FILM vault using standard wikilinks. Where a film’s soundtrack is significant to music history (Scorpio Rising, Easy Rider, Purple Rain), it earns mentions in the relevant album, genre, or scene notes. Where a musician’s work connects to cinema (Miles Davis scoring Elevator to the Gallows, Ennio Morricone’s spaghetti westerns), the connection is noted but the primary analysis lives in whichever vault owns the subject.

City and location

Geography matters. Mention where albums were recorded, what city or region artists were active in, and how place shaped sound. Patti Smith belongs to New York even though she wasn’t born there. Motown is inseparable from Detroit. The Muscle Shoals sound requires Muscle Shoals. Location appears in scene notes primarily, but should surface in artist and album notes wherever it’s relevant to understanding the music.

Prose style

The vault should read like serious long-form music criticism — the kind of writing found in the best issues of The Wire, in the Continuum Encyclopedia, or in the 33 1/3 series at its sharpest. It should do real analysis that approaches an academic level while remaining readable and free of unnecessary jargon. Literate, compelling, and grounded in close listening.

The governing method is hybrid: close listening married to cultural and historical argument. Start with something audible — a bass line, a vocal texture, a chord change, a production choice — and read outward from that detail to a larger claim about influence, genre, or cultural context. Every paragraph should do both sonic and historical work simultaneously.

Specific principles:

  • Lead with the music. Open paragraphs with something the reader can hear rather than an abstract thesis. Let the sonic detail do the argumentative work.
  • Read outward from details. A specific musical observation should open into a historical or cultural argument. McCartney’s bass on “Drive My Car” isn’t just a bass line — it’s evidence of Motown’s influence on the Beatles, which is evidence of a transatlantic exchange that reshaped pop. Move from the small to the large.
  • Vary sentence rhythm. Mix long, syntactically complex sentences that build through clauses and accrue meaning with short declarative ones. Avoid formulaic constructions — “not X but Y,” “both X and Y” — unless they’re genuinely doing work. When two ideas are in tension, let the tension sit in the structure of the paragraph rather than announcing it with a pivot word.
  • Trust the reader. Don’t over-explain. If the vault says Jamerson’s bass work influenced McCartney, the reader can follow that without being told “this is significant because.” Academic rigor without academic hand-holding.
  • Make claims. Every paragraph should stake a position. Not “some argue” or “it could be said” — this vault has a point of view. But claims should be earned by the evidence in the paragraph, not asserted in advance.
  • Leave room for personal voice. The prose should have enough breathing room that a personal anecdote, a first-person aside, or a memory of hearing a record for the first time can be threaded in naturally without breaking the register. This is a personal canon, not a reference work.
  • Avoid AI-isms. No “It’s worth noting,” “It cannot be overstated,” “What makes X remarkable is,” or similar throat-clearing. No redundant summation at the end of a paragraph restating what the paragraph just said. Enter and exit cleanly.

The vault is a bulletproof interactive quasi-textbook written from a music criticism and musicologist perspective that also feels personal — tied to a specific canon of albums heard, loved, and thought about carefully.

Guiding principles

Write less. Link more. Stop early.

Context over completeness. Influence over information. Arguments over summaries.

The vault should feel like a network, not an encyclopedia. Every note exists to connect to other notes. If a note becomes isolated, either link it or delete it.
