STUDIOS

  • Muscle Shoals (priority 1) — 4 refs; Southern soul infrastructure (Soul, Dusty in Memphis, Please Please Me, Authenticity and its discontents)
  • United Western Recorders (priority 1) — 12 refs; the LA studio constellation (Studio 3 / Western 1) behind a huge swath of 1960s pop including Pet Sounds and Wrecking Crew sessions
  • FAME Studios (priority 2) — 3 refs; Muscle Shoals neighbor (separate facility from Muscle Shoals Sound); Aretha sessions, Wilson Pickett sessions
  • American Sound Studio (priority 2) — 3 refs; Chips Moman’s Memphis studio (separate from the Memphis Boys session-musician note); Elvis 1969 Memphis sessions, Dusty in Memphis
  • J&M Studio (priority 2) — Cosimo Matassa’s New Orleans studio; the literal birthplace of much 1950s R&B / rock & roll (could merge with Cosimo Matassa producer note)

SESSION MUSICIANS

  • James Jamerson (priority 1) — Motown’s bassist and arguably the most influential electric bass player in popular music; the Funk Brothers group note can’t carry his individual story
  • Hal Blaine (priority 1) — Wrecking Crew drummer on more #1 singles than any other drummer in pop history; “Be My Baby” intro alone earns him a note, and his reach runs from Spector through Wilson through Simon & Garfunkel
  • Carol Kaye (priority 1) — Wrecking Crew bassist/guitarist; anchored “Good Vibrations,” “California Girls,” and thousands of other sessions; her disputed Motown claims are themselves part of the story
  • Johnnie Johnson (priority 2) — Chuck Berry’s pianist; central to Berry’s sound and to the credit-lawsuit history
  • Scotty Moore (priority 2) — Elvis’s guitarist; referenced in Rockabilly, Rock & roll, “Johnny B. Goode”
  • Earl Palmer (priority 2) — New Orleans → LA bridge; the backbeat that Little Richard, Fats Domino, and early rock & roll are built on, who then became a Wrecking Crew fixture in the 1960s
  • The Memphis Horns (priority 2) — Wayne Jackson and Andrew Love; the horn section on Otis Redding, Sam & Dave, Al Green, Dusty in Memphis, and essentially every Stax and Hi record from 1965 onward
  • The Memphis Boys / American Sound rhythm section (priority 2) — Reggie Young et al.; the other Memphis house band, behind Elvis’s “Suspicious Minds,” Dusty in Memphis, Neil Diamond’s “Sweet Caroline,” and Wilson Pickett late-period sessions

LABELS

  • Atlantic Records (priority 1) — Ertegun/Wexler, rhythm & blues into soul into rock; the most important independent in the postwar era, and the institutional link between Stax, Aretha, and Led Zeppelin
  • Stax Records (priority 1) — Southern soul’s institutional home; Memphis-sound counterweight to Motown, and the label that makes the Booker T/Otis Redding/Sam & Dave story legible
  • Red Bird Records (priority 2) — Leiber-Stoller-Goldner girl group label; the Shangri-Las, the Dixie Cups, the Ad-Libs; brief but concentrated 1964–66 story
  • Folkways Records (priority 2) — Moses Asch’s ethnographic catalog, the Anthology of American Folk Music, and the infrastructure of the folk revival
  • Specialty Records (priority 2) — Little Richard, Sam Cooke, Larry Williams; the New Orleans/LA R&B-to-rock & roll pipeline
  • Vee-Jay Records (priority 2) — Chicago Black-owned independent; John Lee Hooker, Jimmy Reed, the Impressions, and the label that first licensed Beatles singles in the US
