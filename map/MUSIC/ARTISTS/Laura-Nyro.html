Laura Nyro

Genres: Pop soul, singer-songwriter, progressive soul
Scene: Brill Building (adjacent), New York City
Era: 1966–1997 (primary period: 1967–1971)

RYM · Apple Music · Spotify

The piano on “Timer” (1968) shifts from a ballad’s tenderness to a percussive, almost violent intensity in the space of a breath — Laura Nyro’s left hand hammering chords while her voice leaps from a murmur to a gospel shout, the tempo lurching forward as though the song’s structure is being dictated by mood rather than meter. She took the Brill Building’s melodic sophistication, gospel’s emotional directness, jazz’s harmonic restlessness, and the confessional urgency that the singer-songwriter movement hadn’t yet named, and fused them under a performing intensity that the professional songwriting world had no category for. She was a songwriter whose material was so densely personal, so structurally volatile, that the idea of handing it to another singer felt like a mistranslation — even though other singers turned her songs into hits.

Influences and inheritance

Nyro grew up in the Bronx, absorbing doo-wop from the street corners, R&B and soul from the radio, and show tunes from Broadway. Her father was a piano tuner and jazz trumpeter; her mother loved opera. The mix produced a songwriter who could write a three-minute pop single with the harmonic movement of a jazz standard and the emotional arc of an aria. She was signed to a publishing deal as a teenager and initially operated within the Brill Building system, but her performing ambitions — and the sheer density of her material — pushed her beyond it.

Core musical identity

Nyro’s compositions resist standard pop architecture. The chord progressions are dense and chromatic, moving through key centers with a restlessness borrowed from jazz and filtered through gospel’s need to arrive somewhere emotionally. Song structures are fluid, often dispensing with verse-chorus patterns in favor of through-composed sections that build, break, and rebuild. Her piano playing is both the compositional engine and the primary rhythmic instrument — percussive when the song demands intensity, tender when it demands space, and often switching between the two mid-phrase. “Eli’s Coming” builds its momentum not from a repeated hook but from Nyro’s left hand driving the rhythm while her right hand and voice spiral upward together.

Key records

  • Eli and the Thirteenth Confession (1968) — The masterpiece: pop soul, gospel, jazz, and confessional songwriting fused into something that had no precedent and few successors
  • New York Tendaberry (1969) — Darker, rawer, more pianistic; Nyro at her most uncompromising
  • Gonna Take a Miracle (1971) — A covers album of R&B and doo-wop songs, produced by Gamble and Huff with Labelle; Nyro paying explicit tribute to the music that formed her

The Brill Building connection

Nyro’s relationship to the Brill Building is that of a brilliant student who outgrew the school. She worked within the publishing system and her songs were hits for other artists — the Fifth Dimension recorded “Stoned Soul Picnic” (1968), “Wedding Bell Blues” (1966), and “Blowin’ Away” (1968); Blood, Sweat & Tears recorded “And When I Die” (1966); Three Dog Night recorded “Eli’s Coming.” But where Carole King and other Brill Building writers adapted gracefully to the singer-songwriter era, Nyro was already there: she never accepted the songwriter-performer divide in the first place. Her performing style — intense, theatrical, deeply personal — was inseparable from her writing.

Legacy and influence

Nyro’s direct influence is audible in Joni Mitchell (who credited Nyro with making her return to the piano), Kate Bush, Tori Amos, and Fiona Apple — the lineage of female singer-songwriters who treat the piano as a vehicle for intensity rather than gentle accompaniment. Her fusion of Pop soul sophistication with confessional ambition also anticipates Stevie Wonder’s early-seventies work and the art pop of Prince. She remains underknown relative to her influence, partly because her performing career was brief and partly because her music resists the categories that would make it easier to place.
