Venue

CBGB

Address: 315 Bowery, Manhattan (the Bowery at Bleecker)
Active: December 1973 – October 15, 2006
Proprietor: Hilly Kristal

Wikipedia

The room was a former nineteenth-century saloon on the ground floor of a flophouse: long and narrow, a bar down one side, a stage about a foot high at the back, capacity 350, ceiling pipes that dripped on the crowd, greenrooms with no doors, and bathrooms legendary, as one memoirist put it, the way natural disasters are legendary.1 Upstairs, the Palace Hotel rented 224 beds in 105 cubicles to the Bowery’s derelict men — a night cost $2.85 as late as 1980 — and the neighboring storefronts sold restaurant equipment and shoe-trade leather.2 Nothing about the premises explained the sound. In July 1976 the club installed a new PA that was stolen, then replaced, and the replacement made this, by its chronicler’s estimate, the best-sounding live room in New York and maybe the world: Altec A7 speakers, a hundred-watt tube amplifier of the type used at the Indianapolis Motor Speedway, a console built by a Midtown repair shop. It took Hilly Kristal ten years to pay for it, and it meant that a band nobody had signed could hear itself on professional equipment for the price of hauling its own gear through the door.3

Why here, why then

Kristal’s own account of the location is an economics lecture. “Having a rock club on the Bowery, under a flophouse (believe it or not), does have some advantages,” he wrote in 1998: the rent was reasonable ($700 a month when he took the room over in December 1973), the neighbors dressed worse than the rock and rollers, and the surrounding blocks were industrial, so nobody complained about the noise. Within two blocks, six flophouses held about two thousand men; over the years he collected more than three dozen knives and other weapons. “The Bowery was, to repeat, a drab ugly and unsavory place,” he concluded. “But it was good enough for rock and rollers.”4 He came to it sideways. A Village Vanguard manager in the early sixties who had booked Miles Davis and Thelonious Monk, then a Village restaurateur, then co-founder of the Central Park music festival, he had been running the address as a bikers’ bar called Hilly’s on the Bowery since the decade’s turn. In December 1973 he renamed it for the music he intended to book — Country, BlueGrass, Blues, and Other Music For Uplifting Gourmandizers — choosing initials that sounded, he said, a little uncouth. The initials outlived every category on the awning.5

What the room did to the music

The club’s whole influence runs through two rules and a door deal. The rules: a band moved its own equipment, and it played original music — “ORIGINALITY (to me) WAS PRIME, TECHNIQUE TOOK SECOND PLACE,” in Kristal’s own capitals.6 The deal: unknown bands got no guarantee; most of the door money went to the band, the bar stayed with the house, and a band that drew came back. The arrangement made audience-building the whole job and let the club survive on beer while the scene assembled itself. By his own accounting the club did not pay for itself until sometime in 1976.7 Entry was procedural and democratic: mail a live cassette, mostly originals; pass the screening and play a Monday-night audition, a half-hour set; draw, and a weeknight followed; build a following, and the weekend was yours.8 By fall 1976 Kristal was trading bills with Boston’s Rathskeller and Philadelphia’s Hot Club, and the room had become the hub of a national circuit for unsigned original bands — the booking model that Punk’s history credits as the permission made policy.9

The eras of the room

The scene the room is famous for (the Sunday residency, the awning as shrine, the CBGB class of 1975) is told in Punk’s founding branch and in CBGB scene; the room’s own life ran longer on both ends. From late 1982 the club added hardcore matinees, afternoon all-ages shows soon fixed on Sundays, which raised New York hardcore’s next generation until violence pushed Kristal to suspend hardcore booking in 1990.10 The storefront next door at 313, first a record shop and café called the CBGB Record Canteen, became CB’s 313 Gallery, a second stage for the quieter acts the main room’s PA would have flattened, plus a weekly goth night.11 The awning over the door was itself serial: the original was stolen by the Arizona band JFA in 1987 and Kristal sketched a replica for a Bowery sign company; the second sold at Sotheby’s for $30,000 in 2016; the third hangs in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame.12 The end came as a rent fight with the landlord upstairs — by then the Bowery Residents’ Committee, a homeless-services nonprofit. The club’s roughly $19,000 monthly rent was met with a claim of some $90,000 in back rent; the judge ruled the debt false, remarked that CBGB ought to be declared a landmark, and noted all the same that nobody had to renew the lease. It expired August 31, 2005, benefit shows and a Washington Square rally notwithstanding, and the settlement gave the club until fall 2006 to leave.13 The final night, Patti Smith on October 15, 2006, is Punk’s closing exhibit. A menswear store took the room in 2008 and kept stretches of the flyered walls preserved under glass, a museum display in the middle of the sales floor.14

What it changed

The National Park Service’s nomination, written when 313–315 Bowery joined the National Register in 2013, framed the club in institutional terms: a cultural incubator that “fostered new genres of American music” and served the same function as the Bowery’s storied theaters a century before.15 The concrete legacy is the model. Original music as a booking requirement, the door to the band, the audition night as an open front gate — those mechanics made a $700-a-month bar under a flophouse the address where American punk, its art wing, and two generations of hardcore all auditioned. The room closed; the model, and the initials, never did.16
