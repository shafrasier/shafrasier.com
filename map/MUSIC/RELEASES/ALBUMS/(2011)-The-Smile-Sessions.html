The Smile Sessions

Artist: The Beach Boys
Released: November 1, 2011 (recorded 1966–1967)
Label: Capitol Records
Producer: Brian Wilson
Genre:

  • Primary: Baroque pop, psychedelic pop, progressive pop, art pop
  • Secondary: Sunshine pop, sound collage

RYM · Apple Music · Spotify

“Good Vibrations” changes key, tempo, mood, and instrumentation repeatedly across three minutes and thirty-nine seconds while remaining a pop song of overwhelming emotional power. It was assembled from over ninety hours of tape recorded across multiple studios, the most expensive single ever produced at the time (an estimated $50,000, equivalent to roughly $500,000 in 2026 dollars), and it was the proof of concept for something larger: an album-length work that would apply the same modular recording method to an entire suite of American music. Barbershop, ragtime, Hawaiian, Western, avant-garde, and the Beach Boys’ own vocal tradition pushed to its most ecstatic extremes. Brian Wilson called it “a teenage symphony to God.” The album was never finished. The SMiLE Sessions, assembled forty-four years later from the original multitrack recordings, is the closest we can get to hearing what was attempted and what was lost, and the music is extraordinary enough to justify the mythology that grew in the silence.

Musical and production context

Wilson began the sessions in mid-1966, immediately after completing Pet Sounds, with an ambition that had no precedent in popular music. Where Pet Sounds used the Wrecking Crew and Gold Star Studios to create elaborately arranged but structurally conventional songs, SMiLE (2004) abandoned conventional song structure entirely. Wilson recorded short musical sections, sometimes only a few bars, at studios across Los Angeles and planned to assemble them in postproduction, building songs from fragments the way a film editor constructs scenes from takes. The technology of 1966 could barely accommodate the method. Each fragment was a self-contained musical idea, and the composition happened in the sequencing: which fragments followed which, how they connected, what the juxtaposition produced.

The music draws on sources so wide they resist summary. “Heroes and Villains” is a five-minute piece that shifts between cowboy themes, honky-tonk piano, vocal harmonies of staggering intricacy, and passages of pure sonic abstraction. “Cabin Essence” layers banjo, theremin, and voices into something that sounds like American folk music refracted through a psychedelic prism. “Surf’s Up” is Wilson’s most achingly beautiful composition, a song about the failure of communication and the persistence of hope, with the harmonic sophistication of an art song and a lyric (by Van Dyke Parks) whose opacity only deepens its emotional impact. Some fragments name their sources directly: the brief a cappella “Gee” lifts the “how I love my girl” hook from the Crows’ 1953 doo-wop hit of the same name and uses it as introductory material for “Heroes and Villains” in the running order, an explicit homage to the radio-doo-wop tradition Wilson grew up on. The vocal arrangements throughout push the Beach Boys’ harmonies past anything in their catalog. The parts are so closely voiced and chromatically intertwined that individual voices become pure chord, the ensemble functioning as a single instrument.

The collapse

The album fell apart for interlocking reasons. Wilson’s mental health was deteriorating under the weight of his own ambitions and LSD use. Mike Love and Al Jardine resisted material they found uncommercial, pretentious, and impossible to perform live. Capitol Records wanted a follow-up to “Good Vibrations,” not an avant-garde art project. And the competitive pressure from the Beatles — who had responded to Pet Sounds with Revolver and were now working on Sgt. Pepper’s — devastated Wilson. The acute blow came in early 1967, when Wilson heard “Strawberry Fields Forever” on his car radio and reportedly pulled over in tears, convinced the Beatles had reached the territory he’d been heading toward. Whether the comparison was accurate matters less than the fact that Wilson believed it. Sgt. Pepper’s, released that June while SMiLE remained unfinished, only confirmed the feeling. Smiley Smile (1967), the album released in its place, used fragments of the sessions stripped of their orchestral arrangements and re-recorded at home — an intimate, lo-fi record with its own strange beauty that represents a fraction of what was planned.

What it inherits and what it introduces

SMiLE inherits everything Wilson had developed through Pet Sounds — the Wall of Sound production principles, the chamber pop arrangements, the vocal harmony vocabulary — and pushes all of it into genuinely avant-garde territory. What it introduces, even in this posthumous reconstruction, is the concept of the pop album as a collage of American sounds and myths: a musical equivalent of the mural or the great American novel, incorporating folk, classical, popular, and experimental traditions into a single sustained work. The modular recording technique, building compositions from independently recorded fragments rather than tracking whole performances, anticipated the working methods of hip-hop sampling and electronic music production by decades. Wilson was composing the way producers would work in the digital era, using analog tape and razor blades.

Influence and legacy

The SMiLE mythology extends beyond the music itself. The archetype of the ambitious, unreleased album — from Prince’s Black Album to Lauryn Hill’s rumored follow-up to The Miseducation — descends from Wilson’s collapse. Before SMiLE, an unfinished album was a commercial failure. After it, an unfinished album could be a legend, the silence itself generating a mythology that no completed work could match. Musically, the sessions’ influence is audible in the art pop and experimental traditions that emerged in the 2000s: Animal Collective, Panda Bear, Ariel Pink, and the broader freak-folk and neo-psychedelic movements all work in SMiLE’s wake, drawn to its fusion of harmonic beauty and structural adventurousness.

Wilson’s 2004 solo completion (Brian Wilson Presents SMiLE) brought the music to a wider audience, but the 2011 Sessions release is the definitive document: the original Beach Boys vocals, the original Wrecking Crew backing tracks, preserved in the state of beautiful incompletion where their creator left them.
