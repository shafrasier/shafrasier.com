Bringing It All Back Home

Bringing It All Back Home is split down the middle, and the split is the statement. Side one is electric — Bob Dylan backed, for the first time on record, by a rock-and-roll band. Side two is acoustic — Dylan alone with guitar and harmonica, the way the folk world still wanted him.1 He gave each side its full due, the band on one and the lone voice on the other, and the seam between them became one of the dividing lines of 1960s music.2

Musical and production context

Dylan cut the album in three days in January 1965, with Tom Wilson producing for the last time and a loose studio band led by the guitarist Bruce Langhorne, whose giant Turkish tambourine had already handed Dylan a song title.3 Side one is a rush of electric blues and Chuck Berry energy. “Subterranean Homesick Blues” opens the record at a sprint, a torrent of internal rhyme delivered faster than the ear can hold — half talking blues, half something that would not have a name for years.4 “Maggie’s Farm” turns a work song into a flat refusal to labor on anyone else’s terms, the song Dylan would carry, electric, onto the stage at Newport six months later.5

Side two sets the band down. These are the longest, densest acoustic songs Dylan had written, visionary and image-saturated, owing as much to Arthur Rimbaud and the Beats as to any folk source.6 “Mr. Tambourine Man” is a hymn to inspiration itself, a plea to the title figure to play a song and lead the singer off into reverie. “Gates of Eden” and “It’s Alright, Ma (I’m Only Bleeding)” go darker and wider — the latter a seven-minute indictment of money, war, and conformity that carries some of the most-quoted lines he ever wrote, the one about being busy born or busy dying chief among them.7 The record closes on “It’s All Over Now, Baby Blue”, a farewell so resonant it has been heard ever since as Dylan saying goodbye to the folk movement on his way out the door.8

What it inherits and what it introduces

The album finishes what Another Side of Bob Dylan had started. Side two is the acoustic, surreal songwriting of Another Side taken to its limit, longer and stranger. Side one is the leap Another Side only pointed toward, made this time with a full electric band behind him.9 What the record offered everyone else was proof of concept: that a literary lyric and a rock-and-roll backbeat belonged on the same track, the case the rest of the sixties would build on.

Reception

The record broke him commercially. It reached number six in America — his first album in the top ten — and number one in Britain, and “Subterranean Homesick Blues” gave him his first Hot 100 single.10 The promotional film Dylan made for it, flipping hand-lettered cue cards in a London alley while the song plays, is routinely cited as a forerunner of the music video.11 Among folk purists the electric side set off alarm, and the booing that would greet him at Newport that July was already audible in the reviews. But the album’s reach was vast, and it announced that folk’s most celebrated songwriter had walked out of the genre and taken his audience with him.

Influence and legacy

The album’s largest consequence arrived in someone else’s hands. Two months after it appeared, The Byrds released a jangling, electric twelve-string version of “Mr. Tambourine Man” and took it to number one on both sides of the Atlantic; folk rock was suddenly a movement with a name and a hit.12 Within a year the fusion it modeled, literary words over an electric band, was the dominant sound in Anglo-American pop. For Dylan, side one was the door: the run of Highway 61 Revisited and Blonde on Blonde came straight out of it. Bringing It All Back Home is where the folk singer ended and the rock artist began — and where, on side two, he proved he was surrendering nothing he couldn’t replace.
