Another Side of Bob Dylan

In a single three-hour session on the night of June 9, 1964 — fueled, by one account, by a couple of bottles of Beaujolais — Bob Dylan recorded fourteen new songs, eleven of which became Another Side of Bob Dylan.1 The haste was the point. Where The Times They Are a-Changin’ had labored for months over public injustices, this one was written and cut fast and loose, turning the songs inward toward love, doubt, language, and play.2 The topical assignment is gone, and what fills the space is a writer whose subject, this time, is mostly himself.

Musical and production context

Tom Wilson again produced, and the session’s haste shows in the best way: these are first thoughts, takes with the ink still wet.3 Dylan plays piano on the loose, rolling “Black Crow Blues”, and several tracks have the half-improvised feel of a writer trying lines out loud. Gone are the finger-pointing ballads. In their place sit comic talking blues (“I Shall Be Free No. 10”, “Motorpsycho Nitemare”), love songs by turns tender and barbed, and a looser, more image-driven writing that had not been there before.

The turn is clearest in the songs that argue with his own recent self. “My Back Pages” is the renunciation made explicit, a verse-by-verse recantation of the certainties of the protest years, each stanza closing on the line “I was so much older then, I’m younger than that now.”4 “It Ain’t Me Babe” turns a lover’s brush-off into a public one, refusing the savior role his audience kept pressing on him — the answer-man insisting he is not the one they want.5 Even the tenderness cuts both ways: “To Ramona” is a tender talking-to, half comfort and half farewell, and “Ballad in Plain D”, eight minutes on the collapse of his relationship with Suze Rotolo, is so raw that Dylan later called it vengeful and self-indulgent and wished he had left it unwritten.6

Only one song still faces outward. “Chimes of Freedom” is all image — lightning heard as tolling bells, ringing for refugees, the unjustly jailed, “every hung-up person in the whole wide universe” — the social conscience of the protest years dissolved into symbolist poetry.7 The Byrds heard the future in it: within a year they had wired “Chimes,” “All I Really Want to Do”, and two more of these songs into the opening records of folk rock.8

What it inherits and what it introduces

Underneath the new subjects, the form is the one Dylan carried from the coffeehouses: one voice, one guitar, the first-person folk song.9 What is new all points forward — the long lines spilling past the meter, and a free-associative imagery that would define the next two years of his writing. It is the last album Dylan made alone, before Bringing It All Back Home arrived the following spring with a band and an electric guitar and changed everything again.

Reception

At the time, the folk movement called the inward turn a defection. In an open letter in Sing Out!, the magazine’s editor Irwin Silber chided Dylan that his new songs were “all inner-directed now” and that he had “lost touch with people,” seduced by “the paraphernalia of fame.”10 The charge — that going personal was a kind of betrayal — would shadow Dylan for a year and break open at Newport the next summer. Commercially the record did modestly, reaching number forty-three in America and number eight in Britain; critically it now ranks among his essential transitional records, the bridge scholars use to mark where Dylan’s writing changed direction.11

Influence and legacy

Another Side’s influence circled back through the Byrds: their electric versions of these songs helped invent folk rock, and that new jangle fed into Dylan’s own writing within months.12 “My Back Pages” left a mark of its own — its refrain, a twenty-three-year-old calling his former certainties naive, passed into the language as shorthand for thinking better of one’s younger self. The folk world received the album as a retreat; it was the first clear look at the writer Dylan actually was.

See also

  • The Times They Are a-Changin’ (1964) — the companion record six months earlier: where that album is the protest peak and signs off in “Restless Farewell,” this is the morning after, the turn fully taken
  • Folk rock — the genre Another Side seeded at home: within a year the Byrds had turned four of its songs into the electric, chiming records that launched the form
  • Authenticity and its discontents — Silber’s “betrayal” letter and “My Back Pages” are a founding case of authenticity redefined as the freedom to change, against an audience that wanted Dylan fixed in place
