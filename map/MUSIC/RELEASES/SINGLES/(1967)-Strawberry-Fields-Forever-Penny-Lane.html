A Mellotron set to its flute voice plays four bars of something that sounds like a barrel organ heard underwater, and one of the strangest, most ambitious double A-sides in pop arrives.1 Released in February 1967 as the first products of the sessions that became Sgt. Pepper’s, the single pairs two Liverpool childhood memories rendered by the two writers in opposite directions: Lennon’s “Strawberry Fields Forever,” which dissolves a remembered place into a hallucination assembled on tape, and McCartney’s “Penny Lane,” which sharpens a remembered street into hyperreal color. It failed to reach number one in Britain, the first Beatles single since 1963 not to top the chart there, which says less about the records than about how completely they had outrun what a pop single was expected to do.

After the road

The single is the sound of a band that had just stopped performing. The Beatles played their last commercial concert at Candlestick Park in San Francisco on August 29, 1966, and walked off the touring circuit for good, exhausted by stadiums where no one could hear them play.2 When they reconvened at Abbey Road that November, they were a studio band for the first time, with no schedule but the recording calendar and no audience but the tape machine. The first thing they recorded in the new arrangement, on November 24, 1966, was “Strawberry Fields Forever.”3 Martin said it set the agenda for everything that followed.

Both “Strawberry Fields Forever” and “Penny Lane” came out of those early sessions for the album that became Sgt. Pepper’s, and both were pulled to fill a single when EMI pressed for a new release. Neither appears on the album. Martin regretted the decision for the rest of his life, calling the choice to spend the two songs on a single — and so strip them from the record they were written for — the biggest mistake of his professional career.4 The logic at the time was that a band of the Beatles’ stature could not go six months without a record; the cost was that Sgt. Pepper’s lost its two strongest tracks before it was finished.

”Strawberry Fields Forever”

Lennon wrote the song in Almería, Spain, in the autumn of 1966, on acoustic guitar between scenes of the film How I Won the War, picking out a slow, melancholic melody about the grounds of Strawberry Field — a Salvation Army children’s home in the Woolton district of Liverpool, near the house where his Aunt Mimi raised him, where he had played as a boy and loved the summer garden fêtes.5 He pluralized the name for the title. What the demo became over weeks of recording was something pop had not contained before.

The released version is two performances spliced into one. Lennon recorded the song in late November, then decided weeks later that he wanted the gentle opening of an early take joined to the heavier orchestral arrangement of a later one — except the two were in different keys and at different tempos, take 7 in B-flat and take 26 in C, a semitone and a good deal of speed apart.6 He asked Martin and engineer Geoff Emerick to make it work anyway. They did it by varispeed, slowing the faster take down by roughly eleven percent until its pitch and tempo drifted close enough to the other’s to be cut together, the join falling at almost exactly one minute, on the second chorus.7 The seam is faintly audible once you know it is there, a slight lurch in the texture; the achievement is that it passes as a single continuous dream, the song appearing to change states of consciousness mid-phrase. Slowing the second take is also what thickens Lennon’s voice in the back half, smearing it into the drugged, underwater quality the whole record lives in.

The instrumentation is a catalog of the studio used as an instrument rather than a recording medium. The Mellotron’s flute patch carries the introduction, one of the first times the machine was used as a lead voice on a pop record.8 Harrison bows an Indian swarmandal; Martin scores three cellos and four trumpets into the second half, played in registers that make brass and strings sound bruised rather than stately; reversed cymbals hiss in backward; tape loops run at altered speeds underneath.9 After the song seems to end, it fades out and then fades back in for a free-form coda of drums, swarmandal, and Lennon muttering at the edge of audibility, as though the record cannot quite let the memory close.10 The result sounds like remembering itself, vivid and unreachable at the same time.

This is the single that floored Brian Wilson. He heard it on his car radio while still working on SMiLE, pulled over, and took it as proof the Beatles had reached the avant-garde pop territory he was chasing — “they got there first.”11 Whether the comparison was fair mattered less than Wilson’s conviction that it was true; the discouragement it deepened is part of why SMiLE went unfinished.

”Penny Lane”

McCartney answered with a song built from the opposite impulse. Where “Strawberry Fields” dissolves a place, “Penny Lane” renders one in hard, sunlit focus: the bus roundabout in the Mossley Hill district of Liverpool where he and Lennon used to meet, populated by a cast drawn with the clarity of a children’s picture book.12 A barber displays photographs of the heads he has had the pleasure to know; a banker waits for a trim without a coat in the pouring rain; a fireman keeps his clean fire engine clean; a pretty nurse sells poppies from a tray and feels as if she’s in a play.13 McCartney chased what he called a clean American sound, modeled on the bright separation of Pet Sounds, and built the track on layered keyboards rather than a conventional band setup, stacking a rhythm piano with a second one fed through a guitar amplifier for added treble and reverb.14

The song that lands as effortless pop is harmonically restless underneath. The verses sit in B major and the choruses drop to A major, the two keys hinged by pivot chords so smooth most listeners never register the modulation, with a brief swerve into B minor — the parallel minor borrowed for a passing shadow — that darkens the verse before the brightness returns. That swerve is modal interchange: a chord pulled from the parallel mode of the same tonic, the move that gives the verse its flicker of melancholy without ever leaving the key, the same device Wilson had threaded through Pet Sounds a year earlier.15 The signature gesture sits on top of all this. The piccolo trumpet solo, played by David Mason of the New Philharmonia, came at McCartney’s request after he watched Mason perform Bach’s Brandenburg Concerto No. 2 on television; he sang the lines he wanted, Martin notated them, and Mason cut them on a B-flat piccolo trumpet at Abbey Road on January 17, 1967.16 The bright clarino tone lifts a song about a suburban bus stop into a register pop had never borrowed before.

The double A-side as statement

Issuing the two songs together was its own declaration. The Beatles had enough material at this level to spend it on a single rather than bank it for the album, and the pairing distilled the Lennon-McCartney partnership at its furthest reach: Lennon turning a memory inward until it warped, McCartney turning one outward until it gleamed, each arriving somewhere pop had not been. That the two could share a record without either diminishing the other is the partnership’s argument in miniature.

Reception

In Britain the single stalled at number two on the Record Retailer chart — the chart now treated as the official one — held off the top by Engelbert Humperdinck’s “Release Me” (1967), a sentimental ballad outselling the record that had just redrawn what a pop single could be.17 It was the band’s first single since 1963 not to reach number one there, though the gap was partly a chart-rule artifact: a double A-side split its sales across two titles, and on the Melody Maker chart, which counted them together, the record spent three weeks at the top.18 In America the sides were tracked separately, and “Penny Lane” went to number one on the Billboard Hot 100 while “Strawberry Fields Forever” peaked at number eight.19

The chart shortfall reads now as a footnote to a record that the critical consensus eventually placed near the summit of the form. “Strawberry Fields Forever” in particular became a fixed reference point for psychedelia and for the studio as a compositional tool — Rolling Stone moved it from number 76 to number seven on its greatest-songs list in 2021, the Beatles’ highest placement there — while “Penny Lane” settled into the canon as the bright twin, the proof that the same studio sophistication could serve warmth instead of vertigo.20

Influence and legacy

“Strawberry Fields Forever” set the template for psychedelic pop as studio art: a record whose sound exists only on tape, whose production is inseparable from its identity. Every later pop record that treats the studio as a hallucinatory instrument — from Radiohead’s “Everything in Its Right Place” to Frank Ocean’s “Nights” — works in territory this single opened. “Penny Lane” proved the complementary point: that classical instrumentation and pop songwriting could merge with neither losing its identity, and that the most sophisticated arrangement could disappear entirely into pleasure. Together they demonstrated that a single could carry the artistic weight of an album, which is the premise the next decade of pop ambition was built on.

  • Pet Sounds (1966) — the record Brian Wilson was still chasing when “Strawberry Fields Forever” stopped him cold on the radio; “Penny Lane” reaches back toward its clean American sound by design
