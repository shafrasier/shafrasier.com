"Will You Love Me Tomorrow" (1960)

"Will You Love Me Tomorrow"

Artist: The Shirelles
Released: November 7, 1960
Label: Scepter Records
Producer: Luther Dixon
Studio: Bell Sound Studios, New York
Songwriters: Gerry Goffin, Carole King
Genre:

  • Primary: Girl group, Brill Building, pop soul, rhythm & blues

RYM · Apple Music · Spotify

The first girl group record to reach number one on the Billboard Hot 100, and the song that proved the Brill Building model could produce pop music of genuine emotional depth. “Will You Love Me Tomorrow” is a question about sex disguised as a question about romance — will you still want me after tonight? — asked with a directness that the early 1960s’ pop conventions should not have permitted. That it got through, and that it connected with millions of listeners, is evidence of what Gerry Goffin’s lyrics and Carole King’s melody could do when the craft was precise enough to smuggle real feeling past every commercial filter in its path.

Musical and production context

Luther Dixon’s production keeps the arrangement spare enough that the emotional weight falls on the voice and the question it’s asking. Strings provide warmth without overwhelming; the rhythm section stays restrained. King’s melody moves in stepwise motion through the verse, building tension that the chorus releases, and the structure is tight enough that the whole thing feels inevitable by the second listen. The Shirelles’ vocal arrangement — Shirley Owens’s lead against the group’s close harmonies — places the question in a communal context: this isn’t one girl asking, it’s every girl who’s ever had to calculate the cost of trust.

What it inherits and what it introduces

The song inherits Tin Pan Alley’s melodic craftsmanship and the doo-wop vocal group tradition, but it introduces something the Brill Building would make its signature: lyrics that treat teenage emotional experience as worthy of adult psychological precision. Goffin’s lyric doesn’t condescend to its audience. The question it asks is genuinely unresolved — the song offers no reassurance, no easy answer — and that ambiguity is what gives it durability. It opened the door for every subsequent Brill Building song that treated pop as a vehicle for real emotional complexity: “Up on the Roof”, “Walk on By”, “Be My Baby” (1963), “You’ve Lost That Lovin’ Feelin’” (1964).

Chart performance and cultural impact

“Will You Love Me Tomorrow” reached number one on the Hot 100 in January 1961, making the Shirelles the first Black girl group to top the pop chart. The crossover success demonstrated that Black female vocal groups could sell to white audiences without softening the material — a commercial breakthrough that Berry Gordy was watching closely from Detroit.

Influence and legacy

The song’s influence is less sonic than structural: it established the template that the Brill Building’s greatest work would follow. A professional songwriting team writes a song of real emotional weight, a vocal group delivers it with conviction that renders authorship irrelevant, and a producer shapes the result for the widest possible audience. The division of labor, far from diluting the music, concentrates it. Carole King’s own recording of the song on Tapestry (1971), a decade later, strips the arrangement to piano and voice and proves the melody can carry the weight alone — but the Shirelles’ version remains the definitive one, because the collective voice asking the question is part of what makes it land.
