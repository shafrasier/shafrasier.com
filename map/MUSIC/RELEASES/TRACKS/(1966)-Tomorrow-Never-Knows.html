The first thing recorded for Revolver was the last thing anyone expected to hear on a pop album. On April 6, 19661, the Beatles walked into Studio Three at Abbey Road and, instead of cutting another single, built a track that had more in common with the tape experiments of Karlheinz Stockhausen and the drone music of Indian classical tradition than with anything on the pop charts. “Tomorrow Never Knows” sits on a single chord of C major. Nothing resolves. The vocal floats over loops of manipulated tape while a drum pattern holds the only pulse. It lasts two minutes and fifty-eight seconds, and by the time it ends, the vocabulary of what recorded popular music could attempt has permanently expanded.

Musical and production context

Lennon arrived at the session with a set of lyrics adapted from Timothy Leary, Richard Alpert, and Ralph Metzner’s The Psychedelic Experience (1964), itself a rewriting of the Tibetan Book of the Dead as a manual for LSD use.2 He wanted his voice to sound like the Dalai Lama singing from a mountaintop, or a hundred chanting monks.3 The working title was “Mark I.” The final title came from one of Ringo Starr’s malapropisms — “tomorrow never knows” instead of “tomorrow never comes” — and the mismatch between the lyric’s cosmic ambition and the title’s comic deflation is pure Beatles.4

The session was Geoff Emerick’s first as balance engineer, a twenty-year-old promoted to replace5 Norman Smith. His solutions to Lennon’s demands were immediate and permanent. He ran Lennon’s vocal through a Leslie rotating speaker cabinet, the first time a voice had been processed through a device designed for Hammond organs, giving it the quality of transmission from a great distance.6 He close-miked Ringo’s drums against EMI’s standing rules, producing a sound that was punchy and present where Abbey Road’s standard drum recordings were distant and polite.7 Ken Townsend’s automatic double-tracking system, invented during these sessions specifically to spare Lennon the tedium of singing everything twice, thickened the vocal image.8

The tape loops were the track’s strangest and most consequential element. All four Beatles had prepared them at home, recording onto open-reel machines and physically cutting the tape into loops that would repeat continuously. Five loops made the final mix: Paul McCartney’s laughter sped up until it resembled seagulls, a sustained orchestral chord, a Mellotron on its flute setting, another Mellotron alternating between B-flat and C, and a sitar phrase recorded with heavy saturation and played at increased speed.9 On the evening of April 7, Emerick and Martin fed the loops into the Studio Three console from tape machines set up in corridors and adjoining rooms throughout Abbey Road, faders controlled by EMI technicians who could not hear the full mix. The resulting collage was performed live to tape in a single pass. Every mix was unique; the one on the album is the one they happened to get.10

What it inherits and what it introduces

The debts are specific. The drone comes from Indian classical music, which Harrison had been absorbing since meeting Ravi Shankar, and which Lennon encountered obliquely through Leary’s psychedelic rewriting of Eastern philosophy.11 The tape manipulation descends from the musique concrète of Pierre Schaeffer and Pierre Henry and from the electronic experiments Stockhausen was conducting in Cologne12, which Lennon knew about through his art-school education and McCartney through the London avant-garde.13 The rhythmic insistence — Ringo’s pattern is a compressed, syncopated figure with accents that pull against the backbeat, closer to a ritual pulse than a rock groove — owes something to the drone-based repetition of Terry Riley’s early minimalist work. None of these influences were new in 1966. Placing all of them inside a track on a major-label rock album was.

The track proved that the recording studio could function as a compositional instrument on a pop record, that tape itself was raw material for musical construction, and that a rock song could abandon conventional harmony and song form without ceasing to be compelling. Everything that followed reached for those three principles. Psychedelic rock’s studio experiments through 1967 and 1968 all work in the space “Tomorrow Never Knows” opened, from the Beatles’ own Sgt. Pepper’s to Pink Floyd’s The Piper at the Gates of Dawn. The drone and loop structures anticipate krautrock14 — Can’s Tago Mago (1971) and Tangerine Dream’s early electronics operate on principles the track demonstrated were viable within rock. The tape-loop collage method resurfaces in sampling culture: the principle that recorded sound can be cut, manipulated, and reassembled into new compositions runs from “Tomorrow Never Knows” through hip-hop’s sample-based production and electronic dance music’s loop architecture.

Influence and legacy

The track was sequenced as Revolver’s closing song, a decision that frames the album as a journey ending at its most extreme point. It was also the first track recorded for the album, which means the sessions began with the most radical experiment and spent the next three months filling in the territory between that boundary and the album’s more conventional songs. The sequencing invites the listener to arrive at “Tomorrow Never Knows” only after “Eleanor Rigby” (1966), “Here, There and Everywhere”, and “Got to Get You into My Life” have established that the album is still, recognizably, pop. The final track then redefines what that word can contain.

The innovations were absorbed so completely that the track’s strangeness has faded. Leslie-processed vocals, close-miked drums, tape manipulation, drone structures, and studio-as-instrument production are all standard vocabulary now. In 1966, none of them existed in pop. The distance between “Tomorrow Never Knows” and the Beatles’ previous album closer, Rubber Soul’s “Run for Your Life”, is the distance between two different conceptions of what recorded music is for. One is a performance captured; the other is a construction built. That distinction runs through every subsequent argument about authenticity and the nature of the pop record.
