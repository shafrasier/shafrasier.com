British beat boom

Rock → British beat boom

Location: United Kingdom (Liverpool, then London, then the provinces)
Period: 1963–1966
Key genres: Beat music, Merseybeat, British R&B, mod R&B

RYM

For about three years you could hear the British charts change. The radio in 1962 still ran on orchestrated ballads and American imports sung by men in dinner jackets; by the end of 1963 it ran on guitars: three or four young men to a record, harmonising over a hammered backbeat in accents that had never been let near a national hit. The sound itself was beat, worked out in Hamburg cellars and Liverpool halls before London paid attention. The British beat boom is what happened when that sound reached the singles chart and would not get off it: a commercial event with a shape of its own — two waves and a geography — that rebuilt who got signed in Britain and where a hit could come from. It ran from the early months of 1963, when Merseybeat broke out of Liverpool, to 1966, when the album began to outweigh the single and the boom’s economy came apart.

The chart before the boom

Before the boom the British chart had a sound of its own, and it belonged to grown-ups: a solo singer out front and an orchestra behind, performing a song written in a publisher’s office and handed down to whoever could put it over. The seven-inch single was the whole of pop commerce, and the long-player was a cardboard sleeve of hits you already owned, sold to people who did not buy many records. By 1962 there were thousands of teenage guitar bands in Britain, built out of the skiffle craze and playing American records back to one another in church halls, and the chart had no door for any of them. A four-piece that wrote its own songs and took orders from no staff writer did not fit the machinery. The boom is the sound of that door coming off its hinges.

The first wave: Merseybeat takes the charts

It started with a harmonica. The Beatles’ “Love Me Do” crept to number seventeen at the end of 1962 — a thin, bluesy thing, a harmonica over two voices — the first record by a Liverpool group to chart nationally. Months later “Please Please Me” reached number two on the trade chart that would become the official one, and number one on the papers’ own listings. The kind of band the business had kept out was suddenly sitting at the top of it.

One number one is a fluke. What came next was a gold rush. A&R men who had spent 1962 showing beat groups the door spent 1963 signing anything with a guitar and a northern vowel. Decca had auditioned the Beatles on New Year’s Day 1962 and passed — its man Dick Rowe is supposed to have decided guitar groups were finished — and now it could not get to Liverpool fast enough. Brian Epstein’s NEMS handed the capital’s labels a whole city’s worth of acts and they took the lot. Gerry and the Pacemakers sent their first three singles straight to number one: “How Do You Do It?”, “I Like It”, and “You’ll Never Walk Alone”, a feat no British act had managed, and the record held for twenty years. By that autumn the chart read like a Cavern bill: the Searchers, Billy J. Kramer and the Dakotas, Cilla Black, the Swinging Blue Jeans.

The second wave: London R&B rides in

The second wave was louder and it scowled. Late in 1963 The Rolling Stones scraped to number twelve with “I Wanna Be Your Man” (1963), a Lennon-McCartney throwaway the Beatles simply gave them, so that the band that had started the boom wrote the next wave its first hit. But where Liverpool had come up on rock and roll and the harmony groups, London had grown up in the R&B clubs, learning Chicago blues in art-school back rooms, and it arrived meaner. The Kinks got there first: two chords, a razor-slashed speaker cone, and “You Really Got Me”, the hardest number one of 1964. The Yardbirds stretched blues covers into rave-ups; The Who hammered R&B toward feedback in the mod clubs.

The two waves sold opposite pictures of the same young audience. The Liverpool acts arrived in matching suits and bowed together; the London groups were styled to look like they would not. Andrew Loog Oldham, who managed the Stones, worked the contrast on purpose, down to planting the question of whether you would let your daughter near one. The boom was wide enough to run both pictures at once, and the labels now had two products for the same chart.

The provincial breakthrough

The boom also redrew the map. A British pop career had always run through London — the labels, the studios, the managers, and the press all sat inside a few square miles of the capital — and a provincial act either moved there or stayed a local secret. After the Beatles, the labels started looking outward instead, because a record that sold to the whole country had just come from a port three hundred miles north.

Newcastle made the loudest case. The Animals cut “House of the Rising Sun” in a single take in May 1964 — four and a half minutes of minor-key organ and a long climbing guitar figure, far too slow and too long to be a single, everyone said — and it went to number one in Britain in July and number one in America in September, the first British chart-topper in the States that was not a Beatles record. The provinces kept coming: Manchester’s the Hollies, Birmingham’s the Spencer Davis Group and the Moody Blues, and the Zombies, schoolfriends from St Albans whose first single went straight to number two in America. Cities that had only ever bought records were now making them.

The commercial machine

Behind the records was a new kind of business, and the men who made the hits increasingly did not work for the labels. Independent managers and producers developed the acts themselves and leased the finished tapes to Decca, Parlophone, and Pye, keeping the control the majors used to hold. Epstein had built the model and Oldham ran the Stones on it; even George Martin, the one company man in the story, worked as the Beatles’ collaborator more than their supervisor. The label was turning into a pipe the music ran through.

Television did the rest. Top of the Pops went on the air on New Year’s Day 1964, and its first half hour was almost a boom sampler — the Stones, the Dave Clark Five, the Hollies, the Swinging Blue Jeans, and the Beatles at number one — while Ready Steady Go! had been handing Friday nights over to the music since the previous August. Package tours ran a dozen chart acts through provincial cinemas on a single ticket. Under all of it sat the seven-inch single, the cheap object the whole economy was built to sell. At the start of 1964 the thing went transatlantic: the Beatles broke in America, the export wave that became The British Invasion carried the boom worldwide, and that April they held all five of the top places on the American singles chart at once. What America made of it belongs to the invasion. The boom’s own feat was the machine itself: built in a single year, and big enough to make that possible.

Key artists

  • The Beatles — The band that lit the fuse and then walked off before the smoke cleared. Their breakthrough fixed the chart pattern everyone else copied, and when they stopped writing three-minute singles for the album, after Rubber Soul (1965), the boom lost its center of gravity
  • Gerry and the Pacemakers — The first wave at its sunniest: three number ones from a standing start in 1963, a grin and a clear tenor, not one of the three written by the band. When the chart began to prize songwriting they had nothing of their own to offer, and it was over almost as fast as it had begun
  • The Rolling Stones — The second wave’s starting gun. Number twelve in December 1963 looks slight on paper, but it told every label that the London blues clubs were a chart proposition, and most of what was loud and surly over the next three years traces back to it
  • The Animals — The provinces going all the way. A Newcastle band carried a traditional folk lament to number one on both sides of the Atlantic in 1964 and proved a worldwide hit no longer needed a London address
  • The Dave Clark Five — The boom as something you could franchise. Sold out of Tottenham as the Beatles’ rivals, the Five stamped seventeen American Top 40 hits between 1964 and 1967 onto a stomping, saxophone-heavy beat that exported even when the songs did not last
  • The Zombies — The far edge of the boom’s reach. A breathy, minor-key single built on a jazz-schooled keyboard run, number two in America before the band had toured, out of a town with no scene at all; their real masterpiece arrived only in 1968, once the boom was over

Foundational records

  • The Beatles, “Love Me Do” (1962) — A harmonica over two voices, scraping to number seventeen: thin, bluesy, and the first Liverpool record to chart nationally. The boom’s quiet first knock
  • Gerry and the Pacemakers, “How Do You Do It?” (1963) — The song George Martin first pressed on the Beatles, who refused it; handed to Gerry’s group it went straight to number one and set the stampede going
  • The Rolling Stones, “I Wanna Be Your Man” (1963) — Lennon and McCartney finished it in front of the Stones, who waited; the number-twelve cover opened the second front, the boom’s two camps meeting on one record
  • The Animals, “House of the Rising Sun” (1964) — A slow, brooding ballad, all patience where the rest of the boom sprinted; number one in Britain and America, and the first British number one in the States with no Beatle attached. Proof the chart would now follow a provincial band almost anywhere
  • The Zombies, “She’s Not There” (1964) — Minor-key and jazz-touched, a cool outlier among the boom’s bright harmonies; a debut single from a no-scene town that reached number two in America
  • The Spencer Davis Group, “Keep On Running” (1965) — Birmingham at number one in January 1966, the provinces still arriving just as the singles boom began to ebb
  • The Beatles, Rubber Soul (1965) — The flagship stepping off the single and onto the album: the first sign that the chart the boom had conquered was about to be devalued

Dissolution

The boom was outgrown. By 1966 the album was becoming the thing that mattered, and the audience was splitting into two rooms: a singles crowd still filed under “pop,” and an “underground” that bought LPs and treated them as art. Beat was singles music, and most beat groups had nowhere to go. A handful crossed over by turning into something else entirely — the Beatles, the Rolling Stones, the Kinks, the Who — and the rest, built for a chart that no longer wanted them, drifted into cabaret and the nostalgia circuit or out of music for good. When the Beatles released Sgt. Pepper’s on 26 May 1967, the form the boom had ridden was already a period piece.

The same split hardened into a permanent hierarchy. Singles pop was filed as disposable and album rock as serious, and the boom’s own achievement — a group writing and selling its own hits — became the test other music would be measured against. That value system is the one Authenticity and its discontents and The pop factory work through.

Further reading
