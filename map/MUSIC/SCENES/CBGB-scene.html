CBGB scene

Punk → CBGB scene

Periodc. 1974–1980
Location315 Bowery, New York
Genres

LinksRYM

The scene began as a favor on a bar’s worst night. CBGB’s Sundays were dead until the spring of 1974, when Terry Ork, a Warhol-orbit film-bookstore manager, offered Hilly Kristal a fix: give the night to Television, the band Ork had assembled at his day job by introducing the clerks and customers of a Greenwich Village film bookstore to one another until they were a rhythm section and two guitarists, and the bar would at least sell beer to their friends.1 What the Sunday residency actually did was open a door that the club’s one booking rule held open for everyone after them: original music only, a rule that selected for writers and said nothing about sound. That is why the bands that made the room famous had almost nothing in common. Television built cathedrals of interlocking guitar; the Ramones stripped songs to their studs. Patti Smith read poetry over garage chords while Talking Heads played rhythm like an engineering problem, and Blondie wrote chart pop years before the charts agreed. Punk tells the room’s story, awning to final night; this note follows the cohort — how it assembled, argued, recorded, and left.

Eighteen months of arrivals

The cohort gathered fast. The Ramones came in from Forest Hills in August 1974, four months into the Sunday era, and treated the club like a rehearsal room with witnesses: they played it more than seventy times before the year ended, sets averaging about seventeen minutes, the songs so short that early audiences sometimes got the whole catalog twice.2 Blondie assembled the same season from the wreckage of the Stilettos, a girl-group revival trio whose guitarist, Chris Stein, left with the singer, Debbie Harry, a onetime Max’s Kansas City waitress, to build something of their own.3 In the spring of 1975 the room got its proof of concept: seven weeks of Patti Smith and Television on a shared bill, two sets a night, four nights a week, alternating who closed. The run gave Smith’s group a tour’s worth of stage time without leaving Manhattan, worked up most of what became Horses, and drew Clive Davis down to the Bowery; she signed a seven-album deal with Arista in April, before the residency’s chairs were cold.4 Two months later, on June 5, 1975, an art-school trio called Talking Heads played their first show anywhere, opening for the Ramones for an audience that started at about ten people. David Byrne and Chris Frantz had played together at RISD; when their student band dissolved, Frantz talked his girlfriend Tina Weymouth into learning bass so that the new one could exist. They played “Psycho Killer” that first night.5

That August the press arrived and found the scene already formed. Kristal’s three-week festival of unsigned bands, thirty-some acts and up to six a night, drew James Wolcott’s Village Voice cover story, still the best period reading of the room: “No longer is the impulse revolutionary — i.e., the transformation of oneself and society — but conservative: to carry on the rock tradition.” Wolcott heard Television’s “loud intimacy,” saw a Verlaine who admitted “if I’m thinking up there, I’m not having a good night,” pegged Talking Heads as “minimalists” with “a clean, flat image, devoid of fine shading and color,” and called the Ramones “a killer band.”6 The judgment matters because the scene shared it: these were restorationists, stripping rock back to its premise, playing for “musicians and their girlfriends, couples on dates, friends and relatives of band members.” Before London weaponized the word punk, the Bowery’s own vocabulary was milder — the trade name in circulation was “street rock,” though the word itself had been on downtown flyers since 1970.7 The name locked in when Punk magazine’s founders came to the club in November 1975 to scout the Ramones.8

The split

The scene’s founding argument ran through one band. Television began as an even partnership between Tom Verlaine and Richard Hell, and it broke on the question of what a song was for. Verlaine came to refuse Hell’s songs in concert, “Blank Generation” among them, and told him to “stop moving” onstage — the frenzy was upstaging the guitars. Hell left in early 1975, co-founded the Heartbreakers with two ex-Dolls that May, left again, and built Richard Hell & the Voidoids: each exit seeded another of the scene’s bands.9 The stakes were aesthetic and they mapped the scene’s whole territory. Verlaine’s wing was poetry, patience, and interlock; Hell’s was the shout and the self-invented ruin, the wardrobe London would come shopping for. The exported half conquered the world’s imagination; the patient half stayed on the Bowery and made Marquee Moon.10 The room held other frictions. The night in 1976 when Wayne County answered a Dictators singer’s heckling with a microphone stand and a broken collarbone says the queer wing and the wiseguy wing shared a stage, and not always gladly. But the Verlaine–Hell split is the one that organized the music.11

The deal wave

The scene recorded itself before the industry arrived, and the order of those records is its own argument. Smith cut a debut single at Electric Lady in June 1974, the session paid for by Robert Mapplethorpe, the pressing self-released on her own Mer imprint; “Piss Factory” is commonly counted the scene’s first record.12 When Television’s demos went nowhere, Ork followed her lead and invented Ork Records to press “Little Johnny Jewel” in the fall of 1975, seven minutes split across two sides of a 45.13 Smith’s Arista deal had led the way that April; then the rest came at once. Sire took the Ramones that winter — Seymour Stein’s wife Linda saw them first, and a Punk magazine story by Mary Harron helped close the argument. Blondie’s debut landed on Private Stock, a disco label so wrong for them that Chrysalis bought the contract out in 1977. Elektra took Television in August 1976 on Verlaine’s condition that he produce. And Talking Heads held out through an eleven-and-a-half-month courtship (Stein had offered a contract the day after hearing them) before signing with Sire on November 1, 1976.14 The Ramones’ debut made the economics into an aesthetic: staff producer Craig Leon promised the whole album for $6,400 and delivered twenty-nine minutes that initially sold about six thousand copies: the flop that founded a genre.15 By 1976 the emptying was on vinyl: when Atlantic pressed the double LP Live at CBGB’s, the house document of the scene, it contained none of the bands that made the room famous — the industry had already taken them.16

The records disagree on purpose

No two of the records the cohort made once signed agree on what the room was for, and the sessions were as contested as the songs. Horses (1975) was a war between Smith and producer John Cale — “I went to pick out an expensive watercolor painting and instead I got a mirror,” she said later; Cale called the dynamic “an immutable force meeting an immovable object” — and it ended with two of Smith’s lovers, Allen Lanier and Tom Verlaine, trading blows in the studio during the final sessions. The record reached No. 47 and put the Bowery in the national press.17 Marquee Moon (1977) was cut with engineer Andy Johns, who arrived hours late on the first day announcing “I can’t work here!”, then admitted he had already gotten a drum sound; the title track went down in one take that the drummer assumed was a rehearsal, and when Johns suggested another pass, Verlaine told him to forget it.18 Rocket to Russia (1977) perfected the Ramones’ compression; the Heartbreakers pressed the Dolls’ wreckage onto L.A.M.F. (1977); and Blank Generation (1977) gave the scene its self-description, with Hell insisting ever after that the blank was a space to fill.19 Suicide’s debut (1977) used no guitars at all, two men and electronics inventing synth punk, and the world’s tolerance for it was measured in projectiles: within a year Alan Vega’s nose was broken by a Brussels crowd that snatched his microphone, and a Glasgow audience threw a silver axe.20 Blondie’s Parallel Lines (1978) came out of producer Mike Chapman’s stopwatch discipline, which left Harry in tears and provoked the bassist into throwing a synthesizer at him; it repaid the abuse with “Heart of Glass”, No. 1 on the Hot 100 in April 1979, a disco record by a CBGB band.21 By decade’s end the art wing was recording where it lived: Fear of Music (1979) was cut by a mobile unit parked outside Frantz and Weymouth’s Long Island City loft, cables run through the window, Brian Eno producing.22 The Cramps closed the founding era with Songs the Lord Taught Us (1980), rockabilly played as horror film.23

The women who ran the scene

The scene’s memory tends to file its women under two names, Patti and Debbie, and the room was fuller than that. Tina Weymouth, who learned her instrument so a band could exist, became its most imitated rhythm player. Roberta Bayley ran the CBGB door from 1974 to 1978, taking the scene’s cover charge; mid-tenure she bought a camera and shot the photograph on the Ramones’ debut cover after the band rejected the label’s hired session, four leather jackets against a playground fence off 2nd Street.24 Mary Harron gave the Ramones their first press interview in Punk magazine, the story that helped convince Sire to sign them. Anya Phillips designed Debbie Harry’s stage clothes, managed James Chance, and co-founded the Mudd Club, the scene’s after-hours successor room, before dying at twenty-six. The Erasers, founded by guitarist Susan Springfield and drummer Jane Fire, debuted at the club in May 1977 and were opening for the Patti Smith Group by December, hand-picked by Smith. Genya Ravan produced the Dead Boys’ debut, a woman running the board for the scene’s roughest band. And the film the scene is remembered through came half from women’s hands: Bayley’s stills for Punk, GODLIS’s available-light Leica photographs, inside the room and on the sidewalk out front — street photography done at night without a flash.25

Two endings

The scene ended twice, from opposite directions. The refusal came from below: no wave, the downtown wing that heard even the Ramones as too much rock and roll. James Chance, Lydia Lunch, and a cluster of bands that could barely be called bands played an art-space festival in SoHo in spring 1978; Brian Eno was in the audience and convinced Island to fund No New York (1978), four bands, four songs each — the Contortions’ side cut “totally live in the studio, no separation between the instruments, no overdubs, just like a document,” in Chance’s words. No wave was its own scene and knew it: its rooms were galleries and lofts, and its target was the CBGB cohort’s professionalism.26 The promotion came from above: the industry renamed the scene’s playable wing “new wave” and signed it, and by 1977 the founding cohort was on world tours while the Dead Boys, imported from Cleveland at Joey Ramone’s urging and briefly managed by Kristal himself, held down the room’s second generation with Young, Loud and Snotty (1977).27 After that the club became a rite of passage for everyone else’s scenes. What stayed constant was the selection rule the whole cohort passed through: a door that asked only whether the songs were yours.28
