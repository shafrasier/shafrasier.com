Electronic

Influences

A locomotive pulls out of the Batignolles depot, recorded to disc, and the recording is cut and repeated until the train plays rhythm. Pierre Schaeffer’s “Étude aux chemins de fer,” not quite three minutes of railway, went out over French radio on October 5, 1948 — the first broadcast of musique concrète, music assembled from recorded sound.1 Kraftwerk answered the train in 1977: Trans-Europe Express took what a state radio laboratory had done to a locomotive and made it pop.2 Electronic is the laboratory. Its instrument is whatever the engineers just finished building, and the equipment kept walking out of the institutions that owned it, decade by decade, until the laboratory fit in a bedroom.

In 1949 a Bonn phoneticist named Werner Meyer-Eppler published a thesis on synthesizing sound from electrical signals alone, and its subtitle named a music that did not yet exist: elektronische Musik. The word carried a border inside it: the Cologne studio built on his ideas held that true electronic music was synthesized from pure signal, while Schaeffer’s Paris school worked from recordings — the family’s first schism, fought two decades before the family existed.3 The other name accreted from the opposite direction. A UK festival was using “electronica” by 1983, an English label attached it to home-listening techno in the early nineties, and in 1997 the American record industry adopted it, and to a documented extent manufactured it, as the umbrella for that year’s marketing push.4 The artists it advertised spat it out — “We soon disowned that silly ‘electronica’ tag,” said Liam Howlett of the Prodigy, whose album had just spent the summer proving the campaign superfluous.5 The family kept the plain old adjective, which asks less and covers more.

Scope and boundaries

The busiest border is settled by treaty. Dance holds to what was made for floors, whatever the tools — ambient, IDM, and the armchair avant-garde “were made for headphones and armchairs,” as that family’s history puts it — and this family is the other side of the refusal.6 House, techno, and the rave diaspora were built with this family’s instruments, and their stories are told in the rooms that commissioned them, from the Warehouse to the festival pyramid; the shared genres sit under both roots in the reference taxonomies, and they do here too. What the treaty leaves on this side is the listening wing: the album auteurs, the home-listening labels, and the instruments themselves, whoever ends up dancing to them.

The other borders are quicker. Ambient is its own family, because much of that tradition was never electronic at all — its lineage runs through acoustic drones, field recordings, and a gamelan performance at a Paris fair, and the listening lesson it taught this family is told from its own shelf.7 Synthpop holds dual citizenship with Pop: the standard rock histories tell it as a new wave episode, and the merger appears below from this family’s side.8 Krautrock keeps the house of its own that Psychedelia granted it, though the synthesizer wing that grew out of it (Tangerine Dream and the Berlin School) made this family’s music. Electro was hip hop’s invention, made with this family’s machines. Stevie Wonder’s synthesizer run stays inside his own five-year miracle, and industrial built a separate lineage alongside this one, borrowed from in both directions. And the newest children follow the treaty: grime and the UK bass continuum, psytrance and the trance tree, all ride the floor’s branches.9

Major branches

The laboratory century (1920–1967). The prehistory begins in defense research. Lev Termen, a Soviet physicist working on proximity sensors, noticed in October 1920 that his circuit sang when a hand approached it; within months he had given the first public concert on the instrument, and by the winter of 1927 he was touring America as Leon Theremin, Carnegie Hall included.10 The theremin proved that electricity could be an instrument, and it founded nothing; the founding waited for the radio networks. French radio gave Schaeffer a studio, and the Nordwestdeutscher Rundfunk answered on October 18, 1951, approving the Cologne studio that made elektronische Musik an institution — the room where Karlheinz Stockhausen, arriving in 1953 with Paris experience already behind him, realized “Gesang der Jünglinge,” a choirboy’s recorded voice woven into pure synthesized signal, the two rival dialects finally speaking in one piece.11 In America the laboratory was a living room. Louis and Bebe Barron scored MGM’s Forbidden Planet (1956) by overloading homebuilt circuits until they burned out, the first entirely electronic film score, and the Musicians’ Union forced MGM to credit the result as “electronic tonalities” — the Barrons were denied union membership and left off the Oscar ballot.12 The institution had refused, in writing, to call this music. The BBC built its own workshop in 1958, the Radiophonic Workshop, where Delia Derbyshire realized Ron Grainer’s Doctor Who theme in 1963, television’s first electronic signature tune, built from tape before the synthesizer existed to play it. Grainer, hearing it, asked whether he had really written it. “Most of it,” she said. He tried to credit her as co-composer; the BBC blocked it, Workshop staff being anonymous by policy, and her name reached the screen only in 2013.13

The synthesizer goes public (1964–1976). In October 1964, at an audio engineers’ convention in New York’s Barbizon-Plaza Hotel, Robert Moog unveiled a keyboard wired to four metal boxes of voltage-controlled circuits (he privately called the prototype the “Abominatron”) and left with orders he had never expected to take, for an instrument costing a tenth of the six-figure machines the universities ran.14 The escape from the institutes was now a product line, and in 1968 it found its public. Silver Apples wired a homemade rig of oscillators into a working New York rock band and released a debut that June; White Noise, a London studio group moonlighting Radiophonic staff, Derbyshire included, followed within the year.15 Then Wendy Carlos’s Switched-On Bach (October 1968) took the Moog to No. 10 on the Billboard 200, won three Grammys, and passed a million copies by 1974 — Bach as the Trojan horse, the album that carried the synthesizer into homes that would never have bought the avant-garde.16 The Minimoog (1970) finished the job the modular started: the first synthesizer sold in retail stores, from a team that expected to move maybe a hundred units, with Sun Ra among the first to record on one and Tangerine Dream and the Berlin School soon building album-length music from its cousins.17 By 1972 a Moog novelty called “Popcorn” could sell two million copies as one of the first pop hits played entirely on synthesizer, and the machine’s arrival in Black American pop was already underway — Stevie Wonder had sat down at TONTO over Memorial Day weekend 1971, and the five-year run that followed is his own chapter.18

Kraftwerk builds the language (1970–1982). The load-bearing act formed in Düsseldorf in 1970, when Ralf Hütter and Florian Schneider began recording inside the krautrock scene they would spend four years leaving. Hütter framed the project as a question: “What is the sound of the German Bundesrepublik?”19 Autobahn (November 1974) answered with a twenty-two-minute motorway hymn; edited to a three-minute single, it reached No. 25 in America and carried the album to No. 5.20 Trans-Europe Express (March 1977) barely charted in the US and mattered more than almost anything that did — David Bowie, then recording in Berlin, answered it with a track titled after Schneider.21 Computer World (May 1981) closed the run, and “The Model”, a three-year-old album track pressed as a B-side, was reissued that December as a double A-side and gave them a UK No. 1 in February 1982.22 They called what they had built “robot pop,” and Hütter’s own accounting was starker: having no language for the music they wanted, “we created our own language.”23 The inheritance scattered across the Atlantic within a year of Computer World. “Planet Rock” (1982) rebuilt two Kraftwerk records into electro’s founding single (hip hop’s exhibit), and the lawsuit that followed reportedly settled at a dollar per record sold.24 Detroit’s founders described their inheritance in the elevator image Dance already quotes, and that family keeps the founding it describes.25

The pop merger (1977–1989). In 1977 Giorgio Moroder and Donna Summer’s “I Feel Love” put a Moog pulse under disco (Dance’s exhibit, told on its floor), and the merger broke from three directions at once.26 In Tokyo, Yellow Magic Orchestra formed in 1978 around the Roland MC-8, a computer you sequenced instead of played, and Solid State Survivor (1979) made them the family’s first non-Western pillar; their “Computer Game” was in Afrika Bambaataa’s crates within four years.27 In London the conversion was personal: Gary Numan, a punk until he met a synthesizer, took Tubeway Army’s “Are ‘Friends’ Electric?” from No. 71 to No. 1 in June 1979 and held it four weeks; “Cars” topped the chart that September under his own name. Two synthesizer No. 1s in a single summer, from a convert.28 And in Sheffield, the Human League perfected the form’s pop case: “Don’t You Want Me” was the best-selling UK single of 1981 and that year’s Christmas No. 1, then a US No. 1 the following July — a release Philip Oakey had opposed and Virgin forced out anyway.29 Depeche Mode, formed in Basildon in 1980, turned the style into a four-decade institution, past a hundred million records and Q’s verdict as “the most popular electronic band the world has ever known.”30 The rock histories tell this whole episode as new wave; this family counts synthpop as its first pop conquest, and the argument is the border.8

Electronic listening music (1989–2000). Warp was a Sheffield record shop before it was a label, and the label it became in 1989 split the family on purpose. It rode the local “bleep” sound first; the split came with the compilation Artificial Intelligence (July 6, 1992), sleeve art of an android slumped in a listening chair, promising “electronic listening music.” Label co-founder Steve Beckett was blunt about the design: “you weren’t supposed to dance to it!”31 The roster hid behind aliases — Aphex Twin appeared as the Dice Man — and the wing the compilation seeded still answers, awkwardly, to the tag IDM.32 Aphex Twin’s own Selected Ambient Works 85–92 (November 1992), recorded to cassette beginning in his early teens, became the era’s defining document, the record that, in one retrospective’s verdict, carved the space where electronic music could be “domestic, private, suited to headphones as much as to clubs.”33 The 1997 “electronica” campaign the artists had already disowned briefly put the family at the top of the American charts: the Chemical Brothers’ Dig Your Own Hole (April) took a first UK No. 1 album with its lead single already atop the singles chart, and The Fat of the Land (June 30) debuted at No. 1 in both Britain and America, the fastest-selling dance album Britain had seen, with 317,000 first-week UK copies.34 The proof of absorption came from rock’s most prestigious quarter: Radiohead’s Kid A (2000), built under the open influence of Warp’s roster, became the band’s first American No. 1, and a decade later three major outlets named it the best album of its decade.35

The bedroom century (2000–present). Daft Punk ran the era’s establishment arc: Discovery (2001) made French house a pop language, the pyramid tour of 2006–07 lit the American boom Dance chronicles, and at the 56th Grammys in January 2014 the duo won all five awards they were nominated for, Album of the Year for Random Access Memories included — two robots on the Staples Center stage, collecting the industry’s highest honor.36 The UK’s contribution ran from the basement up: dubstep coalesced around a Croydon record shop in the early 2000s, broke nationally through a 2006 BBC radio session, and mutated in America into the maximalist strain critics dubbed brostep, whose figurehead Skrillex turned a self-released 2010 EP into two Grammys.37 The EDM festival economy that followed is the floor’s story, per the treaty; what this family kept was the headphone side, and its emblem stayed anonymous. Burial’s Untrue (November 2007), made in a consumer audio editor by a then-anonymous producer (“only five people know I make tunes”), took a Mercury Prize nomination that jumped its sales tenfold in a week.38 The last branch grew inside the software. The PC Music circle, from 2013, pushed pop processing to the point of abstraction. SOPHIE proved the style could carry grief and glamour at once before her death in 2021, at thirty-four, and 100 gecs turned it feral. The scene’s name, hyperpop, was lifted from a streaming service’s metadata — a genre christened by a database.39 The clock can stop on “Ocean Eyes” (November 2015): a thirteen-year-old Billie Eilish singing her brother’s song in his bedroom, produced on stock sounds in Logic, viral by morning.40

The unreal instruments

A union wrote “tonalities” where “music” should have gone, and every generation after got its own version of the memo: the broadcaster that kept its composer anonymous by policy, the industry that hung a tag on artists who refused to wear it. The charge was always that the machine was doing the work, and the rulings above read as one continuous verdict, renewed for each new instrument.41 What distinguishes this family is the response. It stopped contesting the premise and built an aesthetic out of it: Kraftwerk named their own music robot pop, Warp’s roster dissolved into aliases, Daft Punk accepted Album of the Year in helmets, and Burial stayed nameless until success forced the question. Authenticity and its discontents maps the two poles of pop authenticity, the Romantic performer baring a soul and the Modernist constructor proving mastery through artifice; this family is the Modernist pole given a century of institutional pressure, authorship demonstrated by construction while the author steps out of the frame.42 Punk runs a tribunal over who is real; this family spent a century being told its music wasn’t. It made the disappearance the show.

Key debates

When does the family start? The metadata above disagrees with itself on purpose: Named 1949, Period c. 1968. The field’s reference survey argues for one continuous story, Varèse to the present, technology and composition inseparable from the first tape splice — and on the evidence of technique it is right, since nothing the laboratories invented was ever discarded.43 The popular convention starts the family where the public could buy in: the 1968 season when Switched-On Bach charted and the first synthesizer bands formed, with everything earlier held as ancestry.44 This history follows the convention and keeps the laboratories as prehistory — told above at length, because no other family’s ancestry does so much explanatory work. The name still ran nineteen years ahead of the family, and the gap is the debate in two numbers.

The written-out founders. The corrective literature is now half this family’s shelf. Techno’s standard telling ran from the European rave outward for years before Dan Sicko’s 1999 reclamation and, two decades on, the book-length indictment that retells the genre from inside Black Detroit’s labor history, an argument whose own reviewers concede has yet to be answered.45 The women who staffed the founding laboratories fared no better: the Workshop’s anonymity kept Derbyshire’s name off the screen for fifty years, and the field’s own publisher was still conceding “vastly under-reported contributions of women composers” when the standard survey began absorbing the corrective archive in 2015.46 Both cases turn on the same irony: the family’s founding rooms actually held these people — the Workshop staffed by women, the public era opened by a trans composer, techno built by Black teenagers in a Detroit suburb — and the histories still wrote them out.

  • Toop — Ocean of Sound — Twentieth-century listening drifted toward the ambient — from Debussy hearing gamelan at the 1889 Paris Exposition through Satie, dub, Eno, and Aphex Twin — and ambient means immersion, not background: music that taps the environment's chaotic undertow, since no music truly stays in the background at all.
  • Reynolds — Energy Flash — Rave was a musical civilization with its own lineage — Chicago house and Detroit techno detonating the UK's hardcore continuum, from rave through jungle and garage to grime — and the music and its drug co-evolved: ecstasy shaped the sound, and the sound kept chasing the drug.
