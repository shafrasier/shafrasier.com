Genre

Punk rock

Periodc. 1974–present
LocationNew York, London, and Brisbane
Influences
Rock & rollGarage rockGirl groupsSurf music
LinksRYM

Punk rock is the founding genre of the Punk family — the narrow thing the bare word grew out of. The sound is a set of subtractions: tempos pushed past comfort, solos cut, songs trimmed toward two minutes, harmony reduced to downstroked barre chords and power chords, the vocal a shout, production left raw enough to hear the room. The subtractions were the point. Mid-seventies rock had grown symphonic, sentimental, and expensive, and punk rock answered with less of everything except speed and intent — first in New York, then in London, and, before any London band reached vinyl, in Brisbane.1 The family’s history (the rooms and the tribunal) is Punk’s to tell; this note stays with the sound and the records that carry it.

Origins

The parts were already on the shelf, documented where they live. The boogie of “Johnny B. Goode” flattened into downstroked eighth notes; girl-group chord changes taken at speed; the fuzzed two-chord riff of “You Really Got Me”; rock & roll’s two-and-a-half-minute discipline, which the intervening decade had abandoned.2 What the mid-seventies added was the decision to keep the reductions and discard everything else. The Ramones made that decision audible across fourteen tracks in 1976, and the sound arrived so complete that the genre has been described from that record ever since.3 London’s cohort took the template at higher stakes and higher mass within the year — a detonation whose civic history belongs to the family note — and Brisbane proved the sound was in the air beyond both capitals: the Saints pressed “(I’m) Stranded” on their own Fatal imprint in September 1976, ahead of the vinyl debuts of the Sex Pistols, Buzzcocks, the Damned, and the Clash, with Radio Birdman running a parallel operation in Sydney.4

Musical character

The core is rhythm guitar as percussion. Downstrokes on every eighth note, barre chords or two-note power chords, tempo held at the edge of the playable, and a vocal that treats pitch as optional and diction as a weapon; songs open on the riff, run a compressed verse-chorus, and stop dead.5 Around that core the founding records disagree with each other more than the stereotype allows. The Ramones took the anatomy as speed and subtraction, the Sex Pistols as mass and menace at a stomp, and the Clash as rock & roll classicism with the varnish burned off. Wire compressed it into fragments; Buzzcocks aimed it at the pop single.6 The lyric register runs from slogan to sneer to lovesick, and the stereotype of the mohawked shouter flattens a genre whose founding voices included a poet and several broken hearts.

Foundational records

  • Ramones, Ramones (1976) — the codification: every element of the anatomy present at once, and the record later generations learned the genre from3
  • The Saints, “(I’m) Stranded” (single, 1976) — self-released in Brisbane in September, before the London cohort reached vinyl; the album followed in 19774
  • The Damned, “New Rose” (1976) — by most counts the first British punk single, cut for Stiff with Nick Lowe producing7
  • Sex Pistols, “Anarchy in the U.K.” (1976) — the manifesto single: the London sound’s mass and menace on one side of a 458
  • The Clash, The Clash (1977) — the classicist wing’s founding LP, UK No. 129
  • Sex Pistols, Never Mind the Bollocks, Here’s the Sex Pistols (1977) — the British detonation pressed onto a single LP, straight in at No. 110
  • Richard Hell & the Voidoids, Blank Generation (1977) — the Bowery’s founding generation cutting its own LP after London had already borrowed the look
  • Wire, Pink Flag (1977) — twenty-one songs in thirty-five minutes: the anatomy run through art-school reduction, and the record post-punk and hardcore both learned from11
  • Buzzcocks, Singles Going Steady (1979) — the pop wing’s proof: the same anatomy carrying love songs, collected from three years of 45s

The waves after

The genre outlived its founding moment on the street. In Britain the second wave came up from the terraces: Garry Bushell named the Oi! movement in 1980, taking the word from the shout Cockney Rejects’ singer used to introduce songs, and the UK 82 generation — the Exploited, Discharge, GBH — drove the sound harder and faster until it shaded into hardcore, with Discharge’s drum pattern becoming a genre convention in its own right.12 California ran the opposite experiment: Descendents’ Milo Goes to College (1982) put melody and heartbreak over hardcore tempos, and Bad Religion’s Suffer (1988, on Epitaph) is credited with reviving the Southern California melodic scene that seeded the nineties breakthrough.13 The tag never closed. Wipers stretched it to a ten-minute title track on Youth of America (1981), and the bedroom-fi egg punk wave of the 2010s still files under it: a genre in its sixth decade of new releases.14

Hardcore punk accelerated it and carries the largest of the descendant histories. Pop punk melodized it, Oi! and street punk kept the terraces, and anarcho-punk turned it into lived politics; the splice children (garage punk, glam punk, psychobilly, ska punk, Celtic punk) crossed it into everything adjacent.15
