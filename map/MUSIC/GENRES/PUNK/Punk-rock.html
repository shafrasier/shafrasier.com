Genre

Punk rock

Periodc. 1974–present
LocationNew York, London, and Brisbane, then everywhere
Influences
Rock & rollGarage rockGirl groupsSurf music
LinksRYM

The most imitated guitar style of the late twentieth century began as a counting aid. Johnny Ramone played nearly everything in downstrokes, all six strings of a barre chord attacked on every eighth note, and when Kirk Hammett asked how he came up with the technique that founded two genres’ rhythm language, he answered: “I was just counting. That’s the only way I could count, through downstrokes.”1 That is punk rock in one anecdote: a sound built from subtractions so severe they read as ideology, arrived at for reasons as practical as keeping time. The subtractions are the genre: tempos pushed past comfort, solos cut, songs trimmed toward two minutes, harmony reduced to barre chords and power chords, the vocal a shout, production raw enough to hear the room. Mid-seventies rock had grown symphonic, sentimental, and expensive, and punk rock answered with less of everything except speed and intent — first in New York, then in London, and, before any London band reached vinyl, in Brisbane.2 The family’s history, the rooms and the tribunal, is Punk’s to tell; this note stays with the sound: what it is made of, who made it, and what it kept becoming.

Origins

The parts were already on the shelf. The boogie of “Johnny B. Goode” flattened into downstroked eighth notes; girl-group chord changes taken at speed; the fuzzed two-chord riff of “You Really Got Me”; rock & roll’s two-and-a-half-minute discipline, which the intervening decade had abandoned; and the shelf of orphans Proto-punk collects, the records the founders kept citing.3 What the mid-seventies added was the decision to keep the reductions and discard everything else. The Ramones made that decision audible across fourteen tracks in 1976, and the sound arrived so complete that the genre has been described from that record ever since.4 London took the template at higher stakes within the year, and Brisbane proved the sound was already in the air beyond both capitals: the Saints pressed “(I’m) Stranded” on their own Fatal imprint in September 1976, ahead of the vinyl debuts of the Sex Pistols, Buzzcocks, the Damned, and the Clash, with Radio Birdman running a parallel operation in Sydney.5

The anatomy

The core is rhythm guitar as percussion: downstrokes on every eighth note, tempos held at the edge of the playable (the founding records push toward 180 beats a minute, and live the Ramones ran faster), a vocal that treats pitch as optional and diction as a weapon, songs that open on the riff, run a compressed verse-chorus, and stop dead.6 The no-solos rule got its definitive statement as a joke: on “Boredom,” from the Buzzcocks’ Spiral Scratch EP, Pete Shelley plays a solo of two notes repeated sixty-six times — the slot kept, the content voided, virtuosity mocked from inside its own showcase.7 The rule was permission, not prohibition; the genre always housed players like Television’s guitarists and, later, Bad Brains, whose precision outran most of what punk was rebelling against.

Production split into three founding modes. The Ramones’ debut was cut at Plaza Sound, a thirties-era studio on the eighth floor of Radio City Music Hall — seven days and $6,400, eight floors above the gilt. Producer Craig Leon, working from the idea of “the negative Beatles,” nailed the takes, then overdubbed and mixed until the studio construction sounded like a live set.8 Never Mind the Bollocks ran the opposite way: Chris Thomas, a producer with Beatles pedigree, layered Steve Jones’s guitars past counting (“There are loads of tracks on that — I don’t even remember how many,” Jones said of “Anarchy,” adding that Thomas “kept telling me to tune up and it drove me mad, but looking back I’m glad he did”). The record that codified “anyone can do this” for Britain was an expensive, meticulous studio object, and Jones named the difference himself: “We didn’t just go in and crash, bang, wallop.”9 And Spiral Scratch printed the third mode on its sleeve: a three-hour session in Manchester on December 28, 1976, produced by Martin Hannett, released on the band’s own label with per-track credits on the back cover — “third take, no dubs” for the opener, take counts and overdubs listed for the rest. What the sleeve proved for the sound: the anatomy survived being cheap.10

Foundational records

  • Ramones, Ramones (1976) — the codification: every element of the anatomy present at once, and the record later generations learned the genre from4
  • The Saints, “(I’m) Stranded” (single, 1976) — self-released in Brisbane in September, before the London cohort reached vinyl; the album followed in 19775
  • The Damned, “New Rose” (1976) — by most counts the first British punk single, cut for Stiff with Nick Lowe producing11
  • Buzzcocks, Spiral Scratch (EP, 1977) — the two-note solo and the published process; the sound of the anatomy self-pressed10
  • The Clash, The Clash (1977) — the classicist wing’s founding LP, UK No. 1212
  • Sex Pistols, Never Mind the Bollocks, Here’s the Sex Pistols (1977) — the British detonation, straight in at No. 1, layered like the pop record it swore it wasn’t9
  • Richard Hell & the Voidoids, Blank Generation (1977) — the Bowery’s founding generation cutting its own LP after London had borrowed the look
  • Wire, Pink Flag (1977) — twenty-one songs in thirty-five minutes: punk rock run through art-school reduction, the blueprint post-punk and hardcore both raided13
  • X-Ray Spex, Germfree Adolescents (1978) — a saxophone in the guitar’s chair, singing about consumerism from inside its packaging14
  • Buzzcocks, Singles Going Steady (1979) — the pop wing’s proof: the same three chords carrying love songs, collected from three years of 45s

The women who founded it too

Patti Smith’s Horses arrived five months before the Ramones’ debut, and the founding generation’s women keep getting remembered as exceptions when they were architects.15 Poly Styrene formed X-Ray Spex after seeing the Sex Pistols, put dayglo and consumer culture where London’s leather and nihilism had been, and broke the guitar monopoly by giving the hook slot to a saxophone played by Lora Logic, fifteen when she answered Styrene’s Melody Maker ad in 1976. “Oh Bondage Up Yours!” (1977) opens with a girl announcing, sweetly, that the seen-and-not-heard rule is over, and then screaming the title; critics have treated it as prototypical British punk ever since.16 The Slits handed the mixing desk to the reggae producer Dennis Bovell for Cut (1979), every track written by the four women, and made the era’s most rhythmically radical punk record — bass carrying the songs, guitars scratched into the offbeats, dub space where the wall of downstrokes should be. The reference taxonomies promptly re-filed it as post-punk; the genre’s women kept getting re-shelved out of the genre they helped build.17 In San Francisco, the Avengers, fronted by nineteen-year-old Penelope Houston, opened the Sex Pistols’ last show at Winterland on January 14, 1978: as London’s version died onstage, the next American wave was the undercard.18 And in Los Angeles, Alicia Armendariz, known to the scene as Alice Bag, co-founded the Bags with Patricia Morrison, played the Masque’s first month, cut “Survive” for Dangerhouse in 1978, and stood in Penelope Spheeris’s The Decline of Western Civilization as proof that first-generation LA punk was more Latina and more female than the mohawk stereotype remembers.19

The American spread

The sound moved faster than its rooms could stay open. LA’s answer to CBGB was the Masque, a ten-thousand-square-foot basement under a Hollywood porn theater that Brendan Mullen rented for $850 a month in 1977; the Germs, X, the Weirdos, and the Screamers played it, and the fire marshals closed it inside five months.20 The scene’s label of record, Dangerhouse, pressed the singles that survive it. The city’s two poles mapped the genre’s range: Germs’ (GI) (1979) is the pivot into hardcore punk; X’s Los Angeles (1980), produced by the Doors’ Ray Manzarek for $10,000, ran fifties changes and poetry-workshop lyrics through the anatomy — the classicist wing, west-coast edition.21 The Pacific Northwest contributed the genre’s great refusal: Wipers founder Greg Sage conceived the band as a studio project with a plan of fifteen LPs in ten years and no touring, no photographs, no interviews (“I got labeled ‘the black sheep of the industry,’” he said later, without regret). On Youth of America (1981) he answered the two-minute rule from inside the genre with a ten-plus-minute title track.22

The waves after

In Britain the second wave came up from the terraces. Garry Bushell named the Oi! movement in 1980, taking the word from the shout Cockney Rejects’ singer used to introduce songs, and the UK 82 generation drove the sound harder and faster until it shaded into hardcore.23 The wave’s records argued with the genre’s obituary writers directly: the Exploited’s Punks Not Dead (1981) answered Crass’s own “punk is dead” verdict from two years earlier and reached No. 20 on the UK national chart — the “dead” genre outselling its obituary.24 Discharge compressed the sound into war-reportage bursts on Hear Nothing See Nothing Say Nothing (1982), driven by a galloping drum pattern so distinctive it named a genre: D-beat, the D for Discharge, one drummer’s beat become a worldwide style.25 GBH’s City Baby Attacked by Rats (1982) hit No. 2 on the indie chart and fed directly into Metal’s thrash lineage; Metallica and Slayer both cite it.26

California ran the opposite experiment: melody at speed. Descendents’ Milo Goes to College (1982) put heartbreak over hardcore tempos, and Bad Religion’s Suffer (1988) revived the southern-California melodic line on guitarist Brett Gurewitz’s own Epitaph label.27 That label became the sound’s second economy: in 1994, while the majors harvested the year’s breakthrough, the Offspring’s Smash reached No. 4 and NOFX’s Punk in Drublic went gold, both on Epitaph, with no video and almost no airplay — platinum-scale punk rock with no major label anywhere in the chain.28 Pop punk grew from this melodic lineage into its own genre.

The tag never closed. The newest wave, egg punk — bedroom-fi, Devo-warped, satirical, run out of the Midwestern DIY circuit by bands like the Coneheads and Lumpy and the Dumpers — got its name from a 2017 Instagram meme contrasting “egg punk” with “chain punk,” and by 2024 the rock press was covering the style’s second wave.29 Critics writing in Creem named punk rock backward in 1971; a meme account named its latest strain in 2017. The forum moved from the rock press to the feed, and the job, arguing over what counts, never changed.

Elsewhere

The export produced national punk scenes with their own records; three carry the story. In Japan, the Stalin’s Stop Jap (1982) put Michirō Endō’s provocations onto the national canon lists (Rolling Stone Japan later ranked it among the hundred greatest Japanese rock albums); Endō chose the band name precisely because it was hated, and the stage act ran to firecrackers and butchered hog parts.30 In Brazil, the São Paulo compilation Grito Suburbano (1982) squeezed three bands into twelve rented studio hours and became the country’s first punk record — the Ramones’ seven-day economics compressed by an order of magnitude.31 In Germany, Die Toten Hosen rose from Düsseldorf’s punk cellar to a string of national No. 1 albums — a scale the genre never reached at home.32 The deepest claim comes from Lima, where Proto-punk’s Los Saicos screamed demolition songs in 1965.33

The rules, and who enforces them

The genre’s discipline was always a choice dressed as a limitation, and the proof is that every founding record breaks at least one rule: Bollocks’s uncountable overdubs, Wire’s art-school fragments, Buzzcocks aiming the anatomy at love songs, the Slits giving the desk to dub, Sage’s ten-minute title track. Even the founding constraint was functional, not doctrinal — the downstroke was a way of counting. What made the rules feel like law was the enforcement culture around them, punk’s habit of auditing its own records in public: Mark Perry declaring the genre dead when the Clash signed to CBS, Crass repeating the verdict on vinyl, the Exploited selling the rebuttal into the national top twenty. The tribunal sat in Punk’s rooms, but the argument was conducted in records, and the records kept coming. A genre in its sixth decade of new releases has survived every one of its own funerals.34
