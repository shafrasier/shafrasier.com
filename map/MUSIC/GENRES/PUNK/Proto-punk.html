Proto-punk

Periodc. 1958–1975 (the label itself arrived after 1976)
LocationUnited States (Detroit, New York, Cleveland, the Pacific Northwest), with far-flung outposts
Influences
Rock & rollRhythm & bluesSurf music

Proto-punk is the genre nobody ever played. Its founding record was hated by its own label: when Link Wray handed Cadence an instrumental he had improvised at a Virginia dance — the crowd had demanded it four times — label boss Archie Bleyer wanted nothing to do with it, least of all after Wray poked a pencil through his amplifier’s speaker cone to make the studio take snarl like the live one. Bleyer pressed “Rumble” anyway because his stepdaughter loved it, radio stations in New York and Boston banned it because the title was gang-fight slang, and it reached No. 16 in the summer of 1958 as the only instrumental ever barred from American airwaves: a record with no words, banned for what the guitar implied.1 Nothing connects that 45 to the other records here except what happened afterward. The canon runs from Wray’s ban to an album that sat unreleased for four years while the music it anticipated arrived without it, and nobody who made these records knew they were making the same thing. What they share is that the punks listened, and said so, until a scattered pile of orphans became a lineage.2

The two baptisms

The shelf was named twice, both times by critics, neither time by the bands. The first baptism happened before punk existed. The word was crawling through Creem’s Stooges coverage by late 1970, and in 1971 Lester Bangs built the aesthetic a manifesto in “Psychotic Reactions and Carburetor Dung,” an essay whose grandest gesture is fiction: the Count Five had split after one album, so Bangs simply reviewed the later records he wished the amateurs had made, inventing a discography of glorious non-existent trash.3 The next year the imagined canon became a purchasable object. Lenny Kaye, then a clerk at the Village Oldies record shop, compiled Nuggets for Elektra: a planned series of roughly eight regional LPs argued down to one double album, twenty-seven mid-sixties garage singles, released October 2, 1972, with liner notes observing that a name “has been unofficially coined” for these young, decidedly unprofessional bands — “punk-rock.”4 It hardly sold. Then Sire reissued it in 1976, straight into the middle of the explosion it had predicted, and the compilation read as prophecy; by then Kaye was onstage in Patti Smith’s group. The second baptism came after punk existed, when the older usage needed a prefix to stop colliding with the living genre: “proto-punk,” in Bangs’s hands as an essay title in The Rolling Stone Illustrated History of Rock & Roll by 1981, applied backward to whatever the punks had cited.5 The first book to walk the whole shelf, Clinton Heylin’s From the Velvets to the Voidoids (1993), completed the canonization and made its sharpest structural claim: the pre-history had three American poles, New York, Detroit, and, the surprise, Cleveland.6

The reach-back

Behind Wray sit the sixties’ rawest regional records, each an accident of appetite. Them’s “Gloria” began as a Van Morrison song stretched to twenty minutes of ad-libbed verses at Belfast’s Maritime Hotel, was cut at a 1964 London session where producer Dick Rowe distrusted the band enough to hire session men, and went out as a B-side. In America it split in two: Them’s original stalled at No. 71 because stations balked at one bedroom lyric, while Chicago’s Shadows of Knight swapped the line for a tamer one and rode the same three chords to No. 10, the garage standard laundered for radio by the garage itself.7 In Tacoma, the Sonics cut Here Are the Sonics!!! (1965) for Etiquette, the label run by members of the town’s reigning band, with two-track tape and a single microphone on the whole drum kit; Gerry Roslie screamed originals about poison, witches, and psychosis, and the method — bleed, distortion, no isolation — was punk production a decade early, arrived at through poverty and impatience.8 And in West Germany, five American GIs stationed at Gelnhausen shaved real tonsures, dressed in black habits, renamed themselves Monks, and made Black Monk Time (1966): drums without cymbals, distorted bass, a six-string electric banjo run as a rhythm weapon, and complaints about Vietnam blunt enough that Polydor refused an American release. The tag’s strangest citizens were men in uniform singing about the war to German beat fans; the record then vanished for nearly thirty years before reissues found it an audience.9

New York before the name

The shelf’s East Coast spine is the Velvet Underground at maximum abrasion. White Light/White Heat (1968) was recorded in two weeks at Mayfair Sound with the needles deliberately in the red (“a very rabid record,” John Cale called it, “consciously anti-beauty”), and its seventeen-minute closer, “Sister Ray,” went down in one take by prior agreement, after the engineer walked out and said they could come get him when it was over. It reached No. 199, and Rolling Stone declined to review it; the punk generation treated it as scripture.10 The New York Dolls carried the city’s next chapter in lipstick: a seventeen-week Tuesday residency from June 1972 in the Oscar Wilde Room of the Mercer Arts Center drew Warhol and the British press, and Mercury A&R man Paul Nelson spent months dragging executives to shows before winning them a deal. Creem’s 1973 readers’ poll voted them best and worst new group of the year at once, the whole proto-punk reception problem on a single ballot.11 Their debut (1973) arrived a week before the Broadway Central Hotel collapsed on August 3, 1973, killing four and taking the Mercer Arts Center with it (the same rotten building whose fall pushed The Loft off Broadway: one structural failure ending a founding room of dance music and the proto-punk showcase in an afternoon).12 For the follow-up, Too Much Too Soon (1974), the girl-group-obsessed Dolls hired Shadow Morton, the Shangri-Las’ producer, the tag reaching back into Girl group territory on purpose.13 The word itself was already loose in the city: from their first art-space shows in 1970, Suicide printed postcards reading “PUNK MUSIC BY SUICIDE” — the first known band to self-apply the term, borrowed from Bangs’s Stooges reviews, five years before the movement.14

Detroit

Detroit is the shelf’s engine room, and its two founding bands were signed on the same weekend: Elektra’s Danny Fields flew out in late September 1968, heard the MC5 at the Grande Ballroom and their little-brother band the Stooges in Ann Arbor, and signed both on September 26 — $20,000 for the Five, $5,000 for the Stooges.15 Kick Out the Jams, cut live at the Grande over two Halloween-week nights in 1968, reached No. 30 and then detonated its own career. Hudson’s department store refused to stock it over the title track’s obscenity. The band answered with a full-page ad in the Ann Arbor Argus reading “F— Hudson’s” — with Elektra’s logo on it, billing the label. Hudson’s pulled every Elektra record in response, and Jac Holzman dropped the band. The two Atlantic albums that followed, Jon Landau’s disciplined Back in the USA (1970) and High Time (1971), sold poorly and mapped the argument punk would keep having with itself: tightness or abandon.16 The Stooges’ three albums are three production philosophies. John Cale recorded the debut (1969) nearly live, though Elektra rejected his mix. For Fun House (1970), producer Don Gallucci had the band set up exactly as onstage in Elektra’s Los Angeles studio, no baffles, Iggy Pop moving with a handheld microphone among the amplifiers. And Raw Power (1973) was salvaged in post-production by David Bowie in a single allotted day, a mix fans complained about for decades. These are the records the punk generation named most, and the reason the tag exists at all.17

Detroit also holds the shelf’s great recovered chapter. Death — three Black brothers, Bobby, David, and Dannis Hackney — pivoted from funk to hard rock in 1971 and cut seven songs at United Sound in February 1975; by the family’s account, Clive Davis funded the sessions and demanded a friendlier name, David refused, and the money vanished. They pressed five hundred copies of “Politicians in My Eyes” on their own Tryangle label in 1976 and were forgotten. Before David died he handed his brother the master tapes, insisting that “the world will come looking for these.” In 2009, by way of an attic, a collectors’ grapevine, and a New York Times story, the world did: Drag City released …For the Whole World to See, and Death entered the canon thirty-four years late.18

Cleveland and the outposts

Cleveland is where the shelf became self-aware: bands who had read the critics, building deliberately on Velvets and Stooges foundations before New York’s scene had a name. Rocket From the Tombs lasted barely a year from their June 1974 debut at the Viking Saloon, never released a record, wrote “Sonic Reducer,” “Final Solution,” and “30 Seconds Over Tokyo,” and split over the art-versus-aggression argument; the split’s two heirs divided the inheritance exactly. David Thomas and Peter Laughner formed Pere Ubu and self-released “30 Seconds Over Tokyo” on their own Hearthan label in December 1975 — an American DIY single a full year before Spiral Scratch, Britain’s celebrated first — while Cheetah Chrome and Johnny Blitz took the hard songs to New York, became Dead Boys, and gave the CBGB scene its most violent act.19 Beside them ran the electric eels, who played five public shows in three years, brought a running lawnmower onstage, called the stance “art terrorism,” and were heard only posthumously when “Agitated” surfaced on Rough Trade in 1978.20

The tag’s edges prove the invention kept happening wherever amplifiers met impatience. Debris’, a trio from Chickasha, Oklahoma, released the Velvets-damaged Static Disposal in January 1976, before the first Ramones LP reached them. Hamilton, Ontario’s Simply Saucer cut their 1974 demos with a pair of local brothers named Daniel and Bob Lanois — the future ambient producer’s origin story runs through a Canadian proto-punk basement — and the results stayed unreleased until 1989.21 London’s contribution was an underground: Mick Farren’s Deviants pressed Ptooff! in 1967 and sold it through the underground press, prefiguring the fanzine economy punk would build.22 And in Lima, Los Saicos cut “Demolición” in 1965 (fast, screamed, about tearing a railroad station down), and today a plaque at a Lince street corner declares the district punk’s birthplace. No one at CBGB had heard it; the claim’s force is exactly its isolation. That is the tag’s lesson: proto-punk names a recurring possibility of rock and roll, not a single bloodline.23

The boundary

The edge is commercial: everything before punk rock’s 1976 breakthrough qualifies, which is how Horses — the Bowery’s founding LP — files as proto-punk alongside records cut a decade before anyone had heard of the Bowery scene.24 The tag does not swallow Rock’s garage rock: it overlaps the garage 45s without absorbing them, taking only the ones the punks kept citing. The same records serve more than one ancestry: “You Really Got Me” is claimed as proto-punk and proto-metal at once.25 The defining case remains The Modern Lovers: two 1972 demo sessions for rival labels, six tracks produced by John Cale, the band dissolved in 1974 with the album unmade, and The Modern Lovers finally assembled and released by Beserkley in August 1976, when punk was no longer a prophecy. Its engine, “Roadrunner,” is “Sister Ray” reduced from three chords to two (Jonathan Richman had watched the Velvets obsessively as a Boston teenager), and in August 1977, punk’s imperial year, the 1972 Cale demo of it reached No. 11 on the UK chart as a B-side: a five-year-old rehearsal charting inside the future it predicted.26

The canon

  • Link Wray, “Rumble” (1958) — the banned instrumental the tag reaches back to first1
  • Them, “Gloria” (1964) — the garage standard thousands of bands learned first; Horses opens by rewiring it7
  • The Sonics, Here Are the Sonics!!! (1965) — one microphone on the drums, the screamed originals8
  • Monks, Black Monk Time (1966) — the GIs’ cymbal-less war complaints, refused a US release9
  • The Velvet Underground, White Light/White Heat (1968) — abrasion on purpose; the shelf’s scripture10
  • MC5, Kick Out the Jams (1969) — two Halloween nights at the Grande, and the ad that got them dropped16
  • The Stooges, Fun House (1970) — the stage rebuilt inside the studio; the reference chart’s No. 1 proto-punk album17
  • New York Dolls, New York Dolls (1973) — trash glamour; the bridge the scene walked across11
  • Death, …For the Whole World to See (rec. 1975, rel. 2009) — the recovered Detroit chapter18
  • The Modern Lovers, The Modern Lovers (rec. 1971–72, rel. 1976) — the tag’s defining case26
