Punk

In December 1973, Hilly Kristal opened a club called CBGB at 315 Bowery, the awning promising country, bluegrass, and blues; almost none of it ever played there.1 The room had one booking rule, original music only, and a dead Sunday night that a manager named Terry Ork talked Kristal into handing his unrecorded band, Television, in March 1974 — the residency conventionally credited with starting the club’s punk era, secured on the theory that the band’s friends would drink.2 The Ramones debuted there that August and played the room more than seventy times before the year ended.3 Punk is the permission: the family founded on the claim that anyone in the room can do this, tonight. The other half of the claim arrived with it and never left — a family that gives the stage away for free polices, ferociously, who deserves to keep it.

The name was in print before the music existed. Ed Sanders described his own record as “punk rock — redneck sentimentality” in a March 1970 Chicago Tribune piece. The canonical citation came a year later, in Dave Marsh’s May 1971 Creem column on ? and the Mysterians, with Greg Shaw pressing his own claim in the same pages: a word coined by critics for the garage amateurs of 1965–66, bands already gone from the charts.4 Lenny Kaye’s Nuggets (1972) pressed that prehistory into a canon, its compiler soon to play guitar for Patti Smith, and Punk magazine’s first issue (January 1976, a Lou Reed cartoon on the cover) finally pinned the word to a living scene: “the print version of the Ramones,” its founding editor called it.5 As the family grew, the two-word form narrowed. Punk rock now names the founding genre — the fast, shouted, downstroked mid-seventies sound and the street lineages that kept it — while the bare word covers everything the permission went on to produce.6

Scope and boundaries

Punk refounded Rock from below; the coup’s exhibits (the seven-day debut album, the teatime obscenities, the chord diagram, the “new wave” rebrand that made the CBGB roster safe for radio) already hang in the parent’s fifth act.7 What the refounders built for themselves is this family’s story: the rooms, the fanzines, the labels, and the tribunal that audits all three. The ancestry is a braid the family wears openly — the boogie of “Johnny B. Goode” flattened into downstrokes, girl-group changes taken at double speed, “Leader of the Pack“‘s leather menace, the “Be My Baby” beat with the orchestra thrown out, the two chords of “You Really Got Me” — five documented bloodlines converging on one Queens band, claimed here as a synthesis.8 The pre-1974 inheritance as a whole now answers to proto-punk, a label applied strictly in the rearview, and it is told below as ancestry.9

The borders follow the history. New wave was the industry’s own invention — the accessible wing renamed so American radio would play it — and the parent tells that wing’s story. Garage rock waits in rock’s future as well; punk canonized the 45s as prehistory and moved nothing else. Grunge came up through alternative rock’s circuit. Emo rap grew inside hip hop, whatever the name suggests, and the other name-borrows are just as shallow: folk punk owes Folk a word and nothing more, and cowpunk and dance-punk borrowed from Country and Dance the same way.10 Post-punk is a different case. The experimental exit of 1978 took the permission with it, and the gothic, cold, and dance-rhythm lineages that grew from that exit are this family’s descendants.11 The metal border produced twins raised in different houses: metalcore and grindcore grew up in metal’s press and festival world, crossover thrash and stenchcore in hardcore’s and crust’s.12 And the downtown traffic ran in both directions: the Beastie Boys cut a New York hardcore EP in 1982 before hip hop claimed them for good.13

Major branches

The word before the music (1965–1973). The founding generation never knew it was founding anything: the garage bands of 1965–66 (two chords, a cheap organ, one regional hit) got the name retroactively, from critics, after the music had already come and gone.14 The Velvet Underground supplied the permission’s arithmetic, which Brian Eno delivered as a quip in 1982: the first record hardly sold, but “everyone who bought one of those 30,000 copies started a band.”15 Detroit turned the inheritance violent. MC5 recorded Kick Out the Jams live at the Grande Ballroom over two October nights in 1968, and its opening obscenity got the album refused by a department-store chain and the band dropped by Elektra.16 The Stooges’ debut (1969) and Fun House (1970) built the template every history cites first: Ron Asheton’s two-riff brutalism under Iggy Pop’s provocations.17 The New York Dolls added thrift-store glamour and proud amateurism on their 1973 debut, and exported more than records: Malcolm McLaren managed their 1975 collapse, then carried what he had seen back to London.18 Britain’s own runway — the glam theater and pub-rock circuit London’s future punks grew up watching — is England’s Dreaming’s territory.19

The Bowery laboratory (1973–1976). The scene gathered around the Sunday residency at speed, and its first invention was visual: Richard Hell, Television’s founding bassist, cropped his hair ragged and held his torn shirts together with safety pins, and McLaren said later that the style he sold in London began with his first impressions of Hell — New York cut the clothes, London merchandised them.20 Talking Heads played their first show in June 1975, opening for the Ramones with “Psycho Killer” in the set and roughly ten people in the room; Blondie came up through the same bar.21 Patti Smith’s Horses, produced by John Cale and released November 10, 1975, gave the scene its first LP — a vinyl shortage had pushed the date from Rimbaud’s birthday to Rimbaud’s death anniversary, a coincidence Smith called “magical.”22 The Ramones’ debut followed in April 1976 and codified the sound: fourteen songs, twenty-nine minutes, every chord downstroked.23

The British detonation (1976–1978). The Sex Pistols formed out of McLaren and Vivienne Westwood’s shop at 430 King’s Road and played a first gig in November 1975 that was cut off within minutes.24 The acceleration came from New York: the Ramones played London that Independence Day weekend, 1976, with the Damned’s future members in the crowd at the Roundhouse and the Pistols and the Clash waiting outside Dingwalls to meet them. “Everyone sped up after that Dingwalls gig,” Captain Sensible said.25 By September the 100 Club Punk Special could bill the Clash, the Damned, Buzzcocks, a debuting Siouxsie and the Banshees, and the Pistols across two Oxford Street nights.26 The Damned’s “New Rose” (October 22, 1976, on Stiff) beat the Pistols to vinyl by five weeks — by most counts the first British punk single, its spoken intro lifted from the girl-group shelf.27 “Anarchy in the U.K.” followed in November on EMI; five days after its release the Pistols, booked as last-minute replacements for Queen, answered host Bill Grundy’s on-air dare to “say something outrageous,” and the Daily Mirror’s front page (THE FILTH AND THE FURY!) made punk a national emergency by breakfast.28 EMI dropped them in January; A&M signed and dropped them inside a week in March. “God Save the Queen” came out on Virgin in May, was banned by the BBC, and sat at No. 2 on the official chart in Jubilee week amid credible, never-resolved allegations that the chart rules were changed for exactly that week — the NME’s chart had it at No. 1.29 Never Mind the Bollocks entered the album chart at No. 1 that October past the chains that refused to stock it, while a Nottingham obscenity prosecution over the title collapsed in court.30 The imperial phase burned out in fourteen months: at Winterland in San Francisco, January 14, 1978, Johnny Rotten closed the band’s last show with “Ever get the feeling you’ve been cheated?” and walked off.31 The coda was the ending the tabloids had scripted all along: Nancy Spungen stabbed dead in a Chelsea Hotel room in October 1978, Sid Vicious charged with her murder and dead of an overdose before trial.32

Do-it-yourself becomes infrastructure (1976–1980). The family’s second invention was procedural. Mark Perry, a Deptford bank clerk, started Sniffin’ Glue on a children’s typewriter days after the Ramones’ London weekend; the first issue ran about fifty photocopies, the last about fifteen thousand, and the smudged staples-and-marker look advertised how little it took.33 Buzzcocks pressed Spiral Scratch with a few hundred borrowed pounds and released it in January 1977 on their own New Hormones label — the first English punk record self-released on a band’s own label, its sleeve printing the take count as if to prove anyone could check the work — and the EP is standardly cited as the seed of “indie.”34 The infrastructure grew fast. Rough Trade opened as a West London record shop in February 1976 and became a label in 1978, and Stiff Little Fingers’ Inflammable Material became the first independent-label LP to reach the UK Top 20 in 1979, on its way past a hundred thousand copies. In January 1980 a trade paper printed the first UK Independent Chart, eligibility defined by distribution, which is how “indie” hardened from an economics into a genre word.35 The tribunal convened the same season the infrastructure did. Perry filed the founding indictment: “Punk died the day the Clash signed to CBS” (January 1977, for £100,000).36 Crass built the counter-world the indictment implied: a commune-based collective whose Feeding of the 5000 shipped with a track replaced by silence after pressing-plant workers refused it, whose sleeves printed “pay no more than” over the price, and whose own “Punk Is Dead” formalized Perry’s charge.37

Hardcore America (1978–1986). The second American wave made the permission a national system. Black Flag’s Nervous Breakdown came out on SST, the label Greg Ginn grew from the ham-radio mail-order business he had started at twelve, and the band toured into existence the hall-and-basement circuit that every band after them drove.38 The Germs’ (GI) (October 1979, produced by Joan Jett) is often counted the first full-length hardcore album, and Dead Kennedys’ Fresh Fruit for Rotting Vegetables (September 1980) gave the movement its satirical brain.39 Washington had the fastest band and the strictest conscience. Bad Brains, the Black quartet who set that speed, were blacklisted from their own city’s clubs (“Banned in D.C.” answered the ban) and moved to New York.40 Ian MacKaye and Jeff Nelson founded Dischord in 1980 to press their old band’s EP and ran it on handshakes and fifty-fifty splits; Minor Threat’s “Straight Edge”, forty-six seconds on their 1981 debut EP, accidentally named an abstinence movement MacKaye never meant to lead.41 The economics were all-ages: rented halls, five-dollar doors, matinee shows, tour routes traded through fanzines.42 By mid-decade the wave was breaking its own rules — SST released Hüsker Dü’s Zen Arcade and the Minutemen’s Double Nickels on the Dime in the same July, answering double albums from a genre built on ninety-second songs (“Take that, Hüskers!” ran the Minutemen’s liner jab) — and the Replacements left Twin/Tone for Sire a year after Let It Be.43 The standard end date, 1986, is a historian’s bracket rather than an event; the bands had already scattered into what college radio was learning to call alternative, the decade Our Band Could Be Your Life documents band by band.44

The breakthrough and the tribunal (1990–present). The nineties began with the machinery turned inward: Bikini Kill, formed in Olympia in October 1990, and the Riot Grrrl zine crowd around them aimed punk’s own tools — the fanzine, the demo, the manifesto — at the scene’s sexism, under the program “Revolution Girl Style Now” and a floor policy of girls to the front.45 Then the permission hit the charts. Green Day’s Dookie (February 1994) went diamond by decade’s end and double diamond by 2024; the Offspring’s Smash, released that spring on Epitaph, is commonly cited as the best-selling independent-label album ever.46 The tribunal answered at scale. 924 Gilman Street, the Berkeley collective Green Day came up through, barred them under its no-majors policy. Jawbreaker, who had told Ben Weasel’s zine they had no interest in a major, took a million-dollar DGC deal, and Weasel’s Maximumrocknroll column ran a photograph of the columnist eating his own hat, as promised. Dear You sold about forty thousand copies, the band broke up within a year, and its canonization arrived only posthumously.47 Rancid supplied the counter-proof, turning down the majors (Madonna’s Maverick included) and going platinum on Epitaph, while Fugazi’s five-dollar shows had been proving the ethic nightly for years.48 The mall era followed, Warped Tour from 1995 and blink-182’s Enema of the State (1999) selling pop punk at teen-pop scale, while emo ran its waves from Rites of Spring’s 1985 Dischord debut (the “emo” tag was critics’ work, refused by the band’s own singer) to Jimmy Eat World’s Bleed American (2001), the genre’s first real radio breakthrough.49 Lydon had opened the other exit decades earlier, walking offstage at Winterland into Public Image Ltd, the door through which post-punk left.50 The 2020s returned the sound to the top of the charts, Machine Gun Kelly’s Tickets to My Downfall debuting at No. 1 with Travis Barker producing and “good 4 u” carrying pop-punk grammar to a No. 1 debut of its own.51 The room that started it closed on October 15, 2006, after the landlord declined to renew the lease. Patti Smith played the final set, three and a half hours, and ended past one in the morning with “Elegie”, reading the names of the scene’s dead over the outro. Kristal died the following August, and the awning hangs in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame. The address sells designer menswear now.52

The door policy

The tribunal is the family’s oldest institution, older than most of its genres. Perry filed the first indictment within the founding year, and the docket never closed: Crass’s “Punk Is Dead” against the Exploited’s Punks Not Dead (1981, that year’s best-selling independent record) was an argument between punks with both sides pressing records to make it, and Gilman’s door policy, Weasel’s photographed hat, Fugazi’s ticket ceiling, and Rancid’s refusals ran the same case through the nineties.53 The ferocity has a structural explanation: in a family where the economics are the aesthetics, every commercial success is a crisis of definition.54 Authenticity and its discontents anatomizes the stance underneath (a punk band’s amateurism is performed, as codified as any professionalism), and the tribunal is that stance hardened into institutions.55 The permission was never a skills ceiling. Television’s guitar interplay and Bad Brains’ precision lived inside the same tent; the claim was about the right to start, and it set no ceiling on where anyone finished. Other families argue over who is best; this one argues over who is real — and the argument outlived the safety pins.

Key debates

Which city holds the birth certificate? New York has the room and the date; London has the explosion. The American case is an oral-history montage, the Velvets-to-Ramones lineage in the participants’ own voices; the British case is an art-movement analysis staged from a King’s Road shop, and the two standard texts, Please Kill Me and England’s Dreaming, still disagree about the address.56 The wire between them is The transatlantic feedback loop at its cleanest: Hell’s safety pins crossed the ocean in McLaren’s memory, the Ramones’ Independence Day shows accelerated every London band that met them, and Britain then sold the movement back to America as a coherent thing with a name and a wardrobe.57 This family starts its clock at the Bowery — the room and the rule, 1974 — and gives London the detonation; the Bowery keeps the date, and London keeps the noise.

Whose permission was it? The claim said anyone; the room’s sightlines said less. The band that set hardcore’s speed standard was Black and banned in its own capital, and the founding oral history’s closing defense of the scene’s racism struck its most prominent reviewer as unconvincing.58 The gender audit arrived from inside, riot grrrl running punk’s zine machinery against punk itself. The correctives are now a literature: the Afro-Punk documentary (2003) seeded the festival that carries its name, the White Riot anthology (2011) assembled the race archive (the scene press panned even that), and What Are You Doing Here? carries the question Black women got asked at hardcore and punk shows no less than metal ones.59 The family’s best defense is its own machinery: it hands its critics the means of production as a matter of principle. Whether the permission ever reached the door is the one audit the family has never closed.
