Cinematic classical

Periodc. 1933–present
LocationHollywood first, then wherever the orchestras are — London and Vienna scoring stages, Tokyo, Santa Monica
Influences
Wagner’s leitmotifThe late Romanticism of Strauss and MahlerThe studio system that paid for a symphony every week
LinksRYM

In 1933 a Viennese émigré named Max Steiner laid continuous orchestral music under most of a film about a giant ape, and in doing so fixed the grammar of how movies would sound for the next ninety years. Silent houses had kept pit orchestras for decades, and Steiner had rehearsed the technique the year before on Symphony of Six Million; what King Kong added was a through-composed symphonic score that tracked the drama beat by beat, written in the idiom of the concert hall Steiner had left behind.1 He had reason to know that idiom. His godfather was Richard Strauss, his teachers were of Mahler’s Vienna, and what he carried to California was that whole nineteenth-century sound.2 This is the music almost everyone alive has heard and almost nobody can name. Its own listeners hum the shark and the Force and the Ring; the reference databases call the style cinematic classical, a coinage that appears nowhere in the critical literature and belongs entirely to the map that files it.3

The émigré grammar

Golden-Age Hollywood scoring was European art music with an American passport. The composers who built it — Steiner, Erich Wolfgang Korngold, Franz Waxman, Dimitri Tiomkin — were largely Jewish émigrés fleeing Nazism, and they scored the studios’ pictures in the tonal language of Brahms and Wagner because it was the language they already spoke.4 Their central technique was Wagner’s: the leitmotif, a musical tag pinned to a character or an idea and woven through the whole, so a score could carry the story the way an opera carries it. Korngold made the parallel exact, treating each film as, in his phrase, an opera without singing, and his richest scores read as tone poems in the Strauss line.5 The stakes were not only aesthetic. When Korngold’s music for The Adventures of Robin Hood won at the 1938 Academy Awards, it was the first time the scoring Oscar went to the composer rather than the head of the studio’s music department. Winning that credit for the composer had been a fight the émigrés pressed after Korngold’s own earlier win was handed to an executive who had not written a note.6 That the Robin Hood commission reached him in California while the Anschluss closed Austria behind him gave the argument an edge; Korngold said the score saved his life.7 The sound of the era was everywhere at once, down to the fanfare Alfred Newman wrote in 1933 for a studio logo, still blaring in front of Twentieth Century Fox pictures ninety years on.8

The middle decades

The postwar generation kept the symphonic frame and pushed on its edges. Bernard Herrmann, who had debuted scoring Citizen Kane for Orson Welles, turned the orchestra into an instrument of psychology in his work for Hitchcock: the circling, obsessive figures of Vertigo in 1958, and then Psycho in 1960, scored for strings alone to match the black-and-white photograph, its shrieking murder cue written against Hitchcock’s explicit instruction for no music at all.9 The reference databases file Herrmann’s Psycho under cinematic classical with modern classical alongside it, the taxonomy’s way of registering the atonal, cluster-chord violence that the style, born in Strauss, had grown room for.10 Where Herrmann went inward, Miklós Rózsa went vast, researching antiquity to build the archaic brass sound of the widescreen epic; his Ben-Hur score of 1959 was the longest yet written for a film, recorded by a hundred players, and it set the template for the Roman and Biblical spectacle for years after.11 Jazz found the underscore in the same decade: Alex North’s A Streetcar Named Desire (1951) is generally counted the first dramatic jazz film score, and Elmer Bernstein’s The Man with the Golden Arm (1955) put a big band behind a heroin drama. Jerry Goldsmith carried a different modernism into the studio picture, the percussive, near-atonal Planet of the Apes (1968).12 Then the frame nearly broke. From the late 1960s the studios dismissed their orchestras, and directors reached for compiled pop and rock instead of an original score — The Graduate, Easy Rider, Midnight Cowboy — and for a decade the symphonic score looked like a dead form.13

The revival

It came back in one picture. In 1977 John Williams scored Star Wars for an eighty-seven-piece London Symphony Orchestra across two weeks at Denham, a full-blown late-Romantic score in the Korngold idiom, complete with leitmotifs for every hero and machine, and it revived the symphonic film score wholesale.14 The record sold more than four million copies, an unheard-of figure for orchestral music, and Williams built a run from it: Jaws, Superman, Raiders of the Lost Ark, E.T., Schindler’s List.15 The American Film Institute later ranked his Star Wars the finest film score of the century’s first hundred years, and he became the most Oscar-nominated living person, with more than fifty nominations, second in Academy history only to Walt Disney.16 Danny Elfman and James Horner worked the same neo-Romantic vein, and the technique reached its fullest late flowering in Howard Shore’s score for The Lord of the Rings (2001–03), a leitmotivic web some estimate at around eighty distinct themes, all traceable to a handful of central ideas.17 Steiner’s Wagnerian grammar of 1933 had survived, at maximum scale, into the twenty-first-century blockbuster.

What the tags know

On the reference databases a film score’s genre field answers two different questions at once. One axis names what the release is: film score, and its sibling video game music, both of which live under a separate soundtrack root, nothing to do with the classical tree.18 The other axis names what the music sounds like: cinematic classical and its neighbors (orchestral music, modern classical, ambient), the styles filed inside the classical family. A score usually carries a tag from each. Star Wars reads film score plus cinematic classical plus orchestral music: the release type, the style, and the ensemble that plays it.19 The two tags need not agree. Jeremy Soule’s Skyrim score files as video game music plus cinematic classical plus orchestral music — the release type swapped, the style held, the genre’s own “games and other non-live media” clause made literal.20 Ennio Morricone’s The Good, the Bad and the Ugly skips the parent style for a child of it, filing as film score plus spaghetti western.21 Hans Zimmer’s Inception does the same with the other child, epic music, and no cinematic classical at all.22 His Interstellar, where the score turns minimal and organ-drenched, leads with film score plus ambient and drops cinematic classical to a secondary tag, though Zimmer still carries the style on his own artist page.23 And Hildur Guðnadóttir’s Joker is the one record where cinematic classical and modern classical share a release — the seam where the Hollywood orchestral lineage meets the newer, austerer wave discussed below.24 Five scores, five configurations of the same two questions.

The two children

The style has two children on the tree, each a full world getting its own note in time. Epic music is the trailer sound of the 2010s: taiko drums under massive brass and choir, minor-key figures built to a climax, and the low, blaring brass-and-synth blast known onomatopoeically as the braaam, whose ubiquity is usually traced to Inception.25 A film’s score is one of the last things finished in post-production, while its trailer is cut months earlier, so trailer houses commission bespoke “epic” cues that may never appear in the finished film. Two Steps From Hell and Audiomachine, production-music libraries founded in the mid-2000s, industrialized exactly this: writing cues for a thousand trailers, then discovering the cues had a public and selling them as albums, several of which reached the top of the Billboard classical-crossover chart.26 The other child runs backward in time. Spaghetti western is the sound Ennio Morricone built for Sergio Leone in the mid-1960s: twanging baritone guitar and whistling and ocarina and wordless choir, drawing cues from surf rock and Mexican music and the concert tradition alike, structured to build from a lone resonance to a roaring climax at the duel.27

The living economy

If the museum’s older repertory is a permanent collection, this style is the part of the orchestral economy that still writes new music for a mass audience, and that audience increasingly sits in a game. Game scores climbed the same ladder film scores did, held back at first by hardware that could sound only a few notes at once, then reaching full orchestra and choir as the machines allowed. Nobuo Uematsu, whose Final Fantasy scores earned him the tag of the video-game world’s John Williams, became the first game composer to chart in Classic FM’s listener-voted Hall of Fame; the Distant Worlds concert series built from his scores has played more than two hundred and fifty sold-out shows, reaching Carnegie Hall in 2018.28 Koji Kondo, Nintendo’s first dedicated composer, wrote the Super Mario Bros. and Zelda themes under a constraint that separates game scoring from film scoring at the root: game music must survive infinite repetition, looping under gameplay for hours without wearing out, where a film cue runs once, to picture.29 The concert circuit has caught up to all of it — Journey put a game score beside Williams and Zimmer at the 2013 Grammys, and by 2023 game music had its own Grammy category.30 Meanwhile Hans Zimmer built Remote Control Productions into the supply side of the whole wing, a Santa Monica workshop that trained a generation of screen composers on a stated ethic of shared credit; explaining why he gave Lisa Gerrard a co-credit on Gladiator, he said her contribution was the reason “I have such a problem with specific credits.”31 Then he took the music back out of the screen and onto the arena stage, turning the film composer into a touring headliner across Europe — the factory making the repertory, and the repertory filling the halls.32

The border with the newer wave

One neighbor is often mistaken for this one and files, on the databases, somewhere else. The generation of Max Richter, Jóhann Jóhannsson, Ólafur Arnalds, and Nils Frahm writes chamber-scale music for piano and treated electronics that scores plenty of films, yet their records land under modern classical or ambient, not cinematic classical. The sound decides the shelf, not the paycheck.33 Richter’s “On the Nature of Daylight” reached cinema first as a licensed placement, recurring across Shutter Island and Arrival and a dozen more before the scoring commissions followed.34 The two worlds touch at exactly one record in the evidence: Guðnadóttir’s Joker, which carries both tags, and whose composer came up playing cello across Jóhannsson’s circle before winning the 2019 Best Original Score Oscar — the first woman to take that award since the Academy reunified it into a single category, after more than two decades in which no woman had won any of its scoring prizes.35 The wave the press calls “neoclassical” rejects that name almost to a person: Frahm calls the term “so vacant and dumb,” and Chilly Gonzales, classically trained, wrote an essay regretting that he may have birthed the genre.36 Cinematic classical has the opposite problem, a name no critic uses at all, which makes the pair a study in a music too popular to be granted a settled word.

Is it classical?

The oldest charge against this whole style is that it is merely applied music, composed to serve a picture rather than to stand on its own — a case given its heavyweight form by Theodor Adorno and Hanns Eisler in Composing for the Films (1947), the culture-industry critique of Hollywood scoring, sharpened by the irony that Eisler, a Schoenberg pupil, wrote film scores himself.37 The counter-argument has shifted from aesthetics to institutions, and it has largely won. Prokofiev’s and Shostakovich’s and Copland’s film music entered the concert hall long ago; The Nutcracker and The Rite of Spring were written to accompany a stage spectacle, the only difference from a film cue being screen versus stage. And the same orchestras that once quarantined Hollywood in the “pops” concert now program it to sell tickets: John Williams conducted the Vienna Philharmonic at the Musikverein in 2020, the yellow-label classical establishment putting a film composer in front of its most storied orchestra.38 The academy has followed the box office: film scoring now has a musicology of its own, from the theorist Frank Lehman’s analysis of its harmonic language to Mervyn Cooke’s single-volume history of the field — a scholarship that has stopped asking whether the subject is worthy and started treating it as one.39 The “is it classical?” fight is the exact inverse of the one at the neoclassical border. There, the worry is that ambient piano is too slight to be art. Here, the worry was that film music is too popular and too useful to be art. Both are the museum deciding what it will admit, and on the evidence of the box office and the concert calendar alike, it has admitted this.40
