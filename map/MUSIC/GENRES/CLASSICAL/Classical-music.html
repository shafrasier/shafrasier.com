In 1829, the English organist Vincent Novello wrote in his diary of a Continental chapel that it was the place “I should come to every Sunday when I wished to hear classical music correctly and judiciously performed” — the first sentence the Oxford English Dictionary can find with the family’s name in it.1 The word arrived late and arrived as a verdict. Every other family on this MAP got named by a repurposed slur or a marketer’s bin; this one was christened in the early nineteenth century to canonize the span from Bach to Beethoven as a golden age — a museum label, written for a collection its curators considered finished.2 Classical music is the museum: the family that keeps permanent works instead of passing hits, whose central ritual is the faithful performance of scores by the dead, and whose thousand-year history is the story of how the collection was assembled, who was admitted, and what the music did once the doors were declared closed. The famous joke is that nothing about it is classical (no Greco-Roman thread connects the name to the practice), and the deeper joke is on the label itself, because the collection never actually closed.3 On the reference databases today, the best-ranked classical album of all time is Steve Reich’s Music for 18 Musicians, a 1978 pulse-and-breath ensemble record on a jazz label, voted there by exactly the general listeners the museum was built to educate.4

Scope and boundaries

The family is global at the root. The reference taxonomy’s classical umbrella opens at civilizational scale, with Western classical music (the tradition this note’s arc follows, and by far the largest wing) sitting beside South Asian classical music, the raga-and-tala system split since the sixteenth century into a Persian-inflected Hindustani north and a Carnatic south, and East Asian classical music, the traditions descending from the Chinese courts, whose Zhou-dynasty yayue seeded Japan’s gagaku and Korea’s aak. Southeast Asian classical music brings the gong-chime orchestras (gamelan, kulintang, piphat); Maqāmic music is the one wing a theory system defines, its maqām scales running from Morocco to Central Asia; and individual African court traditions (Buganda royal court music, Burundi’s inkiranya, the Asante kete) stand at the same level because the tree honestly declines to force them under an invented continental hood.5 What the wings share, by the taxonomy’s own sociology, is a structure: each is its culture’s “refined” tradition, distinguished from folk practice by elite patronage, full-time training, and rigid norms of correct performance, which is why the classical concept “seems to be absent in non-hierarchical societies.”6 These wings are named here as the root’s own first level and told nowhere on the MAP yet; they arrive as their own children when their content earns them, and the vault’s standing mentions of Indian classical music describe that tradition, never this arc. The Western tradition’s internal machinery (the six-period scheme, the forms, the tree as the reference databases file it) lives with Western classical music.

The borders are treaties. Electronic owns the laboratory century — Cologne, the tape studios, the concrète-versus-elektronische schism — and the reference taxonomies agree, filing electroacoustic and musique concrète under the experimental root. Third stream, the fusion Gunther Schuller named in 1957, is Jazz’s story to tell. Musical theater is its own future umbrella, operetta the shared border node. Film scoring splits cleanly: cinematic classical, the orchestral style, sits inside this family’s tree, while the film-score release type files under soundtracks. Minimalism lives here, in the Western tree’s modern wing, with one committed exception: the drones of La Monte Young and Éliane Radigue remain Ambient’s ancestry, per that family’s founding note. And the crossover shelves (classical crossover, baroque pop, the neoclassical new-age bin) belong to the popular families that built them.7

Major branches

The long tradition (c. 500–1750). The deep past compresses into one branch because one technology organizes all of it: notation. The tradition the taxonomies date from around 500 begins as liturgical chant (plainsong, codified into the repertories now called Gregorian), and the slow invention of a writing system for sound is what separated this practice from every folk tradition around it, making music storable and, eventually, ownable.8 The names the record keeps are the notated ones: Hildegard von Bingen, the twelfth-century abbess whose visionary chants survive because she wrote them down; Palestrina, whose counterpoint became the church’s model of correctness; Monteverdi, who carried the madrigal into the new theatrical form called opera; Vivaldi and Handel running concerto and oratorio as working commercial enterprises; and Bach, the Leipzig cantor writing church cantatas to deadline. The standard period scheme (Medieval to 1400, Renaissance to 1600, Baroque to 1750) is a teaching convenience, and its historians say so.9 This half-millennium was for church and court. Music served occasions — the mass, the coronation, the dinner — and the idea that a composition was a permanent work, to be preserved and revered, had not yet been invented.10

The museum gets built (1750–1900). It was invented here, and it can be watched happening. Eighteenth-century England went first, performing old works “regularly and reverentially” under the banner of “ancient music” — the historian William Weber’s account of the first repertory culture, a canon emerging “quietly and inconspicuously, out of the practices and customs of musical convention.”11 The first general histories followed in 1776, from Charles Burney and John Hawkins; Johann Nikolaus Forkel’s followed in 1788, and his 1802 biography of Bach, written from correspondence with Bach’s sons, began the revival that turned a dead Leipzig cantor into the tradition’s foundation stone.12 Around 1800, on the philosopher Lydia Goehr’s dating, the work-concept crystallized: music stopped being an event and became a thing, a permanent object belonging to the one who wrote it, with Beethoven as the pivot figure whose scores came to demand strict obedience from performers.13 Then came the label: Danneley’s 1820 title and Novello’s 1829 diary sealed the golden age, quietly reclassifying the living composers of the day as caretakers of a finished collection.2 The nineteenth century filled the museum’s halls: Haydn and Mozart were retrofitted as the classical core; Chopin and Liszt made the virtuoso recital; Wagner and Verdi fought over opera’s future; Brahms composed with the weight of the collection on his shoulders; Tchaikovsky proved the museum exportable to Russia. By 1900 the ritual was set, the silent hall and the sanctified repertory with the conductor as high priest — Goehr’s imaginary museum, fully furnished.13

The American import (1842–1920s). America built the museum’s grandest wings and stocked them almost entirely with imports. The New York Philharmonic was founded in 1842; Boston followed in 1881, Chicago in 1891, Philadelphia in 1900, Cleveland in 1918 — the “Big Five,” staffed substantially by the German immigration of the 1840s and 1850s, whose touring ensembles had seeded orchestral standards city by city.14 The historian Joseph Horowitz’s verdict on what America made of the inheritance is the standing account: a “performance culture,” its peak in the 1890s, built on celebrity maestros and a repertory of European imports, with native composing left at the margins.15 Then, in 1893, Antonín Dvořák, imported to direct New York’s National Conservatory, told the New York Herald that “the future music of this country must be founded upon what are called the negro melodies” — the songs of America, he said, and America’s composers must turn to them. He knew the material because Harry Burleigh, his Black assistant, a baritone and composer, sang him the spirituals. The interview ignited a months-long debate riddled with racist invective, and Dvořák answered it with the New World Symphony that December at Carnegie Hall.16 The prophecy is this family’s hinge with the rest of the MAP: American music did found itself on Black melody, in blues, jazz, gospel, R&B, and everything downstream, almost entirely outside the concert hall that commissioned the prediction.

The broken canon (1900–1957). The twentieth century broke the collection’s frame from inside and out, and the critic Alex Ross’s history tells it as the century’s own biography.17 Mahler and Richard Strauss stretched the inherited forms to their limits. Schoenberg abandoned tonality and then systematized its replacement, and Stravinsky’s rhythmic violence made The Rite of Spring the century’s scandal and then its repertory staple. America’s originals worked in the museum’s blind spots. Charles Ives ran an insurance agency and composed on nights and commuter trains, largely unperformed until the 1930s; his Third Symphony waited until 1947 for its Pulitzer.18 George Gershwin walked jazz into Aeolian Hall in 1924 (the premiere told at Tin Pan Alley), and Aaron Copland built the open-interval Americana that “many consider the sound of American music,” a style he described as “plain, singing, comparatively uncomplicated and slightly folksy.”19 And the museum ran its acquisitions policy in plain sight. William Grant Still’s Afro-American Symphony, premiered by the Rochester Philharmonic in 1931, was the first symphony by a Black American played by a leading American orchestra, and thirty-four orchestras across America and Europe performed it in the 1930s. Florence Price’s Symphony in E minor, premiered by the Chicago Symphony under Frederick Stock on June 15, 1933, at a Century of Progress concert titled “The Negro in Music,” was the first by a Black woman. William Levi Dawson’s Negro Folk Symphony premiered under Stokowski in 1934 to praise from critics Black and white. All three then fell out of the repertory — written out, the corrective histories now argue, by a modernist “standard narrative” that wrote everything before 1910 off as prelude.20

The downtown revolt (1958–1990). The postwar museum split into three establishments, and the interesting one had no building. Uptown, Leonard Bernstein, the first American-born conductor to achieve international acclaim and famous overnight since his unrehearsed 1943 Carnegie Hall substitution, put the canon on national television: fifty-three Young People’s Concerts on CBS from January 1958, three of those years in prime time, while West Side Story carried his harmonies to Broadway.21 In the academy, the serialists inherited Schoenberg’s system and gave up on the audience in print: Milton Babbitt’s 1958 High Fidelity essay argued that advanced music, like advanced physics, owed the public nothing; his editor retitled it “Who Cares if You Listen?”, a title Babbitt spent decades protesting, though his own text had praised the composer’s “total, resolute, and voluntary withdrawal from this public world.”22 Downtown — a real geography, dated by the critic Kyle Gann to the 1960 concert series in Yoko Ono’s Chambers Street loft, against the uptown axis of Lincoln Center, Juilliard, and Columbia — the revolt got a sound.23 Terry Riley’s In C, premiered November 4, 1964, at the San Francisco Tape Music Center with Steve Reich in the ensemble, reduced composition to fifty-three repeating modules and let the players breathe. Reich found phasing in 1965 when two tape loops of a Pentecostal preacher drifted apart on his machines. Philip Glass was still driving a taxi when Einstein on the Beach sold out two rented nights at the Metropolitan Opera House in November 1976.24 When Music for 18 Musicians had its Town Hall premiere that April, the standing ovation was immediate; the 1978 ECM recording carried minimalism to the rock audience, and the wire ran onward: Brian Eno building on Reich’s tape-loop discoveries, the Orb sampling Electric Counterpoint, the family’s pulse becoming Electronic and Ambient ancestry in real time.25 The museum’s response completed the story: Arvo Pärt’s devotional stillness now sits beside Reich near the top of the crowd-ranked classical canon.4

The streaming museum (1990–present). The collection’s late life is enormous, and it runs on new patronage. On the eve of the 1990 World Cup final, José Carreras, Plácido Domingo, and Luciano Pavarotti sang from the Baths of Caracalla to a reported eight hundred million viewers, and the resulting album became the best-selling classical record in history — a feat no classical album has repeated.26 The living orchestral economy moved to the screen: Hans Zimmer’s Remote Control Productions runs film scoring as a deliberately collective workshop (“not having ‘you are the composer, you are the arranger, you are the orchestrator’… helped us to work more collaboratively”), the scores of John Williams and Ennio Morricone became the orchestral repertory of everyday life, and Nobuo Uematsu’s Final Fantasy music, touring as Distant Worlds, became in 2011 the first game-music production to play the Royal Albert Hall.27 The podium renewed itself from outside the old world: Gustavo Dudamel, raised in Venezuela’s El Sistema, takes the New York Philharmonic’s Mahler-and-Bernstein chair in the 2026–27 season, the first Latino to hold it.28 The streaming shelf built its own stars and its own fight — Ludovico Einaudi is billed by his label as the most-streamed classical artist of all time while critics contest both the numbers’ meaning and the genre claim, and the Erased Tapes generation of Ólafur Arnalds and Nils Frahm records piano-and-electronics chamber music the taxonomies file half in and half out of the family.29 And the museum finally audited its basement. In 2009, a couple renovating Florence Price’s derelict summer house in St. Anne, Illinois, found her manuscripts in the attic: two violin concertos, a symphony, music that had come “painfully close to vanishing forever.” In 2022 the Philadelphia Orchestra’s recording of her First and Third symphonies won the orchestra its first-ever Grammy for Best Orchestral Performance.30 The youngest wall fell quietly: Caroline Shaw, the youngest-ever winner of the Pulitzer for music in 2013, spent 2015 and 2016 singing on Kanye West records, and nobody on either side treated it as a scandal.31

The imaginary museum

The family’s institution is not a label or a club; it is the canon itself, and the philosopher Lydia Goehr gave it the name this section borrows: an imaginary museum of musical works, assembled around 1800 and run on rules no other family enforces.13 The repertory is the permanent collection. Fidelity to the score is the house ethic — the performer as curator, interpretation judged the way restorations are judged. The silence of the hall is the velvet rope. The economics are a museum’s too: endowments, boards, subscription patrons, and state subsidy stand where every other family’s market stands, which is what let Babbitt’s wing propose research without an audience and survive the proposal.22 The MAP’s own filing carries the museum’s fingerprint: this is the one family organized composer-first, because the work-concept made the writer the owner of the object.13 And the museum’s acquisitions policy is the moral of the whole arc — Weber’s gentlemen collecting “ancient music,” the 1820s labelers sealing the golden age, the American orchestras buying European, the attic in St. Anne holding what the collection declined to keep. The Invention of Folk Music and Art Music supplies the frame the MAP runs on: the museum and the field were born as each other’s negatives at the same turn of the century, which is why this family’s border disputes are everyone’s origin story.32 Cultural omnivorousness tracks what became of the hierarchy the museum sat on top of; the snob who listened only to this family is now statistically extinct, and the museum’s audience survives as one taste among many.33

Key debates

A finished shape or a living practice? The strongest scholarly version of the museum claim is Richard Taruskin’s: his monument history rests on the postulate that the literate tradition has “a completed shape,” its beginnings known and “its end… now foreseeable.”34 The living evidence keeps voting against the obituary. The crowd-built canon ranks a 1978 minimalist ensemble record first, and the orchestral economy thrives on scores for screens the museum doesn’t count. The Pulitzer’s youngest music laureate moonlights on rap albums, and the recovered Price symphonies win Grammys.4 Read one way, all of that is the museum’s afterlife — rankings and film cues as the collection’s gift shop. Read the other, the tradition simply moved out of the building and kept composing, and “classical” names a living practice again. The family’s own naming history sides with the second reading: the label declared the collection closed in the 1820s, and the collection has been acquiring ever since.

Whose classical? The word will not stay put. Globally, the family’s own taxonomy makes “classical” a structural role (the elite, trained, codified tradition any hierarchical society may grow), which makes the Western habit of using the bare word for one wing a standing imprecision the scholars themselves flag; there is no clean fix, since the alternatives stumble too.35 And at home the title has been claimed twice on this MAP, deliberately: Jazz carries Billy Taylor’s “America’s classical music,” the claim Congress came close to codifying in 1987, and Tin Pan Alley’s Songbook devotees make the same claim for the standards. This family contests neither. Both claims borrow exactly what this arc describes — the museum’s prestige and its right to a repertory — and the borrowing is the strongest evidence of what the museum built. A name coined to close a collection became the world’s shorthand for music that deserves to be kept.

Sources drawn on

  • Southern — The Music of Black Americans — Black American music has a continuous, documentable history from 1619 forward — spirituals, church music, minstrelsy, ragtime, blues, jazz, and the concert tradition as one story — and writing that history as scholarship, without polemic, is itself the field-founding act.
  • Goehr — The Imaginary Museum of Musical Works — The work-concept — music as fixed, repeatable works owned by composers — is not a timeless fact but a regulative concept that crystallized around 1800, and once operative it rewrote the whole practice: the concert ritual, the ideal of fidelity, and the repertory as a museum of permanent objects.
  • Horowitz — Classical Music in America — American classical music rose to a genuine peak in the 1890s and declined after World War I into a 'culture of performance' — celebrity conductors and imported European masterpieces in place of a canon of native works — and the gatekeepers' two errors were preferring Europeans to American composers and past masterpieces to living ones.
  • Taruskin — Oxford History of Western Music — The Western literate tradition — music disseminated through writing — deserves a true history rather than a celebratory survey: an explanation of why things happened as they did, told by one polemical author across six volumes, on the postulate that the tradition has a completed shape whose beginnings are known and whose end is now foreseeable.
  • Gelbart — The Invention of Folk Music and Art Music — Folk music and art music are not natural kinds but a pair of categories invented together around 1800, when Europe stopped sorting music by its function and began sorting it by its origin.
  • Ross — The Rest Is Noise — The twentieth century's classical music is best understood as a history of the twentieth century itself — composers mattered, entangled with wars, states, and technology — and the 'progressive' and 'conservative' camps deserve equal time, because what sounds like noise on first hearing may reveal hidden beauty on the second.
  • Horowitz — Dvořák's Prophecy — Dvořák's 1893 prophecy — that American classical music would be founded on 'negro melodies' — was betrayed: the modernist 'standard narrative' built by Copland, Bernstein, and Thomson declared American music before 1910 inconsequential, writing Burleigh, Coleridge-Taylor, Still, Price, and Dawson into obscurity, and the repair is a 'usable past' that restores them.
