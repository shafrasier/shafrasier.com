Genre

Metal

Periodc. 1970–present
Named1970 (“heavy metal,” first printed as an insult; Steppenwolf’s 1968 “heavy metal thunder” was a motorcycle)
Geographic originBirmingham, England
Influences
British bluesPsychedelia’s loud wing“You Really Got Me“‘s power chord
LinksRYM

In 1965, on his last day at a Birmingham sheet-metal factory, a seventeen-year-old named Tony Iommi was covering for an absent workmate on a guillotine press when the machine took the tips of two fretting-hand fingers.1 Told he would never play again, he melted a dish-soap bottle into homemade fingertips, slackened his strings so they would not hurt, and leaned on two-finger chords because runs were out of reach — and the darker, heavier, chord-massed sound those adaptations produced became a genre’s template.2 Sixty years later, on July 5, 2025, the family gathered at Villa Park in Aston, walking distance from where four factory-town kids had become Black Sabbath: the original lineup one last time, Ozzy Osbourne singing from a black throne because Parkinson’s and a spinal injury had taken his legs, every wing of the music on the bill in tribute. Seventeen days later he was dead.3 The press and the throne bracket one method, and the method names the family: metal is the forge — a sound hammered out of factory-town material, and a community built from the contempt the world kept supplying.

The name arrived as an insult, and the family kept it. William S. Burroughs had a Heavy Metal Kid in his early-sixties fiction, and Steppenwolf’s 1968 “Born to Be Wild” heard “heavy metal thunder” in a motorcycle; the phrase drifted through rock criticism (a 1968 Rolling Stone review heard “heavy metal rock” in Electric Flag) before Mike Saunders damned a band in Rolling Stone in November 1970 as “heavy metal-leaden shit-rock,” then used it approvingly in Creem the following May — the insult turned genre name inside six months.4 Over the decades the old name narrowed. As the family speciated, “heavy metal” came to mean the classic core (the Sabbath-to-Priest-to-Maiden mainline, the sound the seventies built), while the bare word carried the family that grew around it.5

Scope and boundaries

Metal is the family that seceded from Rock, and the parent’s history already tells the departure from its side: the new wave of British heavy metal, Kerrang!, and the Black Album as the milestones of “a parallel world” going its own way.6 What follows tells the same years from inside the world. Two doors lead in. British blues is the bloodline — that family’s own telling runs from Cream’s improvisation through Led Zeppelin’s heaviness to Sabbath’s doom, and its era closes in 1970, at the exact hinge where this one opens.7 Psychedelia is the other door: acid rock played past its limits until it thickened into something heavier, a line that family’s history draws through Blue Cheer.8 The origin exhibits are braided through both: the power chord of “You Really Got Me”, the brutalized volume of Vincebus Eruptum, the permission of the White Album’s “Helter Skelter” — ancestors all, claimed by several histories at once, and none of them yet the thing itself.9

The borders mostly follow the music’s own social history. Glam metal was the Sunset Strip’s invention (pop hooks under metal hair, sold through MTV), a hard rock lineage that never passed through Birmingham; it appears below only where the two worlds collided.10 Grunge came up through alternative rock, whatever it displaced. Trap metal, despite the name, was born inside hip hop, from trap’s machinery and hardcore hip hop’s temperature. Nu metal borrowed hip hop’s rhythms through alternative metal, and that exchange happened here. And metalcore and grindcore were born on the border with hardcore punk, children of both worlds.11

Major branches

The founding forge (1968–1975). The ancestors were loud before the thing had a name: the fuzzed two-note riff of 1964, Blue Cheer’s January 1968 wall of amps, “Helter Skelter’s” permission that November.12 The synthesis came from Aston. Black Sabbath formed in 1968, took their name from a horror film playing across from the rehearsal room (people paid to be frightened, the reasoning went, so why not charge for it in sound), and released Black Sabbath on Friday, February 13, 1970: recorded in a single twelve-hour session, panned on arrival, UK No. 8 anyway.13 The title track’s riff leaned on the tritone, the interval nicknamed diabolus in musica; the “banned medieval interval” folklore is overstated (the phrase is an eighteenth-century pedagogical warning, and no church ever banned it), but the sinister connotation was real and Sabbath weaponized it.14 Paranoid followed in September 1970 and went to UK No. 1; Master of Reality (1971) landed the full down-tuning Iommi’s fingers had demanded, and the genre took it up as a language.15 The neighbors kept their distance in both directions. Deep Purple ran the neighboring track at speed; Led Zeppelin refused the label outright — Robert Plant, pointing at a Judas Priest poster years later: “If I’m responsible for this in any way, then I am really, really embarrassed.”16 The critics refused it too. Lester Bangs’ Rolling Stone review of the debut called Sabbath “unskilled laborers” from “the industrial side of Cream country” and the album “a shuck… just like Cream! But worse” — a factory metaphor more accurate than he knew, aimed at actual factory workers.17

The code hardens (1975–1979). Judas Priest, also from Birmingham, cut the remaining blues tether: Sad Wings of Destiny (1976) set the template of twin lead guitars, operatic vocals, and speed, and the record’s fallen-angel cover introduced the three-pronged cross the band still wears.18 The uniform came two years later: the leather and studs arrived around 1978, drawn from Rob Halford’s private life in leather culture — the genre’s defining costume came from a gay subculture, an origin the scene took decades to say out loud.19 Motörhead, formed in 1975 after Hawkwind fired Lemmy, played faster and uglier than anyone and insisted all the while on being “a rock and roll band” — the bridge to punk that both sides used.20 Ronnie James Dio, arriving in Sabbath at decade’s end, popularized the horns, the corna he took from his Italian grandmother’s ward against the evil eye, though he never claimed to have thrown it first.21 And the theatrical wing supplied the staging, Alice Cooper’s horror vaudeville feeding Kiss’s arena spectacle.22 The press’s verdict hadn’t moved in a decade. The New York Times in 1979: “brutally aggressive music played mostly for minds clouded by drugs.”23

The new wave and ignition (1979–1983). The revival got its name before its records: Sounds ran editor Alan Lewis’s coinage “New Wave of British Heavy Metal” over Geoff Barton’s May 1979 gig review, and the movement it named used the do-it-yourself infrastructure punk had just built (independent singles, fanzines) to put a hundred young bands on vinyl.24 Iron Maiden went from a UK No. 4 debut in 1980 to a No. 1 with The Number of the Beast in 1982.25 Diamond Head’s Lightning to the Nations (1980) mattered less for its sales than its students — “Am I Evil?”, written to out-heavy Sabbath’s “Symptom of the Universe”, became a foundational thrash text.26 The festival economy began at Donington: the first Monsters of Rock drew 35,000 in August 1980, with Judas Priest on the bill.27 Ozzy, fired by Sabbath in 1979, resurrected himself with Blizzard of Ozz and guitarist Randy Rhoads, dead at twenty-five in 1982 when a joyriding tour-bus driver buzzed the bus in a light plane.28 And America answered from the Sunset Strip: Van Halen’s 1978 debut rewired the electric guitar’s vocabulary and seeded the decade’s Los Angeles scene.29

The split decade (1983–1991). The underground built its own distribution first. A 1982 Los Angeles compilation carried an unsigned band misspelled “Mettallica”; their demo that July became a tape-trading sensation, and the cassette network (hand-copied, mailed, traded) served as the genre’s A&R department.30 Thrash came up through it. Metallica’s Kill ‘Em All (1983) opened the run, and Master of Puppets (1986) is widely counted the genre’s first platinum album. Slayer’s Reign in Blood (1986, produced by Rick Rubin) put ten songs into twenty-nine minutes. Megadeth and Anthrax completed what the press dubbed the Big Four.31 The NWOBHM tapes had seeded the Bay Area, and the Americans returned the compliment (Metallica alone covered four Diamond Head songs): the transatlantic loop, running heavier than it ever had.32 Meanwhile MTV ran a second metal this family mostly disowns, a pop-metal tier selling hooks in costume (Bon Jovi, Mötley Crüe); Guns N’ Roses’ Appetite for Destruction, the best-selling debut album in American history, positioned itself as the gutter corrective while charting through the same machine.33 The decade’s panics chose metal as their defendant: the Parents Music Resource Center hearings of September 1985, where Twisted Sister’s Dee Snider testified that the song a Senate wife read as sadomasochism was written about his guitarist’s throat surgery, ended in the Parental Advisory sticker; the Judas Priest subliminal-message trial, brought by the families of two young men who shot themselves in Reno, was dismissed in August 1990 after the families failed to prove the sounds were intentional messages or that they had caused the deaths.34 The era closed with the secession made permanent: Metallica — the Black Album, August 1991 — went on to outsell every other record of the SoundScan age, roughly thirty million copies worldwide; the world that had walked out of rock now ran the bigger economy.35

The extreme wings (1982–1994). Venom, from Newcastle, gave a wing its name with Black Metal (1982), a record more invoked than imitated.36 Death metal’s founding document came from Florida — Death’s Scream Bloody Gore (1987), counted by many as the first true death metal record, with Possessed’s 1985 debut the standing counter-claim — and the scene’s capital became Tampa, where Morbid Angel’s Altars of Madness (1989) made the Morrisound studio the genre’s laboratory.37 Grindcore was invented back in Birmingham: Napalm Death’s Scum (1987, on Earache) cut its first side for £50 and contains “You Suffer”, at 1.3 seconds the shortest song ever recorded, the founding city bookending the family’s first branch and its most extreme.38 Norway’s second wave turned the theater real. Mayhem’s vocalist died by suicide in 1991. The twelfth-century Fantoft stave church burned in June 1992, the first of a wave of arsons. And in August 1993 Varg Vikernes murdered Mayhem’s guitarist Øystein “Euronymous” Aarseth, drawing Norway’s maximum sentence for the killing and three church burnings.39 Doom, meanwhile, stayed home: Candlemass’s Epicus Doomicus Metallicus (1986) named the wing in dog Latin, slow and massive, the branch that never really left 1970.40 Queensrÿche and Dream Theater stretched the family the other way, toward suite-length composition and conservatory chops.41

Crash, mutation, and the fortress (1991–present). Nirvana’s Nevermind flipped MTV’s allegiance in a season, and the standard line says it killed metal; what it killed was metal’s MTV monoculture, while the undergrounds kept growing off camera.42 The sharpest rebuttal came three years into grunge: Pantera’s Far Beyond Driven (1994) entered the Billboard 200 at No. 1, the most extreme record ever to do so to that point.43 Mutation did the rest. Korn’s 1994 debut (downtuned seven-strings, hip hop rhythm, no solos) is usually called the first nu metal album, and the wave it started crested with Linkin Park’s Hybrid Theory (2000), the best-selling debut album of the twenty-first century — while purists disputed all the way whether any of it was metal.44 The economy rebuilt itself in the family’s own name: Ozzfest, founded in 1996 after Lollapalooza turned Ozzy down as irrelevant (Sharon Osbourne: “I’ll show you how relevant he is”), became the artery that carried the nu metal generation.45 Then the fortress years. Wacken, a German village festival that drew 800 people in 1990, now draws 85,000 from eighty countries. Finland counts the most metal bands per capita on earth, by its own government’s boast. Spotify’s listener data ranked metal fans the most loyal of any genre, and Metallica were the only metal act in the global top ten of 2024’s touring economy.46 The institutions eventually arrived too; by the time Turnstile took Best Rock Album in 2026, the family had long since stopped waiting.47 The last act came home: Sabbath’s final bow at Villa Park, July 2025, a benefit that raised a claimed $190 million for two children’s charities and Parkinson’s research — and Ozzy did not outlive the month.48

The pariah economy

The contempt and the self-sufficiency are one story, cause and effect. A music this despised — by the press from the first review, by Congress and the courts within fifteen years — could not rent the industry’s machinery, so it built its own.49 Tape traders moved the records no label would touch. The fanzines grew into a press of the genre’s own, and the festivals into a touring economy that never needed radio. The audience answered with a loyalty streaming platforms still measure.50 Deena Weinstein’s name for that audience has stuck for three decades: the “proud pariahs,” a subculture that took exclusion as identity.51 Even the academy arrived unbidden: the founding studies came in the early nineties, a dedicated scholarly society followed in 2013, and its journal has published since 2015. Jazz campaigned half a century for its canonization; metal’s simply showed up.52

Key debates

Who founded it, and when? Every origin exhibit has a rival claim, and the strongest witnesses disagree from opposite directions: Zeppelin, claimed by every metal history as a foundation stone, refused the genre to the end, while Sabbath, panned nearly everywhere on arrival, never got to refuse anything.53 The elements all predate 1970; the synthesis doesn’t. Dating the family from the Friday-the-13th debut is a convention, but it is the convention the music’s own practice ratified: the tritone, the chord-mass, the two-finger riff (the vocabulary every later wing speaks) assembled on that record and not before.54

Whose fortress is it? The pariah community’s self-image is solidarity, and the scholarship has spent thirty years testing it. Weinstein’s founding sociology defended the audience but read its gender politics through frameworks the field now calls dated. Robert Walser named the mechanism exscription: the world of the music built with the women left out. And Laina Dawes asked the question Black women fans get at the door of the fortress itself — what are you doing here? — documenting a double policing the solidarity story never mentions.55 The global scenes answer from the other side: a Botswana cowboy-metal underground in head-to-toe leather, a metalhead president of Indonesia, Scandinavian towns where the genre is close to a national industry.56 The fortress was always bigger and stranger than its gatekeepers. The debate is whether the door policy ever matched the population.

Sources drawn on

  • Weinstein — Heavy Metal — Heavy metal is a full culture, not just a sound — a bricolage of sonic, visual, and verbal codes sustained by a transaction among artists, fans, and mediators — and its audience of proud pariahs deserves defense, not diagnosis, from the moral panic aimed at it.
  • Walser — Running with the Devil — Heavy metal is musically serious and analyzable — a discourse of power built from distortion, the power chord, and systematically appropriated classical virtuosity — and its gender politics can be read directly out of its sounds and images, including the strategy of simply writing women out.
  • Christe — Sound of the Beast — Heavy metal is a coherent thirty-year tradition with a precise founding — Black Sabbath, February 13, 1970 — and its history is best told the way the fans lived it: band by band, tape by tape, from Birmingham through thrash and black metal to nu metal's arenas.
  • Dawes — What Are You Doing Here? — Black women have always been in metal, hardcore, and punk — as fans, musicians, and inheritors of the Black traditions the music was built from — and the question of the title, asked of them at show after show, is the genre's whiteness talking, not its history.
