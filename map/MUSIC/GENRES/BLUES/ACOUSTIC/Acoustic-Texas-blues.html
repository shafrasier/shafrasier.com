Acoustic Texas blues

Blues → Acoustic blues → Acoustic Texas blues

Period: c. 1905–1982
Location: Texas — Deep Ellum in Dallas, Houston’s wards, the farm and lumber circuits between
Key influences: Songster repertoire, work song, ragtime

RYM

The Texas guitar doesn’t keep time so much as comment on it. Where a Delta player drives a steady, percussive pulse, the Texas style lays back: a relaxed swing in the right hand, the bass line suspended mid-phrase so a single-string run can answer the voice, ornament trailing into ornament. The phrasing is closer to a jazz soloist’s than to a dance drummer’s — accompaniment follows the singer’s breath rather than the bar count — and that improvising, conversational conception of the guitar became Texas’s permanent gift to the blues, acoustic and electric alike.

Origins

The scene assembled itself in Deep Ellum, the Black commercial district that grew up along Dallas’s Central Track — founded in 1873, possibly as a freedmen’s town, and by the 1910s the place where cotton money, vaudeville theaters, and street musicians converged. Around 1912 a young Lead Belly took up with Blind Lemon Jefferson there, working the corners as his partner and guide; the apprenticeship gave the songster tradition and the blues a shared Dallas address. When Paramount began recording Jefferson in 1926, “Long Lonesome Blues” sold in reported six figures — the first time a rural, self-accompanied male bluesman had carried the race market, and the proof that opened the labels to every country blues tradition that followed.

The style’s second act ran through Houston. Lightnin’ Hopkins, discovered singing in the Third Ward, cut his debut for Aladdin in November 1946 — paired with pianist Wilson “Thunder” Smith, which is where the name came from — and proceeded to record more albums than perhaps any bluesman in history, talking, improvising, and refusing to count bars for anyone. The tradition’s other great keeper never left the farm: Mance Lipscomb, a Navasota sharecropper and songster, played community suppers from the 1910s until Chris Strachwitz and Mack McCormick knocked on his door in 1960. His living-room session became Arhoolie Records’ first release. When Hopkins died in January 1982, six years after Lipscomb, the lineage closed as a living practice rather than fading into one.

The sound

Single-string runs against a suspended pulse. The right hand alternates between hammered bass figures and free melodic answers, the time stretching to fit the phrase; the singing rides on top in a relaxed, conversational drawl that can shade into the unmetered moan of the work song. The repertoire stays songster-wide — Lipscomb’s debut album is titled Texas Sharecropper and Songster for a reason — holding ballads, rags, spirituals, and dance numbers alongside the blues proper. At the tradition’s archaic edge stands Texas Alexander, who played nothing at all: a field hollerer who hired guitarists (Lonnie Johnson and Eddie Lang among them) to follow his levee-camp lines around the bar lines they ignored.

Key artists

  • Blind Lemon Jefferson — The style’s first star and country blues’s first commercial force: a high keening voice, unpredictable bar lengths, and a guitar that answered him like a sparring partner. Roughly a hundred sides between 1926 and his death in December 1929; the epitaph on his Wortham headstone is his own line — see that his grave is kept clean.
  • Lead Belly — Jefferson’s Dallas partner and the songster tradition’s king, twelve-string thunder over a repertoire that held everything. The Lomaxes recorded him at Angola in 1933; the folk revival made his songbook permanent.
  • Texas Alexander — The unaccompanied moan on commercial record: some sixty-nine sides from 1927, work-song time in a record-business format, with the era’s greatest guitarists hired to keep up.
  • Lightnin’ Hopkins — The tradition’s longest shadow: a free-metered, talking bluesman who recorded prolifically from 1946 to the end, acoustic and electric by turns, always unmistakably Texan. Rediscovered by Samuel Charters in 1959 and on a Carnegie Hall bill with Joan Baez and Pete Seeger within the year — the template blues rediscovery.
  • Mance Lipscomb — A songster in the fullest sense, holding dance tunes, ballads, spirituals, and blues in one repertoire; discovered by the wider world in 1960 but never “revived,” because he had never stopped.

Foundational records

  • “Got the Blues” / “Long Lonesome Blues” (1926, Blind Lemon Jefferson) — The hit that proved a lone bluesman could carry the race market
  • “Levee Camp Moan Blues” (1927, Texas Alexander) — The unmetered work song on commercial record, Lonnie Johnson navigating alongside
  • “Match Box Blues” (1927, Blind Lemon Jefferson) — Cut three times that year; thirty years later it became Carl Perkins’s “Matchbox,” and then the Beatles’, with Perkins watching from the control room
  • “See That My Grave Is Kept Clean” (1927, Blind Lemon Jefferson) — The dread masterpiece; Bob Dylan closed his 1962 debut with it
  • “Katie Mae Blues” (1946, Lightnin’ Hopkins) — The debut side that launched the last great Texas country-blues career
  • Texas Sharecropper and Songster (1960, Mance Lipscomb) — Arhoolie’s first release: the whole songster world on one record, cut in a living room

Legacy and influence

The single-string conception plugged in and conquered. T-Bone Walker, who as a teenager had himself led Blind Lemon through Deep Ellum, translated the style’s horn-like lines to the electric guitar and built electric Texas blues on them — which makes Jefferson’s phrasing an ancestor of essentially all modern lead guitar. The acoustic line fed the revival instead: Hopkins became its biggest rediscovery, Lipscomb its moral center, and Jefferson’s songbook scattered into rockabilly (“Matchbox”), folk (Dylan’s debut), and everything the Anthology generation touched.

The Texas economy of the music mattered too. Arhoolie, founded on Lipscomb’s porch recordings, became the model documentary label — proof that the tradition’s last quarter-century, lived out on farms and in Houston apartments rather than on charts, was worth recording in full.
