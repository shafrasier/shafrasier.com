Piedmont blues

Blues → Country blues → Piedmont blues

Period: c. 1920–1945
Location: The southeastern seaboard — Atlanta through the Carolinas to Virginia
Key influences: Ragtime, string band, country blues

RYM

The thumb plays stride piano and the fingers play the horn section. Piedmont guitar is ragtime translated to six strings: an alternating bass that bounces where the Delta stomps, a syncopated treble melody dancing over it, the whole machine so buoyant that even the slow blues seem to be strolling. The style belongs to the plateau between the Appalachians and the coastal plain — Atlanta up through the Carolinas to Virginia — and to an economy unlike any other in the blues: tobacco towns, where factory wages and auction-season cash made street musicianship a paying trade.

Origins

The synthesis was ragtime’s. The region’s Black string bands had been working dances across the racial line for a generation, and when the blues arrived it was folded into that dance-first grammar rather than replacing it. Blind Blake’s “West Coast Blues,” cut for Paramount in 1926, put the rolling fingerpicked style on record first — a rag in everything but name — and his eighty-odd sides made him the school’s technical benchmark and its enduring mystery, a man about whom almost nothing was documented until his death certificate surfaced in 2011.

Durham became the capital. The tobacco factories drew the players; the auctions paid them; and a store manager named J.B. Long handled the industry side, securing Blind Boy Fuller his first session in July 1935 and bringing Reverend Gary Davis and washboard player Bull City Red along to it. Fuller became the region’s best-selling artist — over 120 sides in five years, a National steel resonator cutting through every one — and his death in February 1941, at thirty-six, marks the beginning of the end. The war finished it: the tobacco-town audiences dispersed, and by 1945 the living scene had closed.

The sound

Alternating-thumb bass is the signature — the right thumb rocking between bass strings in steady ragtime pulse while two or three fingers syncopate the melody against it, one guitar producing the polyrhythm of a piano professor’s two hands. The feel is the point: danceable, bouncing, melodically generous, the lyric content frequently comic or sly where the Delta’s runs fatalistic. The arrangements grew by addition — washboard, harmonica, kazoo — and the harmonica tradition peaked in Sonny Terry, whose whooping falsetto-and-harp style put the field holler inside the dance music. Gospel ran through it too: Davis, ordained in 1937, took the same virtuoso machinery into sacred material and out the other side.

Key artists

  • Blind Blake — The most technically dazzling guitarist of the 1920s race records era; his ragtime picking remains the style’s benchmark. Paramount’s biggest male blues seller after Blind Lemon, and a biographical near-blank until 2011.
  • Blind Boy Fuller — The commercial peak: tough, direct songs over immaculate picking, the best-selling bluesman of the late 1930s anywhere. Dead at thirty-six in 1941, having seen little of the money his 120-plus sides earned.
  • Reverend Gary Davis — The school’s most advanced harmonic mind and its longest lever. He moved to New York in 1944, sang on Harlem streets, and taught at home for decades — Stefan Grossman and David Bromberg among the students (“I have no children but I have sons”) — seeding the entire 1960s fingerpicking renaissance. Peter, Paul and Mary’s 1962 recording of his “Samson and Delilah” bought him a house in Queens.
  • Sonny Terry & Brownie McGhee — Fuller’s harmonica man (on the From Spirituals to Swing stage in 1938) and the protégé once billed as “Blind Boy Fuller No. 2.” Teamed after Fuller’s death and settled in New York by 1942, into the Guthrie–Seeger–Lead Belly circle, they spent forty years as the style’s touring ambassadors.
  • Blind Willie McTell — The Atlanta adjacency: a twelve-string ragtime master usually filed beside the school rather than in it, whose “Statesboro Blues” (1928, Victor) the style cannot be discussed without.

Foundational records

  • “West Coast Blues” (1926, Blind Blake) — The first recorded Piedmont ragtime guitar; the school’s prototype
  • “Statesboro Blues” (1928, Blind Willie McTell) — The twelve-string landmark on the school’s Atlanta edge
  • “Truckin’ My Blues Away” (1936, Blind Boy Fuller) — The bounce at full commercial strength; its title phrase escaped into the culture at large
  • “Step It Up and Go” (1940, Blind Boy Fuller) — His biggest hit, from one of his final sessions
  • Peter, Paul and Mary’s “If I Had My Way” (1962) — Davis’s “Samson and Delilah” as folk-boom hit: the revival paying the tradition, for once, in royalties
  • “One Dime Blues” (1956, Etta Baker) — Proof the vernacular tradition never broke: recorded on an Appalachian field trip, sixteen years into the style’s official afterlife

Legacy and influence

The school’s second life outran its first. Davis’s Harlem teaching practice turned his fingers into a curriculum — the fingerstyle revival of the 1960s is substantially his students’ work — while Terry and McGhee carried the duo format around the world for four decades. “Statesboro Blues” traveled furthest: Taj Mahal’s 1968 arrangement led directly to the Allman Brothers’ At Fillmore East version, which welded Piedmont songbook to Southern rock at the loudest possible volume. The National Heritage Fellowships that went to Etta Baker, John Cephas, and Phil Wiggins across the decades since mark the same continuity from the institutional side.

What the style left behind is a temperament as much as a technique: the demonstration that the blues could bounce — that sophistication, humor, and dance feel are one of the music’s native dialects, as old as the dread.
