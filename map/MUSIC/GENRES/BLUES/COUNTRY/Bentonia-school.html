Bentonia school

Blues → Country blues → Delta blues → Bentonia school

Period: c. 1918–present
Location: Bentonia, Mississippi
Key influences: Delta blues, open minor tunings

RYM

A guitar tuned to an open minor chord, played soft and circling, under a voice pitched high enough to sound like it’s already left the body. The Bentonia sound is the eeriest corner of the blues: brooding, harmonically suspended, more atmosphere than progression, and it belongs to one town of a few hundred people in Yazoo County, at the southern edge of the Delta. Where the Delta mainstream drives, Bentonia hovers. The school has produced exactly one canonical genius, a handful of keepers, and a century of continuity that no other local blues tradition can match.

Origins

The style’s founder never made a record. Henry Stuckey (1897–1966), a Bentonia farmer, served in France in the First World War and said afterward that he had learned an open minor tuning there from Black Caribbean soldiers — usually given as Bahamians — and brought it home when he was discharged. The story is his own testimony, collected decades later by the researcher David Evans, and nothing documents it independently; what is certain is that by the early 1920s Stuckey was playing Bentonia dances in a tuning nobody else used, an open E minor (with a D minor variant) that players in his line came to call “crossnote.” Around 1924 he taught it to a neighbor in his early twenties named Skip James, and the school had its voice.

James carried the sound to record through the Delta’s usual door: H. C. Speir, the Jackson store owner whose auditions fed the race labels, heard him in early 1931 and sent him to Paramount in Grafton, Wisconsin. The February 1931 session produced eighteen issued sides — “Devil Got My Woman” above all — recorded into the teeth of the Depression, when the race-record market was collapsing. They sold almost nothing. James, embittered, turned to the church for three decades, and the school’s defining documents survived on a handful of 78s.

The sound

Crossnote tuning is the engine. With the open strings sounding a minor triad, the guitar drones dark by default: the player keeps a soft, circling fingerpicked figure running while melody notes rise out of it and sink back, and the harmony barely moves. Over that suspension sits the falsetto — James pitched lines at the top of his range and let them waver, a sound listeners have been calling ghostly since 1931. The repertoire is as shared as the technique. Bentonia players work the same small body of songs across generations, above all the “devil” family: James’s “Devil Got My Woman” and Jack Owens’s later “It Must Have Been the Devil” are the same dread worked by two hands, and the lyric world stays fixed on the devil, death, and loneliness. The blues here is closer to a rite than a dance music.

One town

The school never left home, and that is its history. Owens (1904–1997) farmed in Yazoo County his whole life and ran a weekend juke out of his house, singing the old repertoire into the 1990s; Evans first recorded him at the end of the 1960s, four decades after James’s session, sounding as if no time had passed. His harmonica partner Bud Spires, nearly blind, was the son of a Chicago bluesman who had gone north while the Owens family stayed. The Blue Front Café, opened in 1948 by sharecroppers Carey and Mary Holmes, became the tradition’s clubhouse, and their son Jimmy “Duck” Holmes (b. 1947) took it over on his father’s death in 1970. Holmes learned directly from Stuckey and Owens, founded the Bentonia Blues Festival with his mother in 1972, and still plays the school’s repertoire behind the same counter — the oldest surviving juke joint in Mississippi, run by the last of the Bentonia bluesmen.

Key artists

  • Henry Stuckey — The founder, known entirely through testimony: the veteran who carried the crossnote tuning home, taught it to James and Owens’s generation, and died unrecorded in 1966. The school’s sound is his; the school’s fame never was.
  • Skip James — The genius and the document. His 1931 Paramount sides sold nothing and influenced almost no one for thirty years; his June 1964 rediscovery in a Tunica hospital bed — found by John Fahey, Bill Barth, and Henry Vestine — put him on the Newport Folk Festival stage within weeks, falsetto intact. He died in 1969, having lived just long enough to hear his songs come back to him.
  • Jack Owens — The keeper. A farmer who never sought a career, recorded late by David Evans (It Must Have Been the Devil, Testament, 1971) and feted at festivals in old age with Bud Spires at his side, he is the proof that Bentonia was a living tradition rather than one man’s invention.
  • Jimmy “Duck” Holmes — The present tense. Blue Front proprietor since 1970, festival host since 1972, first album (Back to Bentonia) at 58, and a Grammy nomination at 72 for Cypress Grove (Easy Eye Sound, 2019), produced by Dan Auerbach of the Black Keys.

Foundational records

  • “Devil Got My Woman” (1931, Skip James) — The school’s defining text and one of the strangest records of the prewar era; inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame in 2020
  • “I’m So Glad” (1931, Skip James) — Frantic, glittering fingerpicking under a lyric of pure exhaustion; the Bentonia sound at its most virtuosic
  • “Hard Time Killing Floor Blues” (1931, Skip James) — The Depression’s bleakest contemporary document, sung as if from outside it
  • “22-20 Blues” (1931, Skip James) — Piano-driven and menacing; Robert Johnson reworked it directly into “32-20 Blues” five years later
  • It Must Have Been the Devil (1971, Jack Owens & Bud Spires) — The tradition’s second generation on record at last, forty years after the first
  • Cypress Grove (2019, Jimmy “Duck” Holmes) — Auerbach’s production frames the school’s drone for modern ears; the Grammy nomination brought Bentonia its widest audience since 1964

Legacy and influence

The school’s afterlife runs through other people’s amplifiers. Cream cut “I’m So Glad” on Fresh Cream (1966), and the royalties — the first real money James’s music ever made him — covered his medical bills and, in 1969, his funeral. The records themselves became cinema: “Devil Got My Woman” is the emotional pivot of Ghost World (2001), the 78 that ruins Enid for everything else, and “Hard Time Killing Floor Blues” reached a mass audience through O Brother, Where Art Thou? (2000). The drone-leaning corner of the modern roots world — Auerbach’s productions above all — treats Bentonia as a primary color.

At home the school simply continued. The Bentonia Blues Festival has run every June since 1972 on the Holmes family land, the Blue Front still opens, and the tradition that began with an unrecorded veteran’s tuning is now maintained by a Grammy-nominated juke-joint owner who learned it from the founder himself. No other strand of the blues kept an unbroken local line from the 1920s into the present.
